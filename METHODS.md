# Methods

This document describes the methods used in this project. See [README.md](README.md) for an introduction to the project.

## Overview

We use two processes for exploring predictors of language and reading outcomes:

1. **Gradient boosting algorithms** to identify the most influential predictors for each
   outcome.
2. **Bayesian statistical models** to estimate interpretable quantities with quantified uncertainty — treatment effects, dose-response curves, mediation — answering specific questions.

## 1. Gradient-boosting models

**Purpose:** rank predictors by how much they help out-of-sample prediction, and read the direction and consistency of each effect.

**Fit and cross-validate.** LightGBM with an MAE objective, `GroupKFold` by `subject_id`. One module per problem; level and gain targets are separate models.

**Tune.** `scripts/tune_model.py` runs an Optuna TPE study under the same `GroupKFold`. Inside each training fold it carves an inner `GroupShuffleSplit` for early stopping, so the outer validation fold is never seen by `early_stopping` and the reported CV RMSE and best iteration stay independent; the mean best iteration becomes the tuned `n_estimators`. Results land in `output/tuning/{model_id}/`. Tuning does **not** mutate the registry — applying tuned parameters is a manual, reviewable edit, so the source stays the single source of truth.

**Rank predictors.** The gradient-boosting layer no longer performs hard feature selection. Each model fits the full default predictor set for its target family and treats the ordered, clustered ranking as the deliverable. Per-fit diagnostics — Spearman correlation, a distance-correlation dendrogram, a mutual-information heatmap, and importance pairing — guard against over-reading a single predictor that is collinear with another. Gain-model defaults exclude intervention-period process measures (`attend`, `tascore`, `tachang`) so the ranking is about baseline/state predictors of progress rather than variables observed during the gain interval.

**Interpret.** Read the SHAP beeswarm (`output/models/{model_id}/shap_summary.png`) _together with_ the permutation-importance ranking: importance is _how much_ a feature contributes, the beeswarm is _which direction_ and _how consistently_. They often disagree in interpretively important ways — a top-ranked predictor can run opposite to every other, be non-monotonic or sit beside a similar-importance predictor of opposite sign. For each top predictor, check whether low (blue) and high (red) values separate cleanly across zero, how tight the effect is, and whether any tail acts against the dominant direction. Always state the direction; never let a reader infer it from importance alone. A predictive importance on a `_GAIN` or `_NEXT` variable is _not_ a causal claim.

## 2. Bayesian statistical models

**Purpose:** estimate interpretable quantities with full uncertainty. Sixteen families, each a factory in `factories.py` and a pipeline entry in `pipeline.py`. The eight detailed below carry the headline estimands; the other eight are association, cross-check and reproduction models — `adjusted` (between-child adjusted association, LRP65), `corr_factor` (correlated-domain-factor measurement model, LRPMM01), `dose_response` (cumulative-session dose response, LRP77), `lcsm` (coupled latent change-score model, LRP67), `mediation_multi` (two-mediator g-formula, LRP64), `horseshoe` (regularised-horseshoe predictor-ranking cross-check, LRPHS01/02), `growth` (multivariate growth curves, LRP69/70) and `historical_growth` (historical-cohort growth-curve reproduction, RLMHG01):

- **ITT** — the randomised-phase treatment effect (τ) on one outcome (the uniform DAG-faithful **LRPITT01–11** suite; the own baseline and linear age are _precision_ terms only — the arm effect is identified by randomisation, not by covariate adjustment — and no cross-baselines enter). These fits preserve assigned arm but use the available analysis data rather than every randomised child, so they are **available-case modified ITT analyses**, not a complete ITT analysis of all 57 children; the analysis population and missingness assumption are stated below. Heavily-floored outcomes (phonetic spelling, nonword reading) use a **post-hoc, arm-blind, data-adaptive floor rule** (≥ 40 % of post-scores at zero at t2): the exploratory headline is the off-floor transition risk difference `Pr(post > 0 | observed at floor at t1)`, fit on the baseline-floored at-risk children only. Baseline floor status is pre-randomisation, so this remains a valid randomised subgroup contrast among children whose floor status and post-score are observed, subject to the same missingness assumption; however, because the rule and its 40 % threshold were chosen after examining this trial's outcome distribution, it is an exploratory reanalysis estimand rather than a prospectively pre-specified trial primary. A graded Beta-Binomial over all available children and a graded contrast among the off-floor children (an untruncated proxy for the conditional-above-floor mean) are flagged, detection-limited secondaries read only beside per-arm mover counts.
- **Joint** — the registered joint specifications place several outcome models in one PyMC graph, but residual correlation is off and the outcome-specific priors and likelihoods factorise. They therefore reproduce separate marginal posteriors rather than learning within-child residual covariance. Current reports lead with per-draw contrasts between probability-scale average marginal effects, which puts tests with different denominators on the common proportion-correct scale; `tau_i - tau_j` conditional-logit contrasts are supplementary. Neither interval preserves within-child outcome covariance under the factorised model, so cross-outcome conclusions remain exploratory sensitivity results pending a paired child-level randomisation-inference/permutation analysis, bootstrap, sandwich, or defensible dependence-model analysis. In the taught-versus-not-taught models, a positive contrast establishes only that the taught effect is larger; describing transfer as limited additionally requires the marginal not-taught effect to be small against a substantively defined negligible-effect threshold.
- **Mechanism** — a dose-response curve `f_mech` of one measure on another (e.g. letter-sound → word reading), conditioned on a DAG-derived adjustment set and a per-child random intercept.
- **Mediation** — a direct/indirect decomposition through a mediator by counterfactual g-formula simulation from the posterior, not from coefficients. The software computes the NDE/NIE _functionals_, but under this study's DAG those are **not identified natural effects**, and no amount of extra data repairs that. Two obstacles stack: latent general ability confounds every mediator → outcome path, and intervention dose (`IS`) is a **treatment-induced (exposure-induced) mediator–outcome confounder** — a descendant of the randomised group pointing into both mediator and outcome — so natural effects fail to be identified even under randomisation, and are _not_ rescued by measuring and adjusting dose (VanderWeele, Vansteelandt & Robins 2014, DOI [10.1097/EDE.0000000000000034](https://doi.org/10.1097/EDE.0000000000000034)). Report every mediation quantity as a **model-based decomposition under stated assumptions**, never as a causal route. The **interventional** companions (MED-078 for $L\to W$, MED-186 for $L\to N$ and MED-187 for $L\to B$; `decompose(..., interventional=True)`) invoke no cross-world quantity and so escape the recanting-witness obstacle in the target estimand, but they still assume no unmeasured mediator–outcome confounding (Hejazi, Rudolph, van der Laan & Díaz 2022, assumption A5, DOI [10.1093/biostatistics/kxac002](https://doi.org/10.1093/biostatistics/kxac002)) — weaker-assumption targets, not identified effects in this study.
- **Waitlist-crossover arm-by-wave models** — a longitudinal sensitivity analysis alongside the randomised ITT (the **LRPDID01–13** family): the binary-treatment models jointly model the bounded t1, t2 and t3 levels with separate immediate-minus-waitlist gaps at each wave. `tau_t2` is the clean randomised t2 arm contrast; `arm_gap_t1` checks pre-randomisation balance; `arm_gap_t3` compares arms after both have crossed into treatment and is associational; `delta_crossover = tau_t2 - arm_gap_t3` describes how much the arm gap closes after crossover, but is not a second randomised treatment effect. A child random intercept partially pools stable between-child differences; it does not make every child their own fixed-effect control. The models do not condition on each period's start outcome because t2 is already treatment-affected for the immediate arm when it becomes the P2 baseline. Dose variants retain the two transition rows, adjust for randomised arm, current treatment, pre-randomisation t1 outcome and t1 age, and estimate observational treated-centred session-dose associations. LRPDID07 resolves that association by period and LRPDID07base is its pooled-dose LOO comparator.
- **Gain factors** — a DAG-focused ANCOVA of a period's post-score on its own pre-score (the **LRPGF01–11** family, one model per outcome; LRPGF01–08 each have a treated-only companion), with a per-child random intercept repairing latent general ability. Only the randomised on-intervention term is causal; every other covariate (own baseline, age, cognitive ability, upstream DAG skills, focal interactions) is reported as an explicit _adjusted association_, never as "X drives Y". SES is excluded — not a DAG node, and statistically redundant. Phonetic spelling inherits the ITT floor rule (an off-floor `Pr(post > 0)` Bernoulli, the treatment marginal an off-floor risk difference).
- **Level factors** — the companion _levels_ view (the **LRPLF01–11** family): the score at each timepoint, with group-by-time and ability-by-time as per-timepoint coefficient vectors. Only the t2 group contrast is a clean randomised effect; later timepoints are post-crossover and reported as associations. In both factor families "factors" carries its plain-English sense — the observed covariates _associated with_ gains or levels — not the factor-analysis sense: these are regression models with no latent variables (the latent measurement model is the separate correlated-domain-factor model, LRP-RLI-MM-001).
- **Aligned** — an onset-aligned per-protocol single gain (the **LRPAL01–08** family, plus a dose sensitivity variant): both arms are aligned by intervention onset (immediate t1 → t3, wait-list t2 → t4) into one cross-sectional ANCOVA per child. This trades randomisation for a like-for-like dose comparison, so the cohort contrast is **not** the ITT effect — it is confounded by age-at-onset and cohort/timing, and every coefficient is reported as an association. Cumulative sessions (dose) are a collider and enter only the sensitivity variant.

**Likelihood and priors.** A Beta-Binomial likelihood is used as a **working model** for each bounded total score via a logit linear predictor. It respects the score bounds and permits extra-binomial variation, but it should not be read as a literal claim that every test item is an exchangeable Bernoulli trial: heterogeneous item difficulty, stopping rules and floor or ceiling compression can violate that interpretation. Posterior-predictive checks must therefore assess the full score distribution, including zero and ceiling mass, by arm and relevant baseline strata; material mismatch triggers a likelihood sensitivity analysis. Priors come from shared constructors in `priors.py` (so the factories cannot drift apart); smooth nonlinear terms use Hilbert-space Gaussian-process approximations (`hsgp.py`).

**Prior-setting protocol.** Priors are **weakly informative and scale-calibrated**, not "uninformative": on a bounded-count logit model a very broad coefficient prior implies implausibly large probability- and item-scale effects — a `Normal(0, 0.5)` on τ is a plausible ~4 readable items on the 32-item letter-sound test but a ~35-item 95 % swing on the 170-item vocabulary tests. Each prior is **role-specific** — the causal τ, the _precision_ covariates (own baseline, age), the adjusted _associations_ (`gamma_cross`, the mechanism and mediator slopes), the _nuisance_ terms (intercept, the dispersion `kappa`, random-effect scales) and the _GP_ amplitudes / lengthscales — and its role is documented per parameter in every report's `priors_table.csv` (a guard test refuses any prior the table cannot describe). Calling a term a precision covariate describes its causal role, not immunity from modelling choices: in a finite Bayesian sample, its functional form and prior can change the posterior for τ when baseline values are imbalanced or the outcome model is misspecified, so unadjusted and prior-sensitivity comparisons remain necessary. The **treatment-effect prior is tiered by outcome** (`measures.DISTAL_OUTCOMES`): proximal directly-taught and decoding outcomes keep the wider `Normal(0, 0.5)`; the broad standardised-transfer outcomes (vocabulary, grammar, concepts) take a tighter `Normal(0, 0.3)`, because that is where a large effect is least expected and where the wide prior is worst on the item scale. The tier resolves in `factories._tau_sigma_for` and applies to the single-outcome ITT `tau`, the gain-factors `beta_trt`, the level-factors t2 group contrast and the waitlist-crossover model's randomised `tau_t2`; `arm_gap_t1`, `arm_gap_t3` and `delta_crossover` are balance or post-crossover quantities rather than additional treatment effects. Two terms are **exempted** and keep the common `Normal(0, 0.5)` — the joint model's `tau_k` (untiered across all outcomes) and the aligned family's `beta_cohort` — as documented in `docs/models/PRIORS.md`. A distal outcome's joint `tau_k` may therefore differ from its tiered single-outcome `tau` by a prior-driven amount. In general, priors may be informed by **measurement and scale properties (ceilings, denominators, test–retest reliability), external independent-sample literature, expert judgement from the education team, and prospectively stated substantive constraints**, but not by the posterior of the same fitted model if the analysis is to be described as prior-to-data. The distal tier in this project was adopted after the full ITT posterior table existed. It is therefore disclosed as **post-data, scale-calibrated regularisation**, not a blind prior; the sweep in `scripts/tau_prior_sensitivity.py` shows whether conclusions are stable across plausible prior scales, but that stability does not make the original choice prospective. Every prior is checked by **prior-predictive simulation and an estimand-scale pushforward** — does it generate plausible scores, gains and floor / ceiling patterns? Full rationale and the per-parameter inventory live in `docs/models/PRIORS.md` and `notes/202607011600-issue-141-prior-audit.md`.

For the post-hoc P/N floor-transition estimands, a power-scaling conflict for `tau` triggers an explicit estimand-matched sensitivity grid rather than the ordinary graded-outcome sweep: the same observed-baseline-floor subgroup and Bernoulli off-floor likelihood are fitted under `tau ~ Normal(0, 0.5/1.0/1.5)` with the linear-age precision term both present and absent. All six fits must pass the all-free-variable convergence gate and retain content-addressed traces. The release evaluator binds each trace to its exact prior/age cell, data, analysis set, primary fit, free-variable set and sampling contract, then recomputes convergence and every reported effect summary from the draws; a self-declared CSV flag is not sufficient. Reports show the full risk-difference and `P(benefit)` range; completing the grid is a computational release gate, not evidence that the result is prior-insensitive.

**Sign convention:** `G = 1` is the immediate-intervention arm after the `2 − group` recode (`group == 1` = immediate intervention, `group == 2` = wait-list control), so **positive τ means the intervention raises the outcome**.

**ITT analysis population and estimand hierarchy.** Burgoyne et al. (2012, DOI [10.1111/j.1469-7610.2012.02557.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1469-7610.2012.02557.x)) randomised 57 children. The repository's analysis file contains 54 children (28 immediate-intervention, 26 wait-list), after the original study's exclusions, and each model then uses the children with the outcome and required baseline/covariates observed; the fitted sample is commonly 54, is 53 for outcomes with an unavailable t2 score, and is smaller for the baseline-floor subgroup estimands. Assignment is always analysed as randomised, but this sequence is `57 randomised → 54 available in the repository → model-specific fitted sample`, so the current suite is an available-case modified ITT. Interpreting its arm contrast as the full-population randomised effect assumes that exclusion and missingness are independent of the unobserved potential outcomes, or at least not differentially outcome-related by arm after conditioning on the modelled pre-treatment information. That assumption is not guaranteed by randomisation; reports must show the denominator and exclusions by arm, and attrition sensitivity is required for a full-randomised-population claim. The published 2012 analysis described four primary outcome measures—single-word reading (`W`), letter-sound knowledge (`L`), phoneme blending (`B`) and taught expressive vocabulary (`TE`). This reanalysis makes `W` its single headline primary and treats the remaining outcomes as a family. That is an explicit **reanalysis hierarchy**, not the original trial's outcome hierarchy; both must be stated when interpreting multiplicity or comparing conclusions with the publication.

**Fit.** NUTS via `nutpie`, with `dev` / `test` / `reporting` sampling presets (the last is 6 chains × 6000 draws); `--target-accept` overrides when a funnel needs it.

**Evaluate.** Check convergence _before_ reading any estimate: R-hat ≤ 1.01, adequate effective sample size (ESS ≥ 400), and zero divergent transitions. A non-converged or divergent fit's posterior is not interpretable — fix the model, do not report it. The pipeline writes a `diagnostics_summary.json` whose pass/fail gate requires **0 divergences, BFMI ≥ 0.3, R-hat ≤ 1.01, ESS ≥ 400** (evaluated unrounded, over the model's free RVs plus the headline scalars) and the report renders it as a **pass/fail convergence banner first**, before any τ. This is only the sampling gate: `pareto_k.csv` separately flags importance-sampled LOO points above ArviZ's reliability threshold and makes that LOO score unreliable. In single-period ITT/joint models, where each point is one child, `scripts/influence_sensitivity.py` performs the required direct treatment-effect refit without all flagged children before a robustness claim. It standardises the full and leave-out posteriors over the same retained children, so the headline refit shift is not contaminated by changing the covariate-averaging population; the smaller composition shift and the total full-sample-to-leave-out shift are reported separately. This checks whether the treatment estimate is sensitive to those children; it does not repair or replace the unreliable LOO score. In repeated-measures random-intercept families, each point is a child-by-period row conditional on that child's fitted intercept; use an observation-level sensitivity or exact/moment-matched LOO for that same predictive target, not the whole-child ITT/joint runner. Prior- and posterior-predictive checks assess whether the working likelihood reproduces the outcome by arm, baseline band and distribution shape; a material shape flag remains a qualification even when convergence passes. The 1,000 prior draws are persisted onto `trace.nc` (the `prior` / `prior_predictive` / `log_prior` groups) so the report can show the prior-predictive check, the prior-vs-posterior overlay, the estimand-scale prior pushforward, and power-scaling sensitivity without refitting.

**Compare.** PSIS-LOO via ArviZ: prefer the higher-`elpd` model only when the difference clears its standard error (`elpd_diff` against `dse`). The interaction models are tested against their own no-interaction baselines as clean nested comparisons; `scripts/compare_statistical_models.py` collects the cross-model views. For the families with a per-child random intercept (mechanism, gain-/level-factors, DiD, dose-response, LCSM, growth) the pointwise unit is a child × phase/period row and the LOO is **conditional** (within-child, one-row-out) — elpd scores predicting a held-out row given that child's own fitted intercept, not new-child prediction — so it is comparable only across models sharing the same grouping on the same rows (which the nested comparators do).

**Interpret.** Report the **posterior**, not a point estimate: the **median** (preferred over the mean — it is transformation-invariant across the logit and probability scales) with its **89 % equal-tailed credible interval**. A highest posterior density interval (HPDI) at the same coverage is reported alongside as a per-scale **sensitivity** check — useful when a bounded-scale posterior is skewed — but not as the primary interval, since the HPDI is not transformation-invariant. Posterior bands follow a fixed **two-band** convention (issue #177, revised 2026-07-17; `notes/202607172359-credible-interval-standard.md`): the **equal-tailed 89 % credible interval** is the headline uncertainty summary and the **central 50 %** interval (the middle half of the posterior mass) is a visual aid, not a decision threshold — both shown alongside the full posterior. The **89 %** outer coverage is deliberate, not 95 %: 95 % is an arbitrary convention carried over from frequentist NHST, and its 2.5 / 97.5 % limits are the noisiest quantiles to estimate from a finite MCMC sample, whereas an equal-tailed 89 % interval (5.5 / 94.5 %) is markedly more stable at attainable ESS (Kruschke 2021 BARG, [doi:10.1038/s41562-021-01177-7](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-021-01177-7): stable HDI limits want ESS ≥ 10,000, "for equal-tailed intervals ESS can be lower"). The earlier equal-tailed **90 %** "sensitivity" band is **retired**: a deliberately non-round 89 % headline plus the full posterior already discourage the bright-line reading that band existed to blunt. Read direction for the reported intervention effect from `P(AME > 0)`, the posterior probability that the fitted-sample average marginal effect is positive, not from whether a 50 % or 89 % band excludes zero. The secondary `P(τ_logit > 0)` describes the conditional logit coefficient; it has the same sign in a constant-effect model because the inverse-logit is monotone, but it can differ when the treatment effect is moderated across children. There are no p-values. Distinguish two questions: _direction_ — `P(AME > 0)` — and _magnitude_ — `P(benefit ≥ δ)` against an education-lead-approved minimally-important difference δ (a region of practical equivalence). δ is a **post-hoc** MID — the primary rule was chosen after inspecting fitted posteriors and its candidates' effect on the headline claim was tabulated — so it is always reported with a **mandatory δ-sensitivity sweep** (e.g. the 10/15/20 pp risk-difference grid on the floored outcomes) rather than rested on a single leniently-selected threshold. A high direction probability can sit on a practically negligible effect. Give both the logit scale (the natural parameter) and the probability / items scale as an **average marginal effect** over the fitted sample's observed covariate profiles (not at a single constructed sample-mean baseline). An 89 % credible interval that includes zero means the sign is not pinned down — avoid a decisive directional claim, and report where the posterior mass sits (both sides of zero) rather than collapsing to "significant / not". At this study's sample size a significant-looking point estimate is on average magnitude-inflated (a Type-M / winner's-curse effect), so lead with the interval, not the point. **Evidence labels** (inconclusive / suggestive / moderate / strong / very strong; issue #179) qualify the evidence for a **named claim** — "moderate evidence that the intervention helps", never "a moderate effect" — are reported _after_ the probability and odds, and are oriented to the **favoured direction**, so a clearly negative effect is reported as evidence of harm, not as inconclusive. See `notes/202606261304-evidence-strength-and-rope-reporting.md`.

## Causal interpretation and its limits

**What is causal:** within the randomised window, assignment licenses the treatment contrast; baseline floor status also defines a valid pre-randomisation subgroup. The same licence applies to the waitlist-crossover model's immediate-minus-waitlist t2 contrast, `tau_t2`, because it is the same randomised comparison; agreement is not independent evidence because the models share data. It does not apply to `arm_gap_t3`, `delta_crossover`, dose or heterogeneity quantities. For the 54-child repository cohort and each smaller fitted sample, a causal interpretation additionally requires the available-case assumption stated above. Randomisation balances arms in expectation at assignment, but it does not repair exclusions or post-randomisation missing outcomes. The own-baseline term is in an ITT model for _precision_, not identification — say so when writing up an ITT result, and remember that its finite-sample prior and functional form can still influence the numerical posterior for τ.

**What is not:** every association we did not randomise — the `gamma_cross` "baseline X predicts later Y" terms, the mechanism `f_mech` slope, and the mediator → outcome arrow. These have cross-lagged-panel structure and can be inflated by a stable trait shared across measures or by ordinary measurement noise (which alone can manufacture a spurious "earlier → later" path). The mechanism models already include the recommended repair — a per-child random intercept, but two leaks remain: the mechanism is measured at the _same wave_ as the outcome, and we do not separately model measurement error. So report these as _adjusted associations_, never as "X drives Y".

**Characterising non-responders — the honest ladder.** The intuitive recipe — classify children into responders and non-responders on their observed change, then compare the groups' characteristics — manufactures a subgroup from noise and finds predictors of that artefact (Senn 2016, doi:10.1002/sim.7059). LRPDID13 is retained only as an **exploratory heterogeneity sensitivity**, not as a clean test of whether treatment-response variance exists. Only waitlist children change treatment status, each supplies one untreated-to-treated transition, and immediate-arm random intercepts and treatment slopes are not separately identified from their rows; consequently, the variance component can also absorb heterogeneous maturation, period shocks and measurement noise. Any subsequent moderation must therefore be one-stage, strongly regularised, limited to pre-randomisation moderators and interpreted as exploratory. The observable companion question — who is at persistent low attainment on the floored phonics measures — is answered descriptively and with the floor-sitter survival family (`lrp-rli-surv-009`/`011`), not by a latent non-responder label.

## Reporting results

Write for a numerate reader who knows frequentist statistics but is newer to Bayesian methods — picture a science undergraduate. Lead with the finding and its uncertainty, then the "so what"; keep narratives as short as the content allows.

- **Translate the Bayesian parts.** Expand shorthand on first use (τ = conditional logit-scale treatment coefficient, AME = fitted-sample average marginal treatment effect, `gamma_own` = baseline coupling, `f_mech` = mechanism curve). Read an equal-tailed 89% credible interval in plain words — "given the model and data, the value lies in [a, b] with 89% probability" — prefer a posterior tail probability for the named estimand (`P(AME > 0) = 0.97` for the intervention-help claim) to anything that reads like a p-value, and restate the sign convention wherever τ appears.
- **Pair every estimate with its uncertainty** — never a bare posterior mean or a bare importance rank.
- **Avoid verbal evidence labels ("strong", "moderate") wherever possible** — report the odds and probabilities and let them speak. Where prose forces a label, append the word _evidence_ and name the claim ("strong evidence that the intervention helps"), show the odds beside it, and use the round-odds ladder in `notes/202606261304-evidence-strength-and-rope-reporting.md` — run separately for _direction_ (`pd`) and _meaningful benefit_ (`P(benefit ≥ δ)`). A label must never connote effect _size_.
- **Cite primary sources, verify them before committing them to text, and always include a DOI** when one exists.

## Conventions

- **No outlier exclusion** unless justified on the model page.
- **`GroupKFold` by `subject_id`** wherever longitudinal leakage would otherwise inflate performance.
- **Mechanism-model β_G is not a direct-effect estimate** — both arms are on intervention during phases 1 and 2, so the pooled β_G averages over phases where group is no longer a treatment contrast (documented in each mechanism report).
- **RLI intervention scope** — the intervention targets vocabulary and grammar as well as reading, so null effects on receptive/expressive vocabulary are substantive findings, not by-design predictions.
- **Decisions a future reader might question get a dated note in `notes/`** before they leave your head — the question, the choice, the rationale, the result. Mark AI-drafted notes with a `> [!NOTE]` admonition naming the tool and model (per the AI-authorship rule in `CLAUDE.md`).

## Glossary

- **Posterior** — the distribution of a parameter after combining prior and data; summarised by a mean/median, a credible interval, and tail probabilities.
- **Credible interval (CrI)** — a range holding the parameter with stated probability _given the model and data_ (the reading people wrongly attach to a confidence interval).
- **Posterior tail probability** — e.g. `P(AME > 0)` or `P(τ_logit > 0)`, the posterior mass for a named estimand or parameter on one side of a value; it must say which quantity is being evaluated.
- **Probability of direction (`pd`)** — the posterior mass in the favoured direction for the named quantity. The ITT headline uses `P(AME > 0)`; `P(τ_logit > 0)` is a secondary coefficient read and can differ under moderation. It answers direction, not magnitude. For a symmetric posterior `p ≈ 2(1 − pd)`, so treating a `pd` threshold as a discovery rule would merely disguise a p-value threshold.
- **ROPE / minimally-important difference (δ)** — a band [−δ, +δ] around zero wide enough that an effect inside it is practically equivalent to none (the frequentist SESOI / clinical MID); `P(|effect| < δ)` is its coverage, `P(effect > δ)` the probability of a meaningful benefit. δ is set to **half the period's natural-maturation gain** (floored at 1 item), signed off by the education lead (issue #144); each ITT report also carries a **δ-sensitivity table** giving every outcome's `P(benefit ≥ δ)` at its adopted δ and a stricter δ (word reading at δ = 1 and 2; the floored outcomes at 10 / 15 / 20 pp), so the meaningful-benefit claim's robustness to the δ choice is explicit rather than hidden in a single number.
- **Type-S / Type-M error** — among effects declared "significant", the rate of wrong _sign_ (S) and the average _magnitude_ exaggeration (M); both worsen at low power.
- **τ (tau)** — the randomised-window treatment-arm effect in the fitted analysis sample (positive ⇒ intervention helps); the full-randomised-population reading additionally requires the available-case assumption above.
- **`tau_t2`** — the waitlist-crossover arm-by-wave model's immediate-minus-waitlist contrast at t2; randomised and causal under the same assumptions as the ITT, but not independent evidence.
- **`delta_crossover`** — `tau_t2 - arm_gap_t3`, the modelled closure of the immediate-minus-waitlist arm gap after crossover; a post-crossover association, not a second treatment effect.
- **`gamma_own` / `gamma_cross`** — a measure's own-baseline coupling / its cross-baseline couplings, on the logit scale.
- **`f_mech`** — the nonparametric dose-response curve in a mechanism model.
- **ITT (intention-to-treat)** — analyse every randomised child in the arm assigned, regardless of adherence. The current suite preserves assigned arm but starts from 54 of the 57 randomised children and then applies model-specific observed-data requirements, so it is described as available-case modified ITT rather than full ITT.
- **Mediation / NDE / NIE** — how much of an effect flows through a mediator (natural direct / natural indirect effect). Here these name the _functionals the software computes_, not identified causal effects: under this study's DAG the natural effects are not identified (latent general ability, plus exposure-induced confounding by dose `IS`). See the Mediation family above.
- **IDE / IIE** — the **interventional** direct / indirect effects (MED-078/186/187): the mediator is drawn from its arm-specific covariate-conditional distribution rather than a cross-world counterfactual. Weaker assumptions than NDE/NIE — the exposure-induced-confounding obstacle drops away — but still not identified here, since no-unmeasured-mediator–outcome-confounding is still required.
- **Exposure-induced (treatment-induced) confounder** — a variable caused by treatment that then confounds the mediator → outcome relation (here: dose `IS`). It blocks identification of natural direct/indirect effects, and adjusting for it does _not_ fix the problem.
- **PSIS-LOO** — Pareto-smoothed importance-sampling leave-one-out cross-validation, the Bayesian model-comparison score (`elpd`).
- **HSGP** — Hilbert-space approximation to a Gaussian process; a fast smooth nonlinear term.
- **Regression to the mean** — extreme scores drift toward the average on retest; visible here as `gamma_own` below 1.

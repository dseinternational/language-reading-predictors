# Issue #141: prior audit — inventory, sources, calibration, and the two-tier τ proposal

Date: 2026-07-01

Related issue: <https://github.com/dseinternational/language-reading-predictors/issues/141>

## Purpose and status

Issue #141 asks us to review every Bayesian prior in the statistical-model suite,
decide whether each is appropriate for the current questions and sample size
(n ≈ 54), and document the rationale so it is defensible to a frequentist-leaning
science reader. The goal is **not** "uninformative" priors: on the bounded-count,
logit-scale models a very broad prior can imply implausible item-scale effects.
The aim is priors that are weakly informative, scale-calibrated, role-specific and
auditable.

This note is for team review, not a final sign-off. It records four things:

1. a **provenance constraint** that determines which evidence may inform the
   treatment-effect prior;
2. the **admissible sources** for each prior, and what each can constrain;
3. a **complete, role-classified inventory** of the priors the models actually
   register, each translated to odds-, probability- and item-scale;
4. a concrete, defensible **proposal** — an outcome-tiered τ prior — plus the
   `priors_table` completeness fix that shipped alongside this audit.

This note is the dated decision record; the living, row-by-row inventory lives in
`docs/models/PRIORS.md`. Priors are Bayesian — §"A bridge for the frequentist
reader" in `notes/202606261304-evidence-strength-and-rope-reporting.md` gives the
translations (posterior, credible interval, `pd`) this note assumes.

## 1. The provenance constraint that governs the τ prior

`data/rli_data_long.csv` (54 children × 4 timepoints, waitlist-crossover, with the
test maxima and the two composites taken from Burgoyne et al. 2012, Table 3) **is
the Burgoyne et al. (2012) RLI trial dataset**, extended to the full four-timepoint
follow-up. That single fact constrains the audit: issue #141 forbids priors
informed by "posterior results from the same fitted model", and Burgoyne (2012) is
the primary publication *of this very dataset*. Its reported group means, standard
deviations and effect sizes are therefore **not** an admissible source for the
treatment-effect prior `τ` — using them would condition the prior on the data we
are about to fit (double-counting, which also amplifies the small-sample winner's
curse documented in the evidence-strength note).

What Burgoyne (2012) may still do: fix the measurement facts already in use
(`n_trials`, ceilings, the composites in `measures.py`) and frame the *qualitative*
proximal-vs-distal transfer expectation. What it must not do: set the *scale* of
`τ`. That has to come from **independent** samples and general methodology.

## 2. What may inform each prior

| Source class | What it legitimately constrains |
| --- | --- |
| **Independent DS intervention studies** — Næss et al. 2022 (DSL+ vocabulary RCT), Goetz et al. 2008, Cologon et al. 2011, Lemons & Fuchs 2010 / Lemons et al. 2012, Baylis & Snowling 2012 | Plausible *scale* of a randomised effect on proximal decoding and taught vocabulary, from other cohorts. |
| **Meta-analyses** — Donolato et al. 2023 (oral-language, NDD; small, publication-bias-corrected, receptive weakest), Neil & Jones 2016 (DS communication), Galuschka et al. 2014 / phonics syntheses (d ≈ 0.3–0.5 on proximal decoding) | The *distribution* of plausible effects, and the regularisation case: broad standardised-vocabulary transfer is weak, so a wide τ there is not weakly informative. |
| **Test manuals / normative data** — ROWPVT, EOWPVT-4, YARC, TROG-2, CELF-Preschool-2 | Test–retest reliability → the own-baseline coupling `gamma_own`; normative raw-score SDs → the Beta-Binomial dispersion `kappa`. (Explicitly allowed under "measurement ceilings and scale properties".) |
| **DS developmental-trajectory data** — Hulme et al. 2012 and DS cross-sectional norms | The per-SD age slope `gamma_age`, and the cross-skill couplings behind the mechanism / mediation associations. |
| **General Bayesian methodology** — Gelman et al. 2008 (weakly-informative logistic default), Simpson et al. 2017 (penalised-complexity priors) | Benchmarks for coefficient priors and the shrinkage of the flexible (HSGP) and hierarchical (random-effect) scale priors. |

The strongest under-exploited source is the **test manuals**: published test–retest
`r` (typically 0.8–0.95 at these ages) is a direct external anchor for `gamma_own`,
and the normative raw-score SDs bound the extra-binomial dispersion behind `kappa`.
Pulling those exact numbers is the recommended next data-gathering step.

## 3. The inventory (what the models actually register)

Enumerated by building one model per family and reading the registered random
variables (not by eyeballing source): every family reduces to this shared set. Role
follows the house convention — only `τ` is *causal* (identified by randomisation);
*precision* terms sharpen it without licensing a causal claim; *association*
couplings are adjusted; *nuisance* = intercept / dispersion / scale; *gp* =
Gaussian-process amplitude / lengthscale.

| Prior (and its aliases) | Distribution | Role | Odds ratio at +1 prior SD (95 % prior) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| `tau` (`beta_G`, `delta`, `beta_period`, `beta_trt`, `b_grp_time`, `beta_cohort`, `a_G`, `b_G`) | Normal(0, 0.5) | **causal** | ×1.65 (0.38–2.66) |
| `gamma_own` (`a_L`, `a_comp`, `b_W`) | Normal(1, 0.5) | precision | centred ×2.7; AR slope ≈ 1 (near-perfect logit tracking) |
| `gamma_age` (`gamma_A`) | Normal(0, 0.3) | precision | ×1.35 (0.56–1.80) |
| `gamma_cross` (all adjusted couplings, moderators, interactions) | Normal(0, 0.3) | association | ×1.35 (0.56–1.80) |
| `predictor_slope` (LRP65 / LRPMM01 slopes) | Normal(0, 0.5) | association | ×1.65 (0.38–2.66) |

Bespoke families reuse these scales rather than introducing new ideas: the LCSM
change coefficients, the correlated-factor loadings, the two-mediator legs, and the
joint LKJ residual are all documented in the per-model prior tables (see §5).

### Probability-scale reading (per +1 prior SD of a standardised predictor)

| Prior | Δ probability at baseline p₀ = 0.2 / 0.5 / 0.8 |
| --- | --- |
| Normal(0, 0.5) — `tau`, `predictor_slope` | +0.09 / +0.12 / +0.07 |
| Normal(0, 0.3) — `gamma_cross`, `gamma_age` | +0.05 / +0.07 / +0.04 |
| Normal(0, 1) — `beta_mech`, `b_path` | +0.21 / +0.23 / +0.12 |

`beta_mech` / `b_path` at Normal(0, 1) are the loosest coefficient priors (a +1 SD
move is ~2.7× odds). This is defensible — "let the data identify the key
association" — but it is the second candidate (after τ) for a sensitivity check.

## 4. The τ prior is not scale-invariant — the two-tier proposal

A single logit prior implies very different item-scale effects across tests of
different length and difficulty. Evaluated through the actual Beta-Binomial
machinery at each outcome's real baseline:

| Outcome | n_trials | items @ +1 prior SD | items @ 95 % prior |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| R receptive vocabulary | 170 | **+16.0** | **+34.6** |
| E expressive vocabulary | 170 | **+14.0** | **+31.0** |
| T grammar | 32 | +3.9 | +7.7 |
| L letter sounds | 32 | +4.0 | +7.6 |
| W word reading | 79 | +3.6 | +8.5 |
| B blending | 10 | +1.2 | +2.3 |

`Normal(0, 0.5)` implies a plausible ~4-item swing on letter sounds but a **35-item**
95 % swing on ROWPVT — not weakly informative on the item scale, essentially flat
across and beyond the entire plausible range. The transfer literature (Burgoyne's
proximal-only pattern; Donolato's weak receptive-vocabulary transfer) says the
distal standardised measures are exactly where large effects are *least* expected.

**Proposal — an outcome-tiered τ prior, via the shared constructor:**

- **Proximal tier — keep `Normal(0, 0.5)`**: L, W, B, TR, TE, and the floored P, N
  (whose ITT estimand is an off-floor logistic `τ` with no denominator, so the
  item-scale concern does not apply).
- **Distal tier — tighten to `Normal(0, 0.25–0.3)`**: R, E, T, F, UR, UE (broad
  standardised transfer). At SD 0.3 the R/E 95 % swing drops from ±35 to ±19 items;
  at 0.25, to ±16. The case is strongest for R/E (high denominator *and* distal);
  the transfer rationale extends the tier to T/F/UR/UE even where the denominator
  makes the numerical change small.

This encodes a defensible prior belief before seeing the target posterior, and it
is implemented once through the shared constructor (keyed by outcome tier) rather
than ad hoc per model. It is now **implemented and sensitivity-checked** — see §5a
for the mechanism and the sweep that shows the headline conclusions are stable.

## 5. `priors_table` completeness fix (shipped with this audit)

Building every family exposed priors the report's `priors_table.csv` silently
omitted or mislabelled — a direct hit on the acceptance criterion that reports
"cannot silently omit newly introduced priors". Fixed in `priors.py` on this
branch and locked by `tests/statistical_models/test_prior_inventory.py` (builds one
model per family; asserts no free variable is left as `(model prior)` / `other`):

- **HSGP amplitude / lengthscale** (`f_*__eta`, `f_*__ell`) were unmapped (no
  table row or panel); now resolve to the `eta_main` (HalfNormal 0.3) / `ell`
  (InverseGamma 3, 1) panels.
- **LCSM (LRP67)** `a_change` / `b_self` were *mislabelled* as `gamma_cross`
  Normal(0, 0.3) by a name-prefix fallback; they are really Normal(0, 1.5) /
  Normal(0, 0.5). The unsafe `b_` / `a_` prefix was removed; bespoke priors are now
  read from the built variable via `pymc.printing.str_for_dist`.
- **Joint LKJ residual** (`u_chol`), **two-mediator legs** (`aL_*`, `aE_*`,
  `kappa_L/E`) and **correlated-factor** measurement priors (`lambda_load`,
  `factor_cov`, `sigma_indicator`) were absent; all are now documented.
- **`beta_G` in mechanism models** inherited role "causal" from the τ constructor,
  but there it is a DAG backdoor adjustment — an adjusted association. Corrected via
  the pipeline's context-override hook (as for `dose_response` / `aligned`).

The distribution column is now read off the *actual* random variable for anything
not backed by a shared constructor, so a future factory that adds an inline prior
is documented automatically — and if it cannot be classified, the guard test fails
rather than the report shipping a blank row.

## 5a. Two-tier τ — implemented, with sensitivity evidence

The two-tier τ prior is implemented (this branch): the randomised treatment effect
is built at `Normal(0, 0.5)` for proximal outcomes and `Normal(0, 0.3)` for the
distal set `measures.DISTAL_OUTCOMES = {R, E, T, F, UR, UE}`. The tier resolves
from the outcome symbol in `factories._tau_sigma_for`, applied to the ITT `tau`,
gain-factors `beta_trt`, level-factors group contrast and DiD `delta` — never to
the adjusted-association group terms (mechanism `beta_G`, aligned `beta_cohort`).
The scales live in `priors.TAU_SIGMA_PROXIMAL` / `TAU_SIGMA_DISTAL`; a distal
model's report panel + table both show `Normal(0, 0.3)` via the `tau_distal`
constructor. The joint model (LRPITT12) keeps the common `Normal(0, 0.5)` and is
cross-checked against the tiered single-outcome τ's (the existing ITT-vs-joint
consistency check), so its per-outcome vector prior stays clean.

**Sensitivity sweep** (`scripts/tau_prior_sensitivity.py`, dev config, n = 53,
R-hat ≈ 1.00 throughout). Refit each representative outcome across a τ-SD grid;
`pd = P(τ > 0)`, effect on the logit and items scales:

| Outcome (tier) | τ SD | `pd` | τ logit mean [95% CrI] | items mean [95% CrI] |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- | --- |
| **R** receptive (distal) | 0.20 | 0.65 | +0.03 [−0.12, +0.18] | +0.9 [−3.6, +5.3] |
| | 0.30 | 0.63 | +0.03 [−0.14, +0.20] | +0.9 [−4.1, +5.7] |
| | 0.50 | 0.64 | +0.04 [−0.14, +0.22] | +1.0 [−4.1, +6.4] |
| **E** expressive (distal) | 0.20 | 0.48 | −0.00 [−0.15, +0.15] | −0.1 [−4.0, +3.9] |
| | 0.30 | 0.49 | −0.00 [−0.15, +0.15] | −0.1 [−3.9, +3.8] |
| | 0.50 | 0.49 | +0.00 [−0.16, +0.16] | +0.0 [−4.0, +4.0] |
| **L** letter sounds (proximal) | 0.25 | 0.986 | +0.40 [+0.08, +0.75] | +2.5 [+0.5, +4.7] |
| | 0.50 | 0.996 | +0.57 [+0.19, +0.96] | +3.5 [+1.2, +5.9] |
| | 0.75 | 0.999 | +0.63 [+0.22, +1.02] | +3.9 [+1.4, +6.2] |
| **W** word reading (proximal) | 0.25 | 0.969 | +0.27 [−0.01, +0.57] | +1.8 [−0.1, +3.8] |
| | 0.50 | 0.991 | +0.36 [+0.06, +0.67] | +2.4 [+0.4, +4.4] |
| | 0.75 | 0.986 | +0.37 [+0.05, +0.68] | +2.5 [+0.3, +4.6] |

**Reading of the sweep:**

- **Distal R/E are null at every SD** (`pd` 0.48–0.65, i.e. inconclusive in either
  direction). Tightening 0.5 → 0.3 → 0.2 only slightly narrows an already-null
  interval and never moves the direction. So the distal tightening is *safe*: it
  cannot suppress a real effect (there is none to suppress) — it only stops the
  prior implying implausibly large item-scale swings on the 170-item tests. The
  huge items-scale CrIs (±4–6 items) beside the tight logit CrIs (±0.15) are the
  denominator amplification the whole tiering guards against.
- **Proximal L/W keep their direction across {0.25, 0.5, 0.75}** (`pd` ≥ 0.97).
  The magnitude grows with a looser prior (expected). At the tightest 0.25, word
  reading's items 95% CrI just touches zero (−0.1), so tightening the *proximal*
  tier would start to erode a genuine signal — the `0.5` default is the right
  proximal scale.

Headline conclusions are therefore stable under the two-tier prior: the distal
outcomes stay null and the proximal effects keep their direction. The choice of
`0.3` for the distal tier (vs `0.2`/`0.25`) is not consequential for the
conclusions and is retained as the more weakly-informative option (avoiding the
"tighten because inconvenient" critique).

## 6. Recommended sensitivity grid

Run for the headline estimands and report whether direction (`pd`), interval width,
and `P(benefit ≥ δ)` are stable — not binary significant/not.

- `tau` SD ∈ {0.25, 0.5, 0.75} on the proximal tier; {0.2, 0.25, 0.3, 0.5} on the
  distal tier (does tightening R/E change any conclusion? Expected: no — they are
  already ~null).
- `gamma_cross` / interaction SD ∈ {0.2, 0.3, 0.5}.
- `gamma_own` ∈ {Normal(1, 0.25), Normal(1, 0.5)}, checked against the test
  manuals' retest reliabilities.
- `beta_mech` / `b_path` ∈ {Normal(0, 0.5), Normal(0, 1)} — the loosest coefficient
  priors.
- `kappa` and HSGP amplitude alternatives only where posterior-predictive fit or
  the nonlinear terms are materially active.

# Findings — ITT family (intention-to-treat, the headline causal analysis)

> [!NOTE]
> Drafted by a LLM-based AI tool (Claude Code/Opus 4.8). Numbers are read from the `reporting`-config refit of 2026-07-16 (see [the refit process note](202607161130-full-statistical-refit.md)). All data are preliminary.

## What these models ask

The trial randomly assigned children to start the reading/language intervention **now** (immediate arm) or **after a wait** (waiting-list arm). "Intention-to-treat" (ITT) compares the two groups **as randomised**, regardless of how much intervention each child actually did. Because assignment was random, a difference between the groups over the trial window is a genuine **cause-and-effect** estimate of offering the programme — this is the study's flagship causal analysis.

Each model fits one outcome with a Beta-Binomial model (appropriate for a bounded test score out of a fixed number of items) and reports the treatment effect **τ** translated onto the **items scale** ("+3.5 of the letter-sounds items"). For two outcomes that sit on the floor for most children — phonetic spelling (P) and nonword reading (N) — the headline is instead a **risk difference**: the change in the _chance of scoring above zero_ ("coming off the floor"), in percentage points.

**How to read the evidence label** (used throughout): it grades the probability that the effect is in the stated direction — _suggestive_ ≥ 75%, _moderate_ ≥ 91%, _strong_ ≥ 97%, _very strong_ ≥ 99%; below 75% is _inconclusive_. The label describes **confidence in direction, not size**. A separate ROPE figure ("too small to matter") answers whether the effect is big enough to care about.

## Convergence gate

All 27 ITT models **passed** the gate (R̂ ≤ 1.01, ESS ≥ 400, zero divergences, BFMI ≥ 0.30) — so every result below is safe to interpret.

## The one-paragraph story

The programme most clearly helps the skills it directly teaches and the skills closest to decoding: **very strong** evidence for **letter-sound knowledge**, and **strong** evidence for **word reading**, **phoneme blending**, and **directly taught expressive vocabulary**. Effects on **broad, standardised vocabulary (R and E)** are **inconclusive and probably negligible** (most of the posterior sits inside the "too small to matter" zone) — the benefit does not generalise to general vocabulary over the trial window. The floored spelling/nonword measures lean positive but are too uncertain to call.

## Robustness (the 013–028, 113/114 variants)

The letter-sound and word-reading effects are stable no matter how we stress-test them:

- **Adjusting for general non-verbal ability** (block design; ITT-017–024): L stays +3.5 (very strong), W +2.2 (strong) — the effect is not just abler children doing better.
- **Adjusting for family socioeconomic status** (ITT-013 W +2.5; ITT-113 L +3.4) and its **matched complete-case comparators** (ITT-014 W +2.4; ITT-114 L +3.9): unchanged.
- **Adjusting for site** (ITT-027 W +2.6; ITT-028 L +3.5): unchanged.

That an adjustment barely moves the estimate is the reassuring result: the causal read-out does not depend on these background variables.

## What is causal here, and what is not

Only **τ** (the group contrast) is causal, and only because assignment was randomised. The models deliberately include just a child's **own baseline** score and **age** as _precision_ terms — they are not "predictors of who improves" and should not be read that way (that would be the Table-2 fallacy). Which covariates appear was fixed in advance by the study's causal diagram (DAG); a skill that is absent was excluded by the diagram, not found unimportant.

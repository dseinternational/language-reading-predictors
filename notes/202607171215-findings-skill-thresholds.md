## The question

"To what extent are there **thresholds** for some skills — points beyond which a skill starts to make a more marked difference to rates of progress?" In statistical terms this is a question about **nonlinearity**: does the relationship between one skill (the exposure) and a reading or language outcome bend, so that the same one-unit gain in the skill buys more (or less) progress at some levels than at others? A straight line says "every extra letter sound is worth the same amount everywhere"; a threshold says "the extra letter sounds start to matter more once you have most of them". This note gathers every place in the model suite where a nonlinearity, knee, moderation, or period-varying slope was actually estimated, and reports what the refitted `reporting`-config models say. It complements the [mechanism](202607161800-findings-mechanism.md) and [dose_response](202607161800-findings-dose_response.md) family notes (which carry the full per-model detail) and the randomised-effect [ITT](202607161800-findings-itt.md) anchors.

A note on the Bayesian vocabulary, for readers more used to frequentist reporting: an **89% credible range/interval** is the interval within which the parameter lies with 89% probability _given the data and priors_ — a direct probability statement about the parameter itself, unlike a confidence interval, which is a statement about the long-run behaviour of the procedure. **P(>0)** (equivalently `prob_positive`, `p_pos`) is the posterior probability that the true effect is positive, so P(>0) = 0.98 means a 98% chance the association points the way stated. **ROPE** = "region of practical equivalence", a band around zero deemed too small to matter in practice. Evidence labels follow the project ladder graded on the direction probability: **inconclusive** (<0.75), **suggestive** (0.75–0.91), **moderate** (0.91–0.97), **strong** (0.97–0.99), **very strong** (≥0.99). Labels below are checked against the raw probabilities, not the rounded percentages.

## The single most important caveat, stated up front

**None of these curves is causal.** The only randomisation-licensed (causal) quantities in the whole suite are the intervention contrasts — `tau` in the ITT family, `tau_t2` in the DiD family, the period-1 on-intervention marginal in the gain-factor family, and the t2 group contrast in level-factors. Everything in _this_ note — every skill→skill slope, every knee, every dose–response, every moderation term, every growth coefficient — is a **latent-ability-confounded adjusted association** or a **descriptive** quantity. A child who knows more letter sounds also tends to have higher general ability, better hearing, more supportive input, and so on; the models adjust for what the causal diagram names, but cannot rule out that shared background explains the bend. So read every "threshold" below as **"the association accelerates"**, never as "reaching this level _causes_ faster progress". Three further scale caveats travel with the headline result and are repeated where they bite: (1) a **bounded score with a logit link produces some curvature mechanically** near the floor and ceiling, so part of any "knee" is a feature of the measurement scale, not the child; (2) the knee's **credible interval is wide**; and (3) "threshold" here means a **smooth acceleration**, not a hard switch that flips at one value.

## The headline: letter sounds → word reading accelerates near mastery

The clearest and most consistent nonlinearity in the suite is the **letter-sound → word-reading** association. Five `mechanism`-family models fit this relationship as a flexible curve (a Gaussian-process link) and each ships a `readiness_threshold.csv` summarising where — if anywhere — the curve steepens. All five tell the same story: the curve is **monotonically increasing and accelerating**, so an extra letter sound is associated with _more_ additional word reading once a child already knows most of the set. The "knee" (the letter-sound count at which the slope steepens) sits near the **top** of the observed 2–32 range, and the slope above the knee is roughly **twice** the slope below it.

| **mech-071** (L → W, moderated by expressive vocab) | 156     | 29.5         | 23.5 – 29.5    | 25.5      | 0.023            | 0.063            | 0.9928                  |

The slopes are on the logit (log-odds) scale; the point is the **ratio**, not the absolute value: above the knee the letter-sound → word-reading association is about **1.9–2.8 times steeper** than below it, and `increasing_frac` ≈ **0.993–0.999** means the curve is rising across essentially every posterior draw (very strong evidence that it is monotone, not U-shaped or saturating). On the items scale, mech-058's fitted curve rises smoothly from roughly 5 words read at 2 letter sounds to about 11 at 32, with the average items difference across the full range **+6.7 items** (89% credible range +2.6 to +10.9, P(>0) = 0.996, **very strong**). The complete-case comparator (mech-158) tracks it closely (+6.9 items, +2.4 to +11.4, P(>0) = 0.996), so attrition is not driving the shape.

**Plain-English reading:** letter-sound knowledge appears to "pay off" more markedly for word reading as a child approaches mastery of the sound set — consistent with a **readiness threshold** in which knowing _most_ of the letter sounds is what starts to unlock word reading, rather than each early letter sound contributing equally. This is exactly the kind of nonlinearity the question asks about. But the three scale caveats bite hardest here: the knee's credible range (20–29.5) is **very wide** and pressed against the top of the data, so the _location_ of the knee is poorly resolved; a bounded count with a logit link will manufacture _some_ upward bend near the ceiling regardless of any real developmental threshold; and the curve is a smooth acceleration, not a switch. And, once more, it is an **adjusted association**, not evidence that drilling letter sounds causes reading to take off.

## Do the other skills show a knee? Dedicated GP knee-test models

The knee analysis is a post-processing step that only runs on models fitted with a flexible **Gaussian-process (GP) curve** for the exposure; it is skipped silently whenever the exposure enters as a straight line. In the main reporting suite, letter sounds is the **only** exposure whose GP curve was kept — the vocabulary-exposure mechanisms (mech-056/057/088/089) were each _tried_ as a GP first and only made linear because the curve would not converge (~200–300 divergences, unmoved at `target_accept` 0.99), so their linear estimand is a single slope in which no knee can be looked for. So the honest statement for every skill except letter sounds _was_ "curvature untested", not "the relationship is linear".

To close that gap we added six dedicated GP knee-test models — **mech-156 (RV), mech-157 (EV), mech-188 (TR), mech-189 (TE), mech-190 (phoneme blending B), mech-191 (intervention sessions IS)** — each with its own DAG-derived adjustment set (re-derived by a backdoor d-separation search that reproduces the letter-sound and dose-077 sets exactly), each fitting the exposure as an HSGP curve at `target_accept = 0.999` (the setting that stabilises the letter-sound curve) plus the standardised-input HSGP reparameterisation. These fits are **`rep-lite` exploratory** (4 chains × 4000 draws), so read them as an indicative first look, not a signed-off reporting result. Encouragingly, the curves now converge far better than the linear-fallback note reported: divergences are **≤ 0.11 % of draws** across all six (mech-188 clears the gate with **zero**), so the knee is now genuinely readable rather than lost to a funnel.

The single most informative number is **`increasing_frac`** — the fraction of posterior draws in which the fitted curve is net-rising. Near 1.0 means a real, reliably-rising dose-response in which a knee is well-defined (letter sounds: 0.999); near 0.5 means the curve is essentially flat/noisy, so there is no reliable shape and hence no meaningful knee, regardless of any single knee number the algorithm prints.

**So: do the vocabulary measures show a clear knee? Essentially no.** The two **standardised** vocabulary measures (R, E) show `increasing_frac` ≈ 0.57–0.59 — barely above a coin-flip — meaning the adjusted curve is effectively flat and noisy, with a near-null average association (+0.2 / +0.4 items, both intervals spanning zero). There is neither a knee nor a clear dose-response to speak of: broad vocabulary and word reading are not tightly coupled once the reading-system confounders are held fixed. **Taught receptive vocabulary (TR)** is the one vocabulary measure that behaves like a real rising curve (`increasing_frac` 0.98, and the only one to clear the convergence gate cleanly), with a moderate positive association (+3.7 items, 96 % positive) and a knee near the top of the taught-word range (~18 of ~20) — but its acceleration is mild (~1.2× steeper above the knee) and nothing like the ~2× of letter sounds, so it is at most a **weak** readiness signal. **Taught expressive vocabulary (TE)** rises early then _saturates_ (knee low, slope decelerating) — the opposite of a late-accelerating threshold. **Phoneme blending (PA)** is flat/weak with no knee. **Intervention sessions (IS)** give a reliably rising, moderate positive dose-response (+2.1 items, 97 % positive, consistent with the linear dose-277 slope) but the knee sits at the bottom edge of the range with a credible interval running to the top — i.e. the steepest rise is early/ill-located, so there is **no clear session threshold**, just a broadly monotone association.

Bottom line for this addition: extending the search to five more skills and the dose does **not** turn up a second clean threshold. Letter sounds remains the only exposure with a strong, well-resolved accelerating knee; taught receptive vocabulary is a distant, mild second; standardised vocabulary, taught expressive vocabulary and blending show no usable knee, and session dose rises without one. Every one of these remains a `rep-lite`, divergence-flagged, **adjusted-association** read (never a causal dose-response), and the scale/geometry caveats below still apply.

## Do letter sounds and vocabulary have to be high _together_? (the joint-readiness interaction)

A distinct threshold question is whether the two skills **interact**: does word reading take off only when letter sounds _and_ vocabulary are both high (synergy — "both above a point"), or does either one help on its own? We test this with the letter-sound → word-reading curve **moderated** by each vocabulary measure in turn: letter sounds enter as the flexible HSGP curve, vocabulary as a standardised main effect `gamma_mod * z(vocab)`, and the interaction as `gamma_int * z(letter sounds) * z(vocab)`. The interaction coefficient is symmetric — it is both "how much the letter-sound slope steepens per +1 SD of vocabulary" and "how much the vocabulary slope steepens per +1 SD of letter sounds". Its sign is the whole answer: **`gamma_int > 0` = synergy** (the two reinforce each other, so being high on both gives a _larger-than-additive_ gain — the "both must be high" pattern); **≈ 0 = additive** (each helps independently, either alone is enough); **< 0 = substitutive** (diminishing returns to having both). Because the GP main effect cancels in the corner double-difference, the super-additive "both-high premium" for a ±1 SD move in both skills is exactly `4 × gamma_int` on the logit scale. Expressive vocabulary (EV) was already fitted as mech-071 (reporting tier); we add receptive (R, mech-093), taught-receptive (TR, mech-094) and taught-expressive (TE, mech-095) at the reporting config (mech-093/094 pass the gate; mech-095 is gate-flagged for divergences).

| L × vocabulary             | Model    | Convergence        | `gamma_int` (interaction, logit) | P(interaction > 0)  | Vocabulary main effect (`gamma_mod`) | "Both-high" premium (4·`gamma_int`) |
| -------------------------- | -------- | ------------------ | -------------------------------- | ------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------- |
| L × expressive vocab (EV)  | mech-071 | passed (reporting) | **−0.065** (−0.15, +0.02)        | 0.11 → **89 % neg** | +0.19 (+0.08, +0.30), P>0 ≈ 1.00     | −0.26 (slightly below additive)     |
| L × receptive vocab (RV)   | mech-093 | passed (reporting) | **−0.053** (−0.14, +0.03)        | 0.15 → **85 % neg** | +0.11 (−0.00, +0.23), P>0 = 0.94     | −0.21                               |
| L × taught-receptive (TR)  | mech-094 | passed (reporting) | **−0.062** (−0.14, +0.02)        | 0.10 → **90 % neg** | +0.19 (+0.08, +0.29), P>0 ≈ 1.00     | −0.25                               |
| L × taught-expressive (TE) | mech-095 | reporting, flagged | **−0.085** (−0.17, +0.00)        | 0.06 → **94 % neg** | +0.21 (+0.10, +0.32), P>0 ≈ 1.00     | −0.34                               |

**The answer is a fairly clear "no — they do not need to be high together".** All four vocabulary measures point the _same_ way: the interaction is consistently **negative** (`gamma_int` −0.05 to −0.08), with the probability of a _positive_ (synergistic) interaction only 0.06–0.15 — i.e. an 85–94 % chance the interaction is, if anything, in the **substitutive** direction. Meanwhile every vocabulary measure has a solidly **positive main effect** (`gamma_mod` +0.11 to +0.21, three of four at ~100 % positive). Put together, the picture is **additive with a hint of diminishing returns, not synergy**: being higher on _either_ letter sounds _or_ vocabulary independently goes with higher word reading, and having both high buys _slightly less_ than the sum of the two — the opposite of a joint-readiness threshold where you would need both. The "both-high premium" is negative (≈ −0.2 to −0.3 logit) in every case, so the both-high corner sits at or a little _below_ the additive expectation, never above it.

This mirrors the [blending result below](#does-blending-gate-decoding-mech-072-vs-mech-172): letter sounds and the other code/language skills tend to **substitute at the margin** rather than gate or amplify one another. Read it cautiously, though: the direction is only _suggestive-to-moderate_ (no interval excludes zero), the interaction is a **linear product** (a genuinely nonlinear two-dimensional threshold surface is not identifiable at n ≈ 54 children), and — importantly — letter sounds and vocabulary are **positively correlated** in this cohort (r ≈ 0.55–0.63), so the children who would settle the question (high on one, low on the other) are relatively few and the interaction is weakly powered. And, as everywhere in this note, these are latent-ability-confounded **adjusted associations**, not causal dose-responses; conditioning on the vocabulary moderator can itself open a collider path for the letter-sound main slope, so the main slope is read only jointly with the moderation.

## Does blending "gate" decoding? (mech-072 vs mech-172)

A natural threshold hypothesis in reading is that letter-sound knowledge only translates into **nonword decoding** once a child can also **blend** sounds together — i.e. blending gates the letter-sound → decoding link. The suite tests this with an interaction: mech-072 fits letter sounds → nonword reading (NW) _moderated by_ blending (PA), against mech-172, the main-effects-only baseline.

The moderation term points the **opposite way** to a gate. The letter-sound × blending interaction is **negative**: median −0.327 on the logit scale (89% credible range −0.570 to −0.093, P(>0) = 0.013, i.e. P(<0) = **0.987, strong**). A negative interaction means the letter-sound → nonword slope is **flatter** when blending is high and **steeper** when blending is low — the two code skills partly **substitute** rather than compound. Blending's own main-effect association is positive (median +0.361 logit, P(>0) = 0.985, **strong**; the no-interaction baseline mech-172 puts it at +0.281, P(>0) = 0.958, **moderate**). Model comparison mildly prefers the interaction model — leave-one-out (LOO) gives mech-072 the higher expected predictive accuracy with stacking weight 0.88 vs 0.12 — but the gap is small and inside the noise (elpd difference −3.0, standard error 2.6; the |elpd_diff| < 4 guard means this is _not_ a decisive win).

**Plain-English reading:** the data give **no support for blending gating decoding**; if anything, letter-sound knowledge is _more_ strongly associated with nonword decoding when blending is _weak_ — the profile of two partly-interchangeable code skills, not a threshold where one has to be in place before the other counts. All adjusted associations, none causal; and the nonword outcome is heavily floored (many children score zero), which limits resolution.

## Does the pay-off differ by age? (mech-073, gc-085, and the age-moderation LOO)

Two models let the skill pay-off vary with age. mech-073 (age moderating the letter-sound→reading slope) comes back **weak**; gc-085 (baseline-age × ability on growth rate) finds **small but mostly-resolved positive interactions**, clearest for letter sounds. Neither, though, is an age **threshold** — they are smooth gradients, not a level beyond which skills abruptly matter more.

- **mech-073** (letter sounds → word reading, age-moderated): the age × letter-sound interaction is a small **negative** median −0.057 logit (89% −0.119 to +0.006, P(<0) = 0.928, **moderate**) — a hint that the letter-sound → word-reading slope is slightly _flatter_ for older children — with the age main-effect also mildly negative (−0.069, P(<0) = 0.909, moderate). But the cross-model comparison in `output/statistical_models/comparison/age_moderation_loo_compare.csv` shows the age-interaction model (mech-073) does **not** meaningfully out-predict its no-interaction twin (mech-173): elpd difference just −1.0 (standard error 1.2; |elpd_diff| < 4), so the moderation does not earn its keep. Read as: **weak, unresolved** evidence of a slightly smaller pay-off at older ages, not a threshold.
- **gc-085** (multivariate growth curves, baseline-age × ability interaction on the growth _rate_): the interaction is **inconclusive only for grammar** (+0.008, 89% −0.099 to +0.116, P = 0.55, essentially zero) and leans **positive for the others** — receptive vocab +0.045 (P = 0.86, suggestive), word reading +0.100 (−0.075 to +0.278, P = 0.82, suggestive), expressive vocab +0.073 (P = 0.95, moderate) and letter sounds +0.268 (+0.043 to +0.494, P = 0.97, **strong**). So it does **not** reproduce the age × ability interaction the gain-factor models hinted at for **grammar/concepts** specifically, but it does show a small, GA-confounded, positive age × ability interaction on growth rate for the reading/vocabulary outcomes (clearest for letter sounds). What _is_ clearly resolved in gc-085 is a separate, simpler nonlinearity: the **baseline-age main effect on growth rate is negative for every outcome and strongly so for word reading** (gamma_age[W] = −0.396, 89% −0.541 to −0.256, interval entirely below zero) — older-at-baseline children grow more slowly, most markedly on word reading. That is an age _gradient_, not an age-threshold on any skill's pay-off, and it is a descriptive, ability-confounded association.

## Is the dose association front-loaded? (dose-077 / 083 / 084 / 177)

The `dose_response` family lets the session-dose slope differ across the study's three transition periods, which is the nearest thing the suite has to a "does more of the skill/exposure matter more at some stage" test. For word reading and letter sounds the slope is **front-loaded** — strongest in period 1 (the randomised, all-untreated-baseline transition) and attenuating thereafter — while blending peaks in the middle period.

Word reading and letter sounds decline monotonically from a very strong period-1 slope to a weak, uncertain period-3 slope; blending breaks the pattern by peaking in period 2. **But the evidence for genuine between-period variation is weak**: the between-period slope standard deviation (`sigma_dose_between_period`) has an 89% lower bound essentially at zero (0.010–0.026) in every model, and the pooled no-variation comparator (dose-277) fits well and lands at roughly the period-average slope (~0.143 logit; on the items scale +1.28 items per extra standard deviation of sessions, ≈ 31 sessions, 89% +0.57 to +2.04, P(>0) = 0.998, **very strong** — but observational). So treat front-loading as **suggestive, not established**, and note it is a _period_ pattern (early transitions show a steeper dose slope), not a _dose threshold_ — no model finds a nonlinear knee _in sessions_, and dose is a partial collider, so none of this is causal.

## Where the gain-factor interaction terms land (gf-001)

The gain-factor family carries treatment-moderation terms that would, if resolved, indicate a threshold in _who benefits_. In the word-reading model gf-001 all three are **inconclusive**:

- treatment × own-baseline: median −0.060 logit (89% −0.272 to +0.143, favoured-direction prob 0.676) — **inconclusive**;
- treatment × ability: +0.005 (89% −0.177 to +0.185, prob 0.518) — **inconclusive**;
- age × ability: +0.011 (89% −0.084 to +0.106, prob 0.576) — **inconclusive**.

So there is **no strong evidence of effect-modification thresholds** in the word-reading gain model — no resolved sign that the intervention pays off more for children who start higher, or who are more able, or at a particular age. (Elsewhere in the gain-factor family a _negative_ treatment × own-baseline interaction recurs at moderate-to-strong evidence for several vocabulary and grammar outcomes — children starting higher show a smaller on-intervention increment — with a strong _positive_ exception for phonetic spelling; but these are adjusted associations, and for word reading specifically the term is inconclusive.)

## Families that carry no nonlinearity estimand

For completeness: the **ITT**, **joint**, **level-factors**, **aligned**, **adjusted**, **concurrent**, **mediation**, **measurement**, **horseshoe**, **lcsm**, **growth (core)**, and **Byrne/RLM historical-growth** families are all **linear-in-predictor** (typically a logit-linear Beta-Binomial or Gaussian model) with no spline, knee, or threshold term, so they contribute no direct threshold signal. Three carry threshold-_adjacent_ quantities worth noting: (a) the **mediation** family's confounding-sensitivity _tipping point_ — the fraction of a mediator→outcome path that would have to be pure confounding to erase the indirect effect (0.525 for the letter-sound route in med-059, 0.60 in the time-ordered med-076), all only moderately robust; (b) the **lcsm** _change-on-change_ test (lcsm-091), which finds prior _change_ in a skill does **not** predict later word-reading change (h_L = +0.047, P = 0.645, inconclusive) — only prior _level_ does; and (c) the **Byrne/RLM** historical-growth trajectories, which show clear _curvature over waves_ (the Down-syndrome word-reading trajectory accelerates at wave 3→4 then plateaus at 4→5, final increment +2.0 items, P(>0) = 0.85) — a descriptive trajectory shape, not a skill threshold.

## Bottom line

There is **one genuine, replicated nonlinearity** in the suite: the **letter-sound → word-reading association accelerates as a child nears mastery of the sound set** (knee ≈ 29.5 of 32, slope above the knee ≈ twice the slope below, monotone across ≈99.9% of posterior draws, in all five models that fit the curve). That is consistent with a **readiness threshold** for letter-sound knowledge in early word reading. The dedicated knee-test extension (mech-190/092/156/157/188/189, above) deliberately widened the search to the taught and standardised vocabulary measures, phoneme blending and intervention-session dose — and found **no second clean threshold**: standardised vocabulary shows no rising curve at all, taught receptive vocabulary is a distant mild second (a weak late steepening), taught expressive vocabulary saturates rather than accelerates, blending is flat, and session dose rises monotonically without a locatable knee. The joint-readiness check (mech-093/094/095 + the existing mech-071) is equally negative: letter sounds and vocabulary do **not** have to be high _together_ — all four vocabulary measures show a mildly _substitutive_ (negative) interaction with letter sounds, not the positive synergy a "both above a threshold" story needs, while each skill's main effect is independently positive. Elsewhere the thresholds are likewise either **absent** (blending does not gate decoding either — the code skills substitute here too), **weak/inconclusive** (age moderation of the pay-off; the growth-rate age × ability interaction), or **suggestive-only period patterns rather than knees** (dose is front-loaded early but the between-period variation is barely resolved). And the overriding caveats hold throughout: these are **adjusted associations, not causal dose–responses**; a **bounded score on a logit link manufactures some curvature mechanically**, so a knee is partly a scale artefact; the **knee's credible interval is wide**; and **"threshold" here means an accelerating association, not a hard switch**.

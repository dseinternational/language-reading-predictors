## What this family does and the question it answers

Some skills in this study are so hard for these children that most score **zero** at every timepoint — the score is "on the floor". Two skills are like this: **phonetic spelling (PS)** — spelling words the way they sound, scored out of 92 items (test SPPHON) — and **nonword reading (NW)** — reading made-up letter strings aloud, scored out of just 6 items. When almost everyone scores zero, asking "how many more items did children get?" is nearly hopeless: there is barely any variation to model. So the survival family reframes the question. Instead of asking _how much_ a child improves, it asks _when, if ever, a child first gets off the floor_ — that is, first scores above zero. In survival-analysis language the "event" is coming off the floor, and the model estimates the **hazard**: the chance that a child who is still stuck at zero finally moves above it during a given interval between timepoints.

Concretely, each model is a **discrete-time off-floor hazard model** (also called a person-period model). Every child who starts at the floor at timepoint 1 contributes one row per interval they are still at risk — the three intervals t1→t2, t2→t3, t3→t4 — up to and including the interval in which they come off the floor. A **complementary-log-log (cloglog) link** turns the predictors into a hazard, and because the link is roughly multiplicative, each coefficient exponentiates to a **hazard ratio** (a multiplier on the chance of coming off the floor in an interval; above 1 = more likely / sooner, below 1 = less likely / later). This is the one thing the survival family does that no other family does: it treats "escaping the floor at all" as the outcome, which is exactly the signal the ordinary count models struggle to see.

Two questions it answers. First, is being in the immediate-intervention arm associated with coming off the floor sooner (the `tau` term)? Second, which pre-intervention characteristics — baseline age, baseline letter-sound knowledge, baseline word reading — are associated with coming off the floor sooner (the `beta` terms)? Both are **prognostic** questions ("who moves off the floor, and when?"), and this is a **descriptive / association** family: `causal_status = none` for both models.

## How to read these numbers (short recap)

- **Point estimate = the median** of the posterior (the distribution of plausible values after seeing the data). We report the **89% credible interval** — the range holding 89% of that posterior probability (the Bayesian analogue of a confidence interval; 89% rather than 95% is the deliberate house standard). The survival summary CSV carries only the 89% interval, so no inner 50% band is quoted here.
- **Direction is read from the tail probability** `P(>0)` — the posterior probability the effect is positive — not from whether an interval excludes zero, and never a p-value. The fixed evidence ladder labels a _named directional claim_: **suggestive / moderate / strong / very strong** at tail probability ≥ 0.75 / 0.91 / 0.97 / 0.99; below 0.75 is **inconclusive**.
- **Interpretable units.** Effects here live on the **hazard scale**, so the natural unit is a **hazard ratio** (per-interval multiplier on the chance of coming off the floor), plus the **baseline off-floor probability** the model fits for an untreated child at mean covariates in each interval. The study's usual "translate the probability effect into test items" step does _not_ apply — a hazard is not a proportion-correct, so an items translation would be meaningless. The `beta` covariate hazard ratios are **per one standard deviation (SD)** of the standardised baseline predictor.
- **Causal vs association — the key rule for this family.** _Nothing here is causal._ `tau` looks like a treatment term and its prior is even labelled "causal" in the priors table, but the family designation overrides that: `tau` is anchored on the immediate arm's randomised first interval, yet the estimate pools all three intervals, and **by the final wave both arms have been treated**, so it is a prognostic association, not a randomised effect of record. The `beta` covariates are ordinary adjusted associations. Beware the **Table-2 fallacy** — do not read "children with higher baseline word reading come off the floor sooner" as "raising word reading _causes_ earlier off-floor movement". Residual confounding by latent general ability remains, and concurrent (same-wave) letter-sound knowledge is deliberately **excluded** because it would be a treatment-affected mediator.

### surv-009 — phonetic spelling (PS)

Of the 42 children at the floor at baseline, only **17** came off it across the three intervals (100 person-period rows; 1 row dropped). The treatment term `tau` has a hazard ratio of **0.84** (89% CI 0.46 to 1.56), i.e. the immediate arm's per-interval chance of coming off the floor is a shade _lower_, but the interval spans a halving to a 1.5-fold increase and P(<0) is only **0.67** — **inconclusive**. In plain terms: this model sees no clear signal that intervention arm is associated with when children first spell phonetically.

The prognostic covariates are more informative. **Baseline letter-sound knowledge** carries a hazard ratio of **1.41 per SD** (89% CI 1.03 to 1.94, P(>0)=0.96, **moderate**): children who already knew more letter sounds tended to come off the spelling floor sooner — unsurprising, since phonetic spelling depends on knowing sounds. **Baseline word reading** is similar, HR **1.32 per SD** (89% CI 0.96 to 1.79, P(>0)=0.92, **moderate**). **Baseline age** is HR **1.17 per SD** (89% CI 0.88 to 1.55, P(>0)=0.82, **suggestive**): weakly positive, older children slightly sooner. All three are adjusted associations, not levers. The fitted baseline off-floor probability for an untreated child at mean covariates declines across intervals — **22%** [12%, 36%] in t1→t2, **19%** [8%, 38%] in t2→t3, **16%** [6%, 35%] in t3→t4 — so the children still stuck at the floor by later waves are the hardest cases.

### surv-011 — nonword reading (NW)

Nonword reading has a much lower ceiling (6 items) and more movement: of **36** children at the floor at baseline, **22** came off it (74 person-period rows, none dropped). Here `tau` is positive, hazard ratio **1.35** (89% CI 0.75 to 2.44, log-hazard 0.301 [−0.282, 0.890]), P(>0)=**0.80** — **suggestive** that the immediate arm is associated with coming off the nonword-reading floor sooner. As an illustration of what a 1.35 hazard ratio means in per-interval terms: it lifts a baseline off-floor chance of roughly 27% to roughly 35% in the first interval — a difference of about 8 percentage points at that interval (illustrative only; the estimand is the hazard ratio, and this is still a prognostic association, not a randomised effect).

The covariate story mirrors P but is stronger. **Baseline word reading** is the standout: hazard ratio **1.64 per SD** (89% CI 1.18 to 2.32, P(>0)=**0.99**, **very strong**) — children reading more real words at baseline came off the nonword floor markedly sooner, which is intuitive since both draw on decoding. **Baseline letter-sound knowledge** is HR **1.39 per SD** (89% CI 1.03 to 1.90, P(>0)=0.96, **moderate**). **Baseline age** points the other way but weakly, HR **0.91 per SD** (89% CI 0.68 to 1.20, P(<0)=0.71, **inconclusive**). The fitted untreated baseline off-floor probability is **27%** [16%, 43%], **29%** [14%, 53%], **22%** [9%, 47%] across the three intervals — flatter and higher than for P, consistent with more children escaping the nonword floor.

## What the family concludes

Read together, the two survival models tell a coherent prognostic story that the ordinary count models cannot: **coming off the floor is governed mainly by where a child starts on the code-related skills.** Baseline letter-sound knowledge predicts earlier off-floor movement for _both_ spelling and nonword reading (moderate in each), and baseline word reading is a strong-to-very-strong predictor for nonword reading in particular. These are associations, not levers, but the ordering is exactly what a decoding account of these skills would predict.

On the intervention-arm term, the two models diverge in direction but agree in message with the ITT floored-outcome analysis. The survival family complements **itt-009 (PS)** and **itt-011 (NW)** by asking a different question of the same floored data: ITT asks "did assignment raise the _probability of being off the floor_ at the first post-baseline wave?" (a risk difference in percentage points), whereas survival asks "did assignment shift the _timing_ of first off-floor movement across all intervals?" (a hazard ratio). For **N**, both agree the direction is favourable and only _suggestive_: itt-011 reports +10.0 percentage points off-floor (P(helps)=0.877, suggestive), and surv-011 reports a hazard ratio of 1.35 (P(>0)=0.80, suggestive). For **P**, both are _inconclusive_: itt-009 reports +4.1 percentage points (P(helps)=0.724), and surv-009's treatment hazard ratio of 0.84 is inconclusive. Neither survival model, on its own, adds a clean intervention signal — but neither contradicts the ITT read, and the survival framing recovers the prognostic covariate structure that the floored count models cannot see.

## Caveats and convergence

- **Convergence: both pass cleanly.** surv-009 — 0 divergences, maximum R-hat 1.0002, minimum ESS ≈ 12,500. surv-011 — 0 divergences, maximum R-hat 1.0007, minimum ESS ≈ 14,000. Both clear the gate (R-hat ≤ 1.01, ESS ≥ 400, BFMI ≥ 0.3, 0 divergences) with room to spare; all six per-chain BFMI values are near or above 1.0. Posterior-predictive coverage is adequate: surv-009 has both group cells inside the 50% and 90% prediction ranges; surv-011 has 1 of 2 inside the 50% range and both inside the 90% range. (PSIS-LOO was computed — ELPD −45.1 for P, −40.8 for N — but with only two models there is nothing to rank against.)
- **These are associations, not causal effects.** `tau` is prognostic (both arms treated by the final wave), and every `beta` is an adjusted association. Do not read the covariate hazard ratios as manipulable levers (Table-2 fallacy). Residual confounding by latent general ability remains; the model has no random intercept to absorb stable between-child differences, so heterogeneity is unmodelled beyond the three baseline covariates.
- **Small samples, wide intervals.** Only 42 and 36 children are at risk, with 17 and 22 events; every 89% interval is wide, and point estimates that just clear a threshold are on average magnitude-inflated (the winner's curse). Lead with the intervals and read direction and ordering, not precise magnitudes — the family's own renderer says exactly this.
- **Floor effects and the exploratory context.** These models exist _because_ P and N are heavily floored; that same flooring makes the analysis exploratory. The companion ITT floored headlines (itt-009, itt-011) are flagged in the ITT note as **provisional** — they pass the convergence gate but trip a separate floor-specific release gate (an unresolved prior-data conflict, with the required sensitivity grid not yet produced). The survival models here carry no such release-gate flag, but they share the underlying floored data and its limitations, so the N _suggestive_ signal in particular should be triangulated, not quoted as settled.

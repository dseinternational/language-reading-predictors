This note reports every model in the level-factors family and, following the shared brief, treats the adjusted associations as seriously as the one causal quantity.

## What this family does and the question it answers

The study is a **waitlist-crossover randomised trial**: children with Down syndrome were randomly assigned either to receive a reading/phonics intervention immediately or to receive it after a wait, and were assessed at four timepoints (t1–t4). The immediate arm is on the intervention from t1; the wait-list arm crosses over to it after t2. That single fact governs everything below: a group difference measured **at t2** is created by randomisation alone (only one arm has been treated), but any group difference **at t3 or t4** compares two arms that have _both_ been treated, so it is no longer a randomised comparison.

The level-factors family models each skill's **score at each timepoint** — the absolute level, not a change from baseline — as a function of randomised arm and general ability, each allowed to act differently at each wave. Concretely, group×time (`b_grp_time`) is a vector of four coefficients, one per timepoint, and ability×time (`gamma_ability_time`) is likewise a per-timepoint vector. This is the deliberate contrast with the gain-factors family (note 02), which conditions on each child's own baseline and models the _transition_. Here there is **no own-baseline term**: conditioning a level model on another skill's contemporaneous level would be adjusting for a post-treatment mediator, so it is excluded by design (revised DAG, #247). A causal diagram (DAG) is the directed graph of assumed cause-and-effect links that fixes which variables may be adjusted for.

The question this family answers that the others do not: **how does each skill's level differ between the two arms across the whole four-wave trajectory, and how strongly does general ability track that level at each wave?** It is the "levels view" companion to the gain-based headline. Its one causal quantity — the t2 group contrast — is a cross-check on the intention-to-treat (ITT) effect, and its ability×time gradient is the family's main descriptive "who scores higher" signal.

## How to read these numbers (recap)

See the shared reading guide for the full version. In brief:

- The point estimate is the posterior **median** (the posterior is the full probability distribution for a quantity given data and priors); the median is read on the logit scale (log-odds) or, translated, on the probability (proportion-correct) and items scales.
- Uncertainty is the **89% credible interval** (an 89% posterior probability the value lies in the range), with the inner **50%** as the "most-of-the-mass" range. 89%, not 95%, is the house standard.
- Direction is the **tail probability**, e.g. P(effect > 0); it feeds a fixed **evidence ladder**: inconclusive (< 0.75) / suggestive (≥ 0.75) / moderate (≥ 0.91) / strong (≥ 0.97) / very strong (≥ 0.99). The label describes strength of evidence for a directional claim, never the size of the effect.
- Effects are modelled on a probability scale and translated to **items** by multiplying by the test maximum. For the heavily-floored outcomes P and N the model instead estimates an **off-the-floor risk difference** (a change in the probability of scoring above the floor), reported in percentage points (pp), not items.
- **Causal versus association — the crucial rule for this family.** Only the **t2 group contrast** (`b_grp_time[1]`) is causal: at t2 only the immediate arm has been treated, so randomisation licenses the claim. Every other quantity is an **adjusted association** — the t1 contrast `b_grp_time[0]` (a pre-randomisation baseline-balance check), the t3/t4 contrasts `b_grp_time[2]`/`b_grp_time[3]` (post-crossover, both arms treated), the age coupling (`gamma_A`), the ability×time gradient (`gamma_ability_time`), the group×ability interaction (`gamma_grp_ability`), and the exogenous confounders (hearing `gamma_hs`, speech production `gamma_deapp_c`, phonological memory `gamma_erbto`). Reading a control-variable coefficient as a lever is the Table-2 fallacy. Residual confounding by latent general ability remains for all associations, and the child random intercept (a per-child offset that pools stable individual differences) does **not** stand in for general ability. The adjustment set for each outcome is fixed in advance from the DAG, so a confounder absent from a model was excluded by the diagram, not found unimportant.

One further caveat specific to this family: because the level model conditions on **general ability and the exogenous confounders but not on each child's own baseline score**, the t2 contrast is an _absolute_ level difference, and it therefore absorbs any residual baseline (t1) imbalance between arms. Where the t1 contrast `b_grp_time[0]` is not near zero, read the t2 contrast against it — a treatment effect should show up as t2 _diverging_ from t1, not merely as a non-zero t2 level. The gain-based families (ITT, difference-in-differences, gain-factors) difference the baseline out and are the cleaner, more efficient read of the randomised effect; this family is the corroborating levels view.

### Word and code-related reading skills

**Word reading, W (lf-001).** The causal t2 contrast is a logit +0.217 (50% credible interval +0.053 to +0.383; 89% −0.171 to +0.612), which translates to a median **+1.45 of 79 words** (89% −1.17 to +4.04), or +0.018 on the probability scale. P(benefit) = 0.815 — _suggestive_. Against a pre-specified minimally-important difference of 1 word, P(benefit ≥ 1 item) = 0.61 with 33% of the posterior inside the region of practical equivalence (ROPE, the band around zero counted as "no practical difference"). The t1 contrast is a mild negative (logit −0.209), so the immediate arm starts slightly _below_ the wait-list and pulls level-to-ahead by t2 — a divergence consistent with benefit. The post-crossover contrasts fade (t3 logit +0.134, t4 +0.049, both _inconclusive_), as expected once both arms are treated. The general-ability (Block Design) gradient is a moderate positive at baseline (`gamma_ability_time[0]` +0.261, P = 0.957) tapering to _suggestive_ at later waves, and the linear-age coupling `gamma_A` is a moderate +0.270 (P = 0.940): older and more able children read more words at any wave, as associations.

**Letter-sound knowledge, L (lf-004).** This is the family's clearest causal signal. The t2 contrast is logit +0.419 (50% +1.49 to +3.47 items; 89% logit +0.026 to +0.813), a median **+2.48 of 32 letter sounds** (89% +0.15 to +4.83), +0.077 on the probability scale, P(benefit) = 0.956 — _moderate_, and the 89% interval clears zero. P(benefit ≥ 2 items) = 0.62. The t1 contrast is a noisy negative (logit −0.156, _inconclusive_), so again t2 marks a divergence in the benefit direction. Post-crossover, t3 is flat and t4 leans positive (logit +0.255, _suggestive_) as the crossed-over wait-list arm continues to gain. Block-Design ability is a very strong baseline associate (`gamma_ability_time[0]` +0.337) tapering across waves, and speech production (`gamma_deapp_c`) is a _suggestive_ positive associate (+0.169); note the speech-missingness indicator is a _strong_ negative (−0.266), a data-availability artefact rather than a substantive effect.

**Phoneme blending, B (lf-006).** t2 contrast logit +0.209 (89% −0.191 to +0.605), median **+0.43 of 10** (89% −0.39 to +1.24), +0.043 probability, P(benefit) = 0.798 — _suggestive_. Because the test has only 10 items, the ROPE mass is high (0.87) and P(benefit ≥ 1 item) only 0.13: direction leans positive, practical size uncertain. The t1 contrast is a _suggestive_ negative (logit −0.235), so t2 again shows divergence. Ability×time is _strong_ at t2 (`gamma_ability_time[1]` +0.291), and phonological memory (`gamma_erbto`) is a _moderate_ positive associate (+0.323).

**Phonetic spelling, P (lf-005).** Heavily floored, so the estimand is an off-the-floor risk difference. The t2 contrast is essentially nil: **−0.9 pp** (89% −9.4 to +7.5 pp), P(favoured, negative) = 0.566 — _inconclusive_, with 94% of the posterior inside the ROPE, i.e. very likely negligible. The t1 contrast is a _moderate_ negative (logit −0.579): the immediate arm was well below the wait-list off-floor rate at baseline, which is exactly the kind of imbalance the levels model cannot difference out, reinforcing that P carries no interpretable randomised signal here. The strongest associate by far is phonological memory (`gamma_erbto` +0.707, _very strong_) and age (`gamma_A` +0.436, _strong_): children with better repetition memory and older children are much likelier to be off the spelling floor, as associations.

**Nonword reading, N (lf-011).** Also floored; off-floor risk difference. t2 contrast **+0.5 pp** (89% −10.6 to +11.9 pp), P(benefit) = 0.529 — _inconclusive_, ROPE mass 0.84. As with P, the t1 contrast is a _moderate_ negative (logit −0.557), a baseline imbalance the levels view cannot remove. Phonological memory dominates the associations (`gamma_erbto` +0.636, _very strong_), with speech production a _suggestive_ positive (`gamma_deapp_c` +0.241).

### Broad language measures

**Receptive vocabulary, R (lf-002).** The t2 contrast is _negative_: logit −0.129 (50% −0.194 to −0.067; 89% −0.279 to +0.019), median **−3.80 of 170 words** (89% −8.23 to +0.55), −0.022 probability, P(negative) = 0.918 — _moderate_ evidence the immediate arm sits _below_ the wait-list at t2. This must be read against the baseline: the t1 contrast is already a _moderate_ negative of almost identical size (logit −0.160), so after adjusting for ability the immediate arm was lower at t1 and stays proportionally lower at t2 — there is **no t1→t2 divergence**, hence no evidence of a treatment effect on R, only a residual imbalance the levels model surfaces as a standing gap. This reconciles with the flat, inconclusive ITT τ for R. Post-crossover the gap narrows (t3 _suggestive_ negative, t4 flips to a small positive). The dominant associations are strongly positive and expected: Block-Design ability at every wave (`gamma_ability_time` +0.18 to +0.33, all _very strong_), phonological memory (`gamma_erbto` +0.187, _very strong_), and a _moderate_ age coupling (+0.076).

**Expressive vocabulary, E (lf-003).** Same pattern as R, weaker. t2 contrast logit −0.090, median **−2.35 of 170** (89% −6.44 to +1.80), −0.014 probability, P(negative) = 0.818 — _suggestive_. The t1 contrast is a comparable negative (logit −0.099), so again the "gap" is standing baseline imbalance, not divergence, and it is consistent with the flat ITT τ for E. Post-crossover contrasts drift more negative (t4 logit −0.157, _moderate_), which under crossover is trajectory divergence, not a randomised effect. Block-Design ability is a _very strong_ associate at all waves (+0.25 to +0.33), and phonological memory a _moderate_ positive (+0.149).

### Grammar, concepts and taught vocabulary

**Basic concepts, F (lf-007).** The causal t2 contrast is +0.134 on the logit scale (89% −0.154 to +0.422), which the model translates to **+0.50 of 18 basic concepts** (89% −0.58 to +1.59), P(>0) = 0.772 — _suggestive_ that it helps. Direction and size differ, though: the effect is probably too small to matter — the probability the gain reaches the 1-concept minimally-important difference is only 0.23, and 75% of the posterior sits inside the ±1-concept region of practical equivalence. The associations are pronounced: Block-Design ability is _strong_ to _very strong_ at every wave (`gamma_ability_time` +0.21 to +0.42), and age a _suggestive_ positive (`gamma_A` +0.137). So general ability strongly tracks concept scores; the randomised t2 signal leans positive but is soft.

**Receptive grammar, T (lf-008).** The causal t2 contrast is +0.027 on the logit scale (89% −0.183 to +0.237) = **+0.20 of 32 grammar items** (89% −1.38 to +1.79), P(>0) = 0.584 — _inconclusive_, and probably negligible in size (probability of reaching the 1-item difference 0.21; 68% ROPE mass). Post-crossover t3 leans negative (_suggestive_). Ability×time is _moderate_ at baseline and rises to _very strong_ by t4 (`gamma_ability_time[3]` +0.325), and the group×ability interaction is a _moderate_ positive (+0.176) — a more-able-children-diverge-more association, not a lever.

**Taught receptive vocabulary, TR (lf-009).** t2 contrast logit +0.067, median **+0.36 of 24** (89% −1.01 to +1.72), +0.015 probability, P(benefit) = 0.662 — _inconclusive_. Block-Design ability (`gamma_ability_time` +0.23 to +0.32, _very strong_ throughout) and phonological memory (`gamma_erbto` +0.276, _very strong_) are the strong associates.

**Taught expressive vocabulary, TE (lf-010).** t2 contrast logit +0.069, median **+0.32 of 24** (89% −1.06 to +1.72), +0.013 probability, P(benefit) = 0.643 — _inconclusive_. Phonological memory is a _very strong_ positive associate (`gamma_erbto` +0.414) and Block-Design ability _strong_ to _very strong_ across waves. The post-crossover t3 contrast is a _moderate_ negative (logit −0.328).

## What the family concludes

Read as a set, the level-factors family sends two messages.

First, its **one causal quantity — the t2 group contrast — corroborates the ITT headline in direction, but softly and less efficiently.** The code-related and directly-taught skills lean positive at t2 (L _moderate_, +2.5 letter sounds; W _suggestive_, +1.5 words; B _suggestive_, +0.4; F _suggestive_), while the broad standardised vocabularies (R, E) show _no_ treatment-driven divergence and the floored P/N are flat. That ranking is the same one the ITT suite reports — L (very strong, +3.5 items), W (strong, +2.4), B (strong, +1.0), tapering to inconclusive on R and E — but every level-factors label is one or two rungs weaker than its ITT counterpart. The reason is structural, not substantive: the levels model estimates _absolute_ group levels and does not difference out each child's baseline, so between-child variability swamps the signal and any residual t1 imbalance leaks in. This is visible directly in R and E, where the apparent negative t2 gap is simply the standing t1 imbalance carried forward (`b_grp_time[0]` ≈ `b_grp_time[1]`), not a decline caused by the intervention. The gain-based families — ITT τ, difference-in-differences, gain-factors — difference the baseline away and are the authoritative read; this family is the confirming levels view, and where it appears to disagree (R negative), the disagreement dissolves once you compare t2 against t1.

Second, the family's **associations tell a coherent developmental-scaffolding story.** Two predictors dominate almost every outcome, at essentially every wave: **general non-verbal ability (Block Design)** and **phonological memory (Early Repetition Battery)**. Block-Design ability×time is _very strong_ for R, E, TR, TE and F and _moderate_ for W, L, B; phonological memory is a _very strong_ positive associate of R, TR, TE, P and N. Children who start more able and with better verbal short-term memory sit higher on language and reading at every wave — but these are adjusted associations, confounded by latent general ability and not levers to pull (the Table-2 fallacy). They describe _who scores higher_, which complements the randomised families' account of _what the intervention causes_, and they line up with the mediation family's finding that the word-reading gain runs through the letter-sound route.

## Caveats and convergence

- **Convergence: all 11 models pass the gate cleanly.** Zero divergences in every fit; maximum R-hat across the family is 1.0025 (threshold 1.01); minimum effective sample size is 1,859 (threshold 400); minimum BFMI is 0.65 (threshold 0.3). No fit carries a convergence caveat.
- **F (lf-007) and T (lf-008) — resolved 2026-07-20.** These two originally emitted no items-scale contrast or ROPE report because basic concepts and receptive grammar had no agreed minimally-important difference (δ) in the registry — a deliberate deferral, not a fault. δ was adopted at 1 item for each by the standing ½-natural-maturation rule (both floor to 1) and the two models refit, so their items-scale t2 contrast and ROPE report are now emitted on the same footing as the other nine. The δ values are rule-derived and flagged pending education-lead ratification (#144); if the lead sets a different δ, only the size-claim probabilities (`P(benefit ≥ δ)`, ROPE mass) for F/T shift — the logit and items contrasts and their direction are unaffected.
- **The t2 contrast is an absolute level, not a change.** It does not difference out baseline, so it absorbs residual t1 imbalance (clearest in R, E, P and N, where `b_grp_time[0]` is materially non-zero). Always read t2 against t1; the gain-based families are the cleaner randomised estimate.
- **Only t2 is causal.** The t3 and t4 group contrasts are **post-crossover associations** — both arms have been treated by then — and are labelled as such throughout. The t1 contrast is a pre-randomisation balance check, also an association.
- **Floored outcomes P and N** carry little information at the level scale: their estimand is an off-floor risk difference, both t2 contrasts are inconclusive with high ROPE mass, and their baseline imbalances are large. Interpret them alongside the dedicated survival/floor analyses, not as standalone randomised results.
- **Small sample.** With ~54 children (210–215 child-wave observations), point estimates that just clear a threshold are on average magnitude-inflated; lead with the intervals. The item intervals on R and E are wide because those tests have 170 items.
- **Residual confounding.** Every association remains confounded by latent general ability; the child random intercept pools stable individual differences but does not control for ability. Adjustment sets are DAG-pre-specified, so a confounder absent from a given outcome's model (e.g. no speech term for W) was excluded by the diagram, not found unimportant.

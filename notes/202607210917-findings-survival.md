## What this family probes

Some skills in this study are so hard for these children that most score **zero** at every timepoint — the score sits "on the floor". Two skills are like this: **phonetic spelling (PS)** — spelling words the way they sound, scored out of 92 items (test SPPHON) — and **nonword reading (NW)** — reading made-up letter strings aloud, scored out of just 6 items. When almost everyone scores zero, asking "how many more items did children get?" is nearly hopeless: there is barely any variation to model. So this family reframes the question. Instead of asking _how much_ a child improves, it asks _when, if ever, a child first gets off the floor_ — that is, first scores above zero. In survival-analysis language the "event" is coming off the floor, and the model estimates the **hazard**: the chance that a child who is still stuck at zero finally moves above it during a given interval between waves.

Concretely, each model is a **discrete-time off-floor hazard model** (also called a person-period model). Each child who begins at the floor contributes one row per between-wave interval they are still at risk — the intervals running across the three phases — up to and including the interval in which they come off the floor. A **complementary-log-log (cloglog) link** turns the predictors into a hazard, and because that link is roughly multiplicative, each coefficient exponentiates to a **hazard ratio**: a multiplier on the chance of coming off the floor in an interval, where a value above 1 means _more likely / sooner_ and below 1 means _less likely / later_. This is the one thing the survival family does that no other family does — it treats "escaping the floor at all" as the outcome, which is exactly the signal the ordinary count models struggle to see.

**What it controls for.** Both models adjust for the same pre-specified, DAG-fixed set of pre-intervention characteristics — **baseline age, baseline letter-sound knowledge, and baseline word reading** — the prognostic `beta` terms. These are the child's starting position on the code-related skills that a decoding account expects to govern early spelling and nonword reading. Concurrent (same-wave) letter-sound knowledge is deliberately **excluded**, because conditioning on it would mean adjusting for a treatment-affected mediator. Both models are marked `estimand = descriptive` with `causal_status = none`: this is a **descriptive / association** family, and _nothing in it is a causal effect_.

## How to read these numbers

- **Point estimate = the median** of the posterior (the distribution of plausible values after seeing the data). Uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** — the range holding 89% of the posterior probability, the Bayesian counterpart of a confidence interval; 89% rather than the customary 95% is the deliberate house standard. The survival key-findings summary carries the 89% interval only, so no inner 50% band is quoted here.
- **Direction is read from the tail probability** — the posterior probability the effect points the favoured way — not from whether an interval excludes zero, and never as a p-value (there are none here). The fixed evidence ladder labels a _named directional claim_: below 0.75 is **inconclusive**, then **suggestive** (≥ 0.75), **moderate** (≥ 0.91), **strong** (≥ 0.97), **very strong** (≥ 0.99). The label describes the strength of evidence for the direction, never the size of the effect.
- **Interpretable units.** Effects here live on the **hazard scale**, so the natural unit is the **hazard ratio** (per-interval multiplier on the chance of coming off the floor), together with the **baseline off-floor probability** the model fits for an untreated child at mean covariates across the intervals. The study's usual "translate the effect into test items" step **does not apply** — a hazard is not a proportion-correct, so an items translation would be meaningless.
- **Causal vs association — the key rule for this family.** _Nothing here is causal._ The `tau` term looks like a treatment coefficient, but the family designation is decisive: because **both arms have been treated by the final wave**, and the estimate pools all the intervals, `tau` is a **prognostic association over all waves, not a randomised effect of record**. The `beta` covariates are ordinary adjusted associations. Beware the **Table-2 fallacy** — do not read "children starting higher on word reading come off the floor sooner" as "raising word reading _causes_ earlier off-floor movement". Residual confounding by latent general ability remains for every association here.

### surv-009 — phonetic spelling (PS)

Here 41 children at the floor at baseline contribute 100 person-period rows across the three phases. The treatment term `tau` has a hazard ratio of **0.84** (89% credible range 0.46 to 1.56) for coming off the floor in an interval — a shade _below_ 1, meaning a marginally lower per-interval chance for the treated arm, but the interval spans a near-halving to a roughly 1.5-fold increase. The posterior probability of a negative association is **67%** — **inconclusive** evidence that the reported term accompanies later movement off the floor. In plain terms, this model sees no clear signal that intervention arm is associated with _when_ children first spell phonetically. This is a **prognostic association over all waves**; because both arms are treated by the final wave, it is not a randomised effect of record.

The model also adjusts for baseline age, baseline letter-sound knowledge, and baseline word reading as prognostic `beta` covariates, but the fit's key-findings summary surfaced only the treatment term and the baseline hazard, so no per-covariate hazard ratios are quoted here (the per-parameter survival CSV was not surfaced in the ground truth). For an untreated child at mean covariates, the fitted **baseline off-floor probability ranged from 16% to 22% across intervals** — declining somewhat over time, consistent with the children still stuck at the floor by later waves being the hardest cases.

### surv-011 — nonword reading (NW)

Nonword reading has a much lower ceiling (6 items) and shows more movement: 36 children at the floor at baseline contribute 74 person-period rows. Here `tau` points the other way — a hazard ratio of **1.35** (89% credible range 0.75 to 2.44) for coming off the floor in an interval, with the interval running from a mild reduction to a roughly 2.4-fold increase. The posterior probability of a positive association is **80%** — **suggestive** evidence that the reported term accompanies earlier movement off the floor. As before, this is a **prognostic association over all waves**, not a randomised effect: both arms are treated by the final wave.

The same three baseline prognostic covariates (age, letter-sound knowledge, word reading) are adjusted for; their per-parameter hazard ratios were not surfaced in the ground truth, so they are not quoted. For an untreated child at mean covariates, the fitted **baseline off-floor probability ranged from 22% to 29% across intervals** — flatter and higher than for phonetic spelling, consistent with more children escaping the nonword-reading floor.

## What to take away

The two survival models reframe two heavily-floored skills — where the ordinary count models have almost no variation to work with — as a question about **timing**: when, if ever, does a still-floored child first score above zero? Both fits **pass the convergence gate**, so the posteriors are trustworthy summaries even though the data are sparse.

On the intervention-arm term the two models diverge in direction: for phonetic spelling (PS) the treated-arm hazard ratio is **0.84** and the evidence is **inconclusive** (P(<0) = 0.67); for nonword reading (NW) it is **1.35** and the evidence is **suggestive** in the favourable direction (P(>0) = 0.80). Neither term is causal — both arms are treated by the final wave, so each is a prognostic association pooling all intervals, not a randomised effect. Both models also carry pre-specified baseline prognostic covariates (age, letter-sound knowledge, word reading), but only the treatment term and the baseline off-floor probability were surfaced in the fit's key findings, so the covariate hazard ratios are described qualitatively rather than quoted.

The fitted baseline off-floor probabilities — **16–22% per interval for PS, 22–29% for NW** — put the numbers in plain terms: an untreated child at typical starting values had roughly a one-in-five to one-in-four chance of coming off the floor in any given between-wave interval, higher and steadier for nonword reading than for spelling. As with every result in the study, samples are small (41 and 36 children at risk), the 89% intervals are wide, and these are **descriptive associations, not levers**. The survival framing complements the floored ITT analyses of the same two outcomes by asking about timing rather than a single-wave off-floor probability; the suggestive nonword-reading signal in particular should be triangulated, not quoted as settled.

## What this family probes

The gain-factor models are an **ANCOVA** re-analysis of the reading/phonics intervention. ANCOVA (analysis of covariance) here means: model each skill's score at the _end_ of a period from that same skill's score at the _start_ of the period, plus a set of covariates. Because the study measured children at four timepoints, each child contributes several "period transitions" (start → end), and the model **stacks** them all — every on-intervention period and every untreated period — into one regression, with a **child random intercept** (a per-child offset that lets children who are generally higher or lower sit at their own level, pooling that stable difference across their rows). The score is a count of correct items out of a fixed maximum, so it is modelled on a **logit (log-odds) scale** — the log of the odds of a correct item — and reported back as a change in the expected **proportion correct**, then translated into **items** (proportion × the test maximum) so the effect reads in the natural units of the test.

The family answers two questions the intention-to-treat (ITT) suite does not tackle in one model. **First**, it provides a second, differently-built estimate of the causal intervention effect: the **on-intervention treatment marginal** — the average change caused by being on the intervention, _averaged over the period-1 transition only_ (the genuinely randomised, all-untreated-baseline transition, the same contrast the ITT effect rests on). This is the single quantity in each model that randomisation licenses us to call **causal**; it is one of only three causal quantities in the whole suite (the others being the ITT τ and the difference-in-differences t2 contrast). **Second**, and just as important, it reports a panel of **adjusted associations** — how a child's own baseline, age, cognitive ability, and the baseline levels of _other_ skills relate to how much they gain — which describe _who progresses_, not what the intervention does. The adjustment set for each model (own baseline, linear age, cognitive ability, the DAG-specified upstream skills, and the non-measure confounders hearing, speech and phonological memory) is **fixed in advance from the causal diagram (DAG)**, so a skill absent from a given model was excluded by the diagram, not found unimportant. Socio-economic status is deliberately excluded (it is not a DAG node and is statistically redundant here).

There are 19 models: 11 primary models (`gf-001`–`gf-011`, one per outcome) and 8 **treated-only companions** (`gf-101`–`gf-108`). The companions drop the untreated-period rows and keep only rows where the child was on the intervention; because everyone in that subset is treated, there is no on-intervention contrast, so the companions have **no causal term at all** — every coefficient in them is an adjusted association. They exist to check that the associational structure is stable when we look only at gains made while receiving the intervention.

## How to read these numbers

Full detail is in the index note; briefly: the point estimate is the posterior **median**; uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** (the range the value plausibly occupies, house standard — "there is an 89% posterior probability the value lies in this range"). Direction is read from the **tail probability**, e.g. P(effect > 0) = 0.90, read directly, never as a p-value (there are none here). The **evidence ladder** attaches a claim-strength label to that probability — inconclusive (< 0.75), suggestive (≥ 0.75), moderate (≥ 0.91), strong (≥ 0.97), very strong (≥ 0.99) — oriented to the favoured direction and describing _evidence of direction_, **never** effect size. Size is a separate claim: the project agreed, after its initial results review, a **smallest difference that matters** (a minimally-important difference, δ) for each anchored outcome; `P(benefit ≥ δ)` is the probability the gain clears that threshold, and the **ROPE mass** (region of practical equivalence, the probability the effect is too small to matter either way) quantifies "probably negligible". Because δ was set post-hoc, those size figures should be read beside the threshold-sensitivity analysis. With only ~54 children, estimates that just clear a threshold are on average inflated (the "winner's curse"), so lead with the interval, not the point.

**The one causal quantity in this family is the treatment marginal.** Every covariate coefficient below — own baseline, age, cognitive ability, other skills, hearing/speech/phonological memory, any interaction term — is an **adjusted association**. Reading a control variable's coefficient as if it were a lever is the **Table-2 fallacy**; these coefficients describe patterns, holding the adjustment set fixed. Residual confounding by latent general ability remains for all of them, and the child random intercept only pools stable between-child differences — it does **not** stand in for general ability.

For the two heavily-floored outcomes, phonetic spelling (**PS**) and nonword reading (**NW**), most children score at or near zero, so the model uses a **floor rule**: instead of an item count it models the probability of being _off the floor_ (scoring above zero) at period end using a Bernoulli (yes/no) likelihood. For PS and NW the treatment marginal is therefore a **change in the off-floor probability** — a risk difference in percentage-point terms — not a count of items.

### Outcome by outcome

**Word reading — gf-001 (WR).** The strongest single causal result in the family: an on-intervention gain of about **+2.6 items** on the EWRSWR word-reading test during the randomised first period (89% credible range +0.9 to +4.3). There is a **99% probability the true effect is positive — very strong evidence** the intervention helps. The size claim is also favourable: the probability the benefit reaches the agreed 1-item threshold is 93%, and only 7% of the posterior is too small to matter either way. Its single most clearly resolved adjusted factor is the child's own starting score on this measure — higher starters tended to score higher afterwards (99.9% probability for that direction; an adjusted association, not a cause).

**Letter-sound knowledge — gf-004 (LS).** The other clear causal win: about **+3.3 letter sounds** of 32 during the randomised first period (89% range +1.6 to +5.0), with a **99% probability the effect is positive — very strong evidence** of benefit. The probability the gain reaches the agreed 2-item threshold is 88%, with 12% of the posterior too small to matter. The best-resolved adjusted factor is again the child's own baseline (99.9% probability for direction; an association).

**Phoneme blending — gf-006 (PA).** A positive effect of about **+0.8 of 10 blends** (89% range +0.1 to +1.6), with a **90% probability the effect is positive — suggestive evidence** of benefit. Direction is fairly clear but size is not: the probability the gain reaches 1 whole blend is only 36%, and 64% of the posterior is too small to matter either way, so the effect is probably real but small. Own baseline is the top adjusted factor (99.9%; an association).

**Basic concepts — gf-007 (LF).** About **+1.1 of 18 concepts** on the CELF (89% range +0.1 to +2.0), with an **86% probability the effect is positive — suggestive evidence** of benefit. The probability it reaches the 1-item threshold is 55%, with 45% too small to matter. Own baseline is the best-resolved adjusted factor (99.9%; an association).

**Taught expressive vocabulary — gf-010 (TE).** About **+1.2 of 24 taught expressive words** (89% range +0.0 to +2.3), with an **82% probability the effect is positive — suggestive evidence** of benefit. The probability it reaches 1 word is 59%, with 41% too small to matter — leaning positive, size uncertain. Own baseline is the top adjusted factor (99.9%; an association).

**Taught receptive vocabulary — gf-009 (TR).** About **+1.1 of 24 taught receptive words** (89% range −0.1 to +2.2), with a **63% probability the effect is positive — inconclusive** (the interval spans zero). The probability it reaches 1 word is 53%, with 46% too small to matter. Own baseline is the best-resolved adjusted factor (99.9%; an association).

**Receptive grammar — gf-008 (RG).** About **+0.8 of 32** on the TROG-2 (89% range −0.5 to +2.2), with a **66% probability the effect is positive — inconclusive**. The probability it reaches 1 item is 42%, with 57% too small to matter. Own baseline is the top adjusted factor (99.9%; an association).

**Receptive vocabulary — gf-002 (RV).** The broad standardised-vocabulary story seen throughout the suite: the median is very slightly **negative**, about **−1.4 items** on the ROWPVT (89% range −5.3 to +2.4). There is an **83% probability the true effect is negative — suggestive evidence** of harm, but this must be read against the size figures: the probability of a benefit reaching the agreed 2-item threshold is only 7%, and 52% of the posterior is too small to matter either way. With over half the mass practically negligible and the winner's-curse caution applying to a small sample, this is best read as **no detectable effect**, not established harm. Own baseline is the top adjusted factor (99.9%; an association).

**Expressive vocabulary — gf-003 (EV).** Also flat: about **+1.1 items** on the EOWPVT (89% range −2.1 to +4.3), with a **58% probability the effect is positive — inconclusive**. The probability it reaches the 2-item threshold is 33%, with 61% too small to matter. Own baseline is the best-resolved adjusted factor (99.9%; an association).

**Phonetic spelling — gf-005 (PS, floored).** Using the off-floor rule, the treatment marginal is a **−3 percentage-point** change in the chance of scoring above zero (89% range −13 to +6). There is a **62% probability the effect is negative — inconclusive** evidence that the intervention lowers the chance of coming off the floor. The probability of a benefit reaching 10 percentage points is 1%, and 86% of the posterior is too small to matter — overwhelmingly negligible. Its best-resolved adjusted factor is a code-route skill: baseline **letter-sound knowledge** (99.9% probability for direction; an association).

**Nonword reading — gf-011 (NW, floored).** Also off-floor: **+2 percentage points** in the chance of scoring above zero (89% range −8 to +13). There is a **62% probability the effect is positive — inconclusive** evidence that the intervention raises the chance of coming off the floor. The probability of a benefit reaching 10 percentage points is 12%, with 85% too small to matter. As with spelling, the best-resolved adjusted factor is baseline **letter-sound knowledge** (99.9%; an association). With so few children off the floor, PS and NW are the least informative causal estimates in the family.

## The treated-only companions (gf-101–gf-108)

These re-fit the eight main item-count outcomes on **on-intervention rows only** (dropping the waitlist arm's untreated period 1), so NW falls from ~160 to ~130–135 observations. As designed, the on-intervention contrast becomes constant and is dropped, so **there is no causal coefficient** — every result is an adjusted association describing gains _made while on the intervention_. All eight pass the gate. Each companion's single best-resolved adjusted factor is shown below; every one reaches a 99.9% probability for its direction, and every one is an association, not a cause.

The associational picture is essentially unchanged from the primaries: the child's own baseline is the dominant adjusted predictor everywhere it applies, and for the floored spelling companion (gf-105) — as in the pooled fit — the strongest resolved link is instead baseline letter-sound knowledge, the code route. The stability of these associations across the full-sample and treated-only views is the companions' main contribution.

## The adjusted associations — who progresses (not causal)

The ground truth surfaces, per model, the single **most clearly resolved** adjusted association, and the pattern is consistent: in every item-count model the child's **own starting score** on the outcome is the best-resolved predictor of their end score (a 99.9% probability for the positive direction in each). This is largely the ANCOVA structure itself — higher starters end higher — and is an **adjusted association, not a cause**. The exceptions are the two floored **code** outcomes, phonetic spelling (gf-005, gf-105) and nonword reading (gf-011), whose best-resolved adjusted link is baseline **letter-sound knowledge** rather than own baseline, tracing the code route the mediation family also finds; and the treated-only grammar companion (gf-108), whose best-resolved link is baseline **receptive vocabulary**. All of these describe developmental structure — _who_ progresses, holding the DAG-fixed adjustment set constant — and are subject to the Table-2 fallacy warning: they are patterns, not levers, and residual confounding by latent general ability remains for every one.

## How this compares with the randomised (ITT) result

The gain-factor treatment marginal and the ITT effect τ are built differently — a period-stacked ANCOVA versus a single-outcome intention-to-treat model — but target the same randomised, all-untreated-baseline contrast, so they should agree. Qualitatively they do: the two headline benefits, **word reading** (gf-001, very strong) and **letter-sound knowledge** (gf-004, very strong), are the same clear wins the ITT suite reports; the broad standardised vocabulary outcomes **RV and EV** are flat and probably negligible in both framings; and the floored **PS and NW** outcomes stay inconclusive whichever way the model is built. The direct numeric τ-versus-marginal comparison table lives with the ITT family note (`notes/202607210901-findings-itt-suite.md`) and the cross-model comparison outputs; the numbers are not repeated here because they belong to those fits. Note that basic concepts (LF) and receptive grammar (RG) have no model in the core ITT-001–011 suite, so those two have no ITT comparator and are direction-only reads.

## What to take away

The gain-factor ANCOVA delivers a **second, independent causal estimate** that confirms the intervention's clearest effects. On the causal treatment marginal there is **very strong evidence of benefit** on **word reading** (+2.6 items, 99%) and **letter-sound knowledge** (+3.3 items, 99%); **suggestive evidence** on **phoneme blending** (+0.8 items, 90%), **basic concepts** (+1.1 items, 86%) and **taught expressive vocabulary** (+1.2 items, 82%); and **inconclusive** results on taught receptive vocabulary (63%), receptive grammar (66%), expressive vocabulary (58%), and the two heavily-floored outcomes phonetic spelling (62% toward a decline) and nonword reading (62%). Broad standardised **receptive vocabulary** shows a suggestive-but-probably-negligible lean toward a small decline (83% negative, but 52% of the posterior too small to matter), best read as no detectable effect. This is the same gradient the ITT and difference-in-differences families report, so the headline is robust to how the model is built — but with ~54 children the point estimates that just clear a threshold are on average inflated, so lead with the intervals.

Its distinctive contribution is the **who-progresses** panel. The single best-resolved adjusted predictor is a child's **own baseline** on the outcome (99.9% in every item-count model), except for the floored **code** skills — spelling and nonword reading — where baseline **letter-sound knowledge** leads instead, and treated-only grammar, where baseline **receptive vocabulary** leads. These are descriptions of developmental scaffolding, **not** causal levers, and every one carries the Table-2 fallacy caveat and residual confounding by latent general ability. All 19 fits **pass** the convergence gate cleanly, so there are no computational flags to hold; the binding limits are substantive — small sample, floor effects on PS and NW, and the fact that only the treatment marginal is causal.

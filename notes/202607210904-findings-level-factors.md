## What this family probes

The study is a **waitlist-crossover randomised trial**: children with Down syndrome were randomly assigned either to receive a reading/phonics intervention immediately or to receive it after a wait, and were assessed at four timepoints (t1–t4). The immediate arm is on the intervention from t1; the wait-list arm crosses over to it after t2. That single fact governs everything below: a group difference measured **at t2** is created by randomisation alone (only one arm has been treated by then), but any group difference **at t3 or t4** compares two arms that have _both_ been treated, so it is no longer a randomised comparison.

The level-factors family is the companion **levels view** to the gain-factors family (note 02). Instead of modelling change from baseline, it models each skill's **actual score at each of the four timepoints** — the absolute level — as a function of randomised arm and general ability, with each allowed to act differently at each wave. Concretely, the group-by-time term (`b_grp_time`) is a vector of four coefficients — a separate group gap at each timepoint — and the ability-by-time term (`gamma_ability_time`) is likewise a per-timepoint vector of slopes. This is the deliberate contrast with the gain-factors family, which conditions on each child's own baseline and models the transition.

**What it adjusts for, and what it pointedly does not.** The models adjust for general non-verbal ability and for the revised causal diagram's (DAG's) exogenous confounders — hearing, speech production, and phonological memory (verbal short-term memory) — each entered so that its association with the score can vary. A causal diagram is the directed graph of assumed cause-and-effect links that fixes which variables may be adjusted for. Crucially, the level model carries **no own-baseline term and no other-skill adjusters**: conditioning a levels model on another skill's contemporaneous level would be adjusting for a variable that sits on the causal path from group to outcome — a post-treatment mediator of the very group-by-time effect we want — and would bias it. Those adjusters are therefore excluded by design (revised DAG, #247). The adjustment set for each outcome is fixed in advance from the diagram, so a confounder absent from a given model was excluded by the diagram, not found unimportant.

## How to read these numbers (recap)

See the shared reading guide for the full version. In brief:

- The point estimate is the posterior **median**; uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** (an 89% posterior probability the value lies in the range), not the customary 95%.
- Direction is read from the **tail probability**, e.g. P(effect > 0), fed to a fixed **evidence ladder**: inconclusive (< 0.75) / suggestive (≥ 0.75) / moderate (≥ 0.91) / strong (≥ 0.97) / very strong (≥ 0.99). The label describes strength of evidence for a directional claim, never the size of the effect. Direction and size are separate claims: a high tail probability says an effect is _probably_ in that direction, not that it is _large_.
- Effects are modelled on a probability (proportion-correct) scale and translated back into **items** by multiplying by the test maximum. For the two heavily-floored outcomes — phonetic spelling (PS) and nonword reading (NW) — the model instead estimates an **off-the-floor risk difference**: the change in the probability of scoring above zero, reported in percentage points (pp), not items.
- Each headline also carries a **size claim** against a pre-specified minimally-important difference (δ, the smallest change the project agreed would matter in practice): the probability the benefit reaches δ, and the ROPE mass — the share of the posterior inside the region of practical equivalence, a band around zero counted as "no practical difference". These δ values were agreed after the initial results review and are post-hoc, so read them beside the threshold-sensitivity analysis.
- **Causal versus association — the crucial rule for this family.** Only the **t2 group contrast** (the group-by-time coefficient at the first post-baseline wave) is causal: at t2 only the immediate arm has been treated, so randomisation licenses a cause-and-effect reading. **Every other quantity is an adjusted association** — the group contrasts at t3 and t4 (post-crossover, both arms treated), the group contrast at t1 (a pre-randomisation baseline-balance check), the ability-by-time gradient, and the exogenous confounders (hearing, speech, phonological memory). Reading a control-variable coefficient as a lever is the Table-2 fallacy. Residual confounding by latent general ability remains for all associations, and the per-child random intercept pools stable individual differences but does **not** stand in for general ability.

One caveat specific to this family: because the level model conditions on general ability and the exogenous confounders **but not on each child's own baseline score**, the t2 contrast is an _absolute_ level difference, and it therefore absorbs any residual baseline imbalance between the arms rather than differencing it out. A treatment effect should show up as t2 _diverging_ from t1, not merely as a non-zero t2 level. The gain-based families (intention-to-treat, difference-in-differences, gain-factors) difference the baseline away and are the cleaner, more efficient read of the randomised effect; this family is the corroborating levels view.

### Word and code-related reading skills

**Word reading, WR (lf-001).** The causal t2 contrast is a median **+1.4 of 79 words** (89% credible range −1.2 to +4.0), with a 81% probability the true effect is positive — _suggestive_ evidence that the intervention helps. Against the agreed minimally-important difference of 1 word, the probability the benefit reaches that size is 61%, and the probability the effect is too small to matter either way (ROPE mass) is 33% — so direction leans positive and a practically-meaningful size is plausible but not settled. Only this t2 comparison is randomised; the later-timepoint group differences, after the wait-list children had crossed over, are associations. Gate: pass.

**Letter-sound knowledge, LS (lf-004).** This is the family's clearest causal signal. The t2 contrast is a median **+2.5 of 32 letter sounds** (89% +0.2 to +4.8), and the 89% interval clears zero. There is a 96% probability the effect is positive — _moderate_ evidence that the intervention helps. Against the agreed 2-item difference, the probability the benefit reaches that size is 62%, with 37% ROPE mass: this is the one level-factors outcome where both direction and a practically-meaningful size are reasonably well supported. The later-wave contrasts are post-crossover associations. Gate: pass.

**Phoneme blending, PA (lf-006).** The t2 contrast is a median **+0.4 of 10** (89% −0.4 to +1.2), with a 80% probability of being positive — _suggestive_ evidence of benefit. Because the test has only 10 items, the practical-size claim is weak: the probability the benefit reaches the agreed 1-item difference is only 13%, and 87% of the posterior sits inside the ROPE. Direction leans positive; practical size is very uncertain. Gate: pass.

**Phonetic spelling, PS (lf-005).** Heavily floored, so the estimand is an off-the-floor risk difference. The t2 contrast is essentially nil: a median **−1 percentage point** change in the chance of scoring above zero (89% −9 to +7 pp), with a 57% probability of being negative — _inconclusive_ evidence, and 94% of the posterior lies inside the ±10 pp ROPE (the probability of reaching the agreed 10-pp difference is only 2%), i.e. very likely negligible. PS carries no interpretable randomised signal at the level scale here; read it alongside the dedicated survival/floor analyses. Gate: pass.

**Nonword reading, NW (lf-011).** Also floored; off-floor risk difference. The t2 contrast is a median **+1 percentage point** (89% −11 to +12 pp), with a 53% probability of being positive — _inconclusive_, ROPE mass 84% and only a 9% probability of reaching the 10-pp difference. As with PS, there is no interpretable randomised signal at the level scale. Gate: pass.

### Broad standardised language measures

**Receptive vocabulary, RV (lf-002).** The t2 contrast is _negative_: a median **−3.8 of 170 words** (89% −8.2 to +0.6), with a 92% probability the effect is negative — _moderate_ evidence, phrased by the model as the intervention being harmful at this wave. Read this with care and with the family caveat in mind. First, the size claim runs the other way: the probability of a benefit reaching the agreed 2-item difference is only 2%, with 24% ROPE mass. Second, and more importantly, this is an _absolute_ level model that does not difference out each child's baseline, so a negative t2 level gap can reflect residual baseline imbalance carried forward rather than a decline caused by the intervention. The gain-based intention-to-treat (ITT) family, which differences the baseline out, reports a flat, inconclusive effect on RV (see notes 01 and the reading-guide spoiler); that is the authoritative randomised read, and the level-factors negative should be treated as a levels-view artefact to reconcile against it, not as evidence of harm. Gate: pass.

**Expressive vocabulary, EV (lf-003).** The same pattern as RV, weaker. The t2 contrast is a median **−2.4 of 170** (89% −6.4 to +1.8), with an 82% probability of being negative — _suggestive_ evidence of decline. The probability of a benefit reaching the agreed 2-item difference is 5%, with 40% ROPE mass. As with RV, this is an absolute level gap that absorbs baseline imbalance and is consistent with the flat ITT effect for EV; the gain-based families are the cleaner read. The later-wave contrasts are post-crossover associations. Gate: pass.

### Grammar, concepts and taught vocabulary

**Basic concepts, LF (lf-007).** The causal t2 contrast is a median **+0.5 of 18 basic concepts** (89% −0.6 to +1.6), with a 77% probability of being positive — _suggestive_ evidence of benefit. Direction and size differ, though: the effect is probably too small to matter — the probability it reaches the agreed 1-concept difference is 23%, and 75% of the posterior lies inside the ±1-concept ROPE. Gate: pass.

**Receptive grammar, RG (lf-008).** The causal t2 contrast is a median **+0.2 of 32 grammar items** (89% −1.4 to +1.8), with a 58% probability of being positive — _inconclusive_, and probably negligible in size (a 21% probability of reaching the 1-item difference; 67% ROPE mass). Gate: pass.

**Taught receptive vocabulary, TR (lf-009).** The t2 contrast is a median **+0.4 of 24** (89% −1.0 to +1.7), with a 66% probability of being positive — _inconclusive_. The probability of reaching the 1-item difference is 23%, with 72% ROPE mass. Gate: pass.

**Taught expressive vocabulary, TE (lf-010).** The t2 contrast is a median **+0.3 of 24** (89% −1.1 to +1.7), with a 64% probability of being positive — _inconclusive_. The probability of reaching the 1-item difference is 22%, with 72% ROPE mass. Gate: pass.

Note on LF and RG: basic concepts and receptive grammar are reported here on the same items-scale footing as the other graded outcomes, with a minimally-important difference of 1 item adopted for each (rule-derived and pending education-lead ratification, like the rest of the registry). If the lead sets a different δ, only the size-claim probabilities (probability of reaching δ, and ROPE mass) for LF and RG would shift — the items contrast and its direction are unaffected.

### The associations, in direction only

Two adjusted associations recur across the family, and the ground truth for this refit reports them at the design level rather than as surfaced coefficients, so they are described here in direction only, without numbers. **General non-verbal ability** (entered as an ability-by-time gradient) and **phonological memory** (verbal short-term memory) track higher language and reading levels at essentially every wave: children who start more able and with better verbal memory sit higher on these skills throughout. The exogenous confounders hearing and speech production are adjusted for likewise. Every one of these is an **adjusted association** describing _who scores higher_, confounded by latent general ability, and not a lever to pull (the Table-2 fallacy). They complement the randomised families' account of _what the intervention causes_ rather than competing with it.

## What to take away

Read as a set, the level-factors family sends two messages.

First, its **one causal quantity — the t2 group contrast — corroborates the intention-to-treat headline in direction, but softly and less efficiently.** The code-related skills lean positive at t2 (letter-sound knowledge _moderate_, +2.5 letter sounds and the only interval to clear zero; word reading _suggestive_, +1.4 words; phoneme blending _suggestive_, +0.4; basic concepts _suggestive_, +0.5), while the taught-vocabulary and grammar outcomes are _inconclusive_ (TR, TE, RG), and the two floored outcomes (PS, NW) are flat and negligible. The broad standardised vocabularies RV and EV show _negative_ t2 level gaps (RV _moderate_, EV _suggestive_) — but because this is an absolute-levels model that does not difference out baseline, those gaps most plausibly reflect residual baseline imbalance carried forward rather than intervention-caused decline, and they reconcile with the flat, inconclusive ITT effect on RV and EV. The ranking is the same one the ITT suite reports, but softened by one or two rungs; the reason is structural (between-child variability swamps an absolute-level signal), not substantive. The gain-based families — ITT, difference-in-differences, gain-factors — are the authoritative randomised read; this family is the confirming levels view.

Second, the family's **associations tell a coherent developmental-scaffolding story.** General non-verbal ability and phonological memory track higher levels of language and reading at almost every wave. These are adjusted associations — they describe who scores higher, remain confounded by latent general ability, and are not levers to pull — and they line up with the mediation family's finding that the word-reading gain runs through the letter-sound route.

## Caveats and convergence

- **Convergence: all 11 models pass the gate cleanly.** No fit carries a convergence caveat.
- **Only t2 is causal.** The t3 and t4 group contrasts are **post-crossover associations** — both arms have been treated by then — and are labelled as such throughout. The t1 contrast is a pre-randomisation balance check, also an association.
- **The t2 contrast is an absolute level, not a change.** It does not difference out baseline, so it absorbs residual t1 imbalance. This matters most for RV and EV (where the negative t2 gaps are most plausibly standing baseline imbalance) and for the floored PS and NW. Always read t2 as divergence from t1; the gain-based families are the cleaner randomised estimate.
- **Floored outcomes PS and NW** carry little information at the level scale: their estimand is an off-floor risk difference, both t2 contrasts are inconclusive with high ROPE mass. Interpret them alongside the dedicated survival/floor analyses, not as standalone randomised results.
- **Small sample.** With roughly 54 children (207–215 child-wave observations), point estimates that just clear a threshold are on average magnitude-inflated (the winner's curse), so lead with the intervals. The item intervals on RV and EV are wide partly because those tests have 170 items.
- **Residual confounding.** Every association remains confounded by latent general ability; the child random intercept pools stable individual differences but does not control for ability. Adjustment sets are DAG-pre-specified, and this family deliberately omits own-baseline and other-skill adjusters because conditioning on them would block or bias the group-by-time effect.

# Findings — word-reading growth, cross-family synthesis (outcome W, EWRSWR)

> [!NOTE]
> Drafted by a LLM-based AI tool (Claude Code/Opus 4.8). Cross-family synthesis of the reporting-config refit of 2026-07-16 (see [process note](202607161130-full-statistical-refit.md)); reviewed and extended on 2026-07-17 to cover all models in the family. Preliminary.

## What this note asks

The question is deliberately broad: **what do the data seem to show about the factors influencing growth in word reading (outcome W, the EWRSWR capped item count) across all time points?** No single model answers it. The project fits sixteen model families, and roughly a dozen of them touch word reading from a different angle — the randomised trial effect, what carries that effect, the concurrent skill structure at each wave, the temporal ordering of skills, dose, baseline predictors of who gains, and the shape of the growth curve. This note pulls those threads together. Each per-family reading is written up in its own note; the most relevant are [ITT](202607161800-findings-itt.md), [gain_factors](202607161800-findings-gain_factors.md), [DiD](202607161800-findings-did.md), [level_factors](202607161800-findings-level_factors.md), [aligned](202607161800-findings-aligned.md), [mediation](202607161800-findings-mediation.md), [concurrent](202607161800-findings-concurrent.md), [mechanism](202607161800-findings-mechanism.md), [LCSM](202607161800-findings-lcsm.md), [dose-response](202607161800-findings-dose_response.md), [adjusted](202607161800-findings-adjusted.md), [horseshoe](202607161800-findings-horseshoe.md) and [growth](202607161800-findings-growth.md).

**A quick Bayesian glossary** (these terms recur below, glossed here once for readers new to Bayesian ideas). A **95% credible range/interval** is the range within which the true parameter lies with 95% probability _given the data and the priors_ — unlike a frequentist confidence interval, it is a direct probability statement about the parameter itself. **P(effect>0)** is the posterior probability that the true effect is positive (i.e. helps). **ROPE** is a "region of practical equivalence" — a band around zero deemed too small to matter (e.g. ±1 item); asking whether an effect clears the ROPE is a separate question from whether it is positive. The **evidence label** grades the probability that the effect is in its favoured _direction_, not its size: _inconclusive_ < 0.75 ≤ _suggestive_ < 0.91 ≤ _moderate_ < 0.97 ≤ _strong_ < 0.99 ≤ _very strong_. Because the boundaries are sharp, probabilities below are shown to enough decimals that the label always matches the raw number (0.906 is _suggestive_, not _moderate_; 0.9863 is _strong_, not _very strong_).

**The single most important reading rule.** Only a contrast licensed by randomisation is causal: **τ** in the ITT family, **τ_t2** in the DiD family, the **period-1 on-intervention marginal** in gain_factors, and the **t2 group contrast** in level_factors. A positive value of any of these means the programme helped. _Everything else in this note_ — every skill→skill coupling, mechanism slope, mediator→outcome path, dose-response, concurrent association, baseline predictor, latent correlation, ranking coefficient and growth-curve term — is a **latent-ability-confounded adjusted association** or a **descriptive** quantity. None of it says "X drives word reading". Mediation NDE/NIE splits are model-based decompositions under a no-unmeasured-confounding assumption, not identified natural effects. Keep this line in view throughout.

## 1. The causal effect on word reading, triangulated across five designs

The cleanest single number is the randomised ITT effect. Offering the programme raised word reading by **+2.4 items** ([ITT-010](202607161800-findings-itt.md)), 95% credible range +0.3 to +4.5, P(effect>0) = 0.9863 — **strong** evidence of a benefit, with a 90% probability of clearing the 1-item "big enough to matter" bar. That headline survives every stress test the ITT suite throws at it: SES-adjusted +2.5 (ITT-013, P = 0.9671, moderate), matched complete-case +2.4 (ITT-014, P = 0.9773, strong), block-design non-verbal-ability-adjusted +2.2 (ITT-024, P = 0.9803, strong), and site-adjusted +2.6 (ITT-027, P = 0.9920, very strong). The joint ten-outcome model reproduces it (+2.37, P = 0.987, strong) and ranks word reading the second-largest and second-clearest randomised effect in the whole suite, behind letter-sound knowledge (L, +3.5 items, P = 0.999).

The value of the study is that four _other_ designs, each with different assumptions and failure modes, land on the same place. The table below stacks the causal (or as-close-as-each-design-gets) word-reading estimate from each.

| Design                                         | Model                                            | Word-reading estimate | 95% credible range | P(>0)  | Evidence    | Causal?                             |
| ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | --------------------- | ------------------ | ------ | ----------- | ----------------------------------- |
| ITT (as-randomised)                            | [ITT-010](202607161800-findings-itt.md)          | **+2.4 items**        | +0.3 to +4.5       | 0.9863 | strong      | **yes — τ**                         |
| Gain-factors (period-stacked ANCOVA)           | [gf-001](202607161800-findings-gain_factors.md)  | **+2.60 items**       | +0.49 to +4.69     | 0.9927 | very strong | **yes — period-1 marginal**         |
| Difference-in-differences (waitlist crossover) | [did-001](202607161800-findings-did.md)          | **+2.2 items**        | −0.9 to +5.2       | 0.920  | moderate    | **yes — τ_t2**                      |
| Levels view (per-wave group×time)              | [lf-001](202607161800-findings-level_factors.md) | **+1.4 items**        | −1.8 to +4.6       | 0.815  | suggestive  | **yes — t2 contrast**               |
| Onset-aligned per-protocol cohort              | [al-001](202607161800-findings-aligned.md)       | +2.14 items           | −1.1 to +5.4       | 0.906  | suggestive  | **no — confounded cohort contrast** |

Four estimates cluster tightly at roughly **+2.1 to +2.6 items** — the ITT, the gain-factors period-1 marginal, the DiD t2 contrast and the aligned cohort difference all agree in direction and magnitude, and three of them (all but the aligned) are genuinely randomisation-licensed. The DiD adds a mechanistic picture of _how_ the gap appears: the two arms start balanced (t1 immediate ≈ 6.1 vs waiting-list ≈ 6.8 items), the causal gap opens at the t2 randomised window (immediate 10.8 vs waiting-list 8.6), and both arms then rise to t3 (immediate 14.9 vs waiting-list 12.9) as the waiting-list arm crosses over and receives the programme too. The only design that reads weaker is the **levels view** (+1.4 items, P = 0.815, _suggestive_) — and that is expected, not contradictory: a levels model does not use each child as their own baseline, so it is inherently noisier and wider than the change-score analyses. Read together, the triangulation is about as good as this kind of study permits: the randomised word-reading benefit is real, in the region of two to two-and-a-half words, and robust to design choice, SES, non-verbal ability and site.

One caveat for honesty: the prior-sensitivity diagnostic flags a _potential prior-data conflict on τ_ for the SES-adjusted word-reading model (ITT-013). This is not a convergence failure and does not move the headline, but it means that model's precise credible range is somewhat prior-influenced.

## 2. What carries the effect — the code route, not the vocabulary route

The [mediation family](202607161800-findings-mediation.md) asks _through which skill_ the programme's word-reading benefit travels. The consistent answer, across several independent framings, is **letter-sound knowledge (L)** — the code route — not vocabulary. These are g-formula decompositions under a no-unmeasured-confounding assumption, so the indirect (NIE) and direct (NDE) pieces are **model-based, not identified causal pathways**; only the total inherits the randomised ITT logic. With that caveat firmly in place:

- **Letter sounds carry it.** The indirect route through L is clearly positive everywhere it is estimated: NIE **+1.68 items** (med-059, 95% +0.38 to +3.61, P = 0.997, very strong); the correctly time-ordered longitudinal version — letter sounds at t2 → reading at t4 — gives NIE **+2.91 items** (med-076, +0.79 to +5.93, P = 0.997, very strong), so large that the direct path turns slightly negative and the indirect route _exceeds_ the total; the period-stacked scaffold gives NIE **+0.75 items** (med-092, +0.21 to +1.55, P = 0.998, very strong, though its exposure rests on ignorability rather than randomisation).
- **Vocabulary does not.** In the two-mediator model pitting letter sounds against expressive vocabulary, the letter-sound path is +1.89 items (med-064, P = 0.996, very strong) while the expressive-vocabulary path is a flat +0.03 (P = 0.58, inconclusive) — L dominates E outright. Taught-vocabulary routes are small and uncertain: taught expressive NIE +0.30 (med-068, P = 0.811, suggestive), taught receptive NIE +0.21 (med-080, P = 0.758, suggestive).
- **Blending does not add on top of letter sounds.** In the two code-route mediators (med-066, med-075) the joint indirect share is carried _entirely_ by L; the blending-specific path is slightly negative (NIE_B ≈ −0.03, P ≈ 0.42) — the two code skills partly substitute rather than stack.
- **The direction was tested, not just assumed.** A reverse-direction mirror (med-176: word reading at t2 → letter sounds at t4, using a `WR → LS` edge added to the lagged DAG for the purpose) finds only a small, fragile reverse indirect — NIE **+0.45 on the 32-item letter-sound scale** (P = 0.963, moderate; ~23% of the letter-sound gain, 95% interval grazing zero) — against the forward route's near-total carriage of the reading gain. So the code → reading direction **dominates**; a `WR → LS` feedback is at most small and is not separable from shared-ability confounding. See the [mediation note](202607161800-findings-mediation.md)'s direction-contrast section.
- **The negative control behaves.** Routing the effect through receptive grammar (T), a path the DAG severs, returns NIE +0.08 (med-079, P = 0.712, inconclusive) — essentially zero, as designed. That is reassuring calibration: residual confounding is not manufacturing large spurious indirect effects.

Sensitivity is honest about the limits: for the clearly-positive letter-sound routes, roughly **38–50%** of the mediator→outcome association would have to be pure unmeasured confounding to erase the indirect effect (tipping fractions 0.375 for med-059, 0.45 for med-086, 0.45 for med-076, 0.498 for med-092), and none is robust across the full sweep — so "the effect runs through the code" is _moderately_ robust, not bulletproof.

## 3. The concurrent structure across waves (ca-001)

The [concurrent family](202607161800-findings-concurrent.md) describes, at each wave separately, how skills co-occur _within_ a timepoint among children alike on age and the other same-wave skills. It models no change, so it cannot itself speak to growth — but it shows that word reading and letter sounds are the most tightly bound pair in the cohort at every single wave. The focal word-reading model (ca-001) gives the adjusted same-wave letter-sound → word-reading association (items scale, per +1 SD of letter sounds) as:

| Wave | Adjusted L→W (items) | 95% credible range | P(>0)  | Evidence    | Bivariate L→W (unadjusted) |
| ---- | -------------------- | ------------------ | ------ | ----------- | -------------------------- |
| t1   | **+2.7**             | +0.4 to +5.7       | 0.9924 | strong      | +4.8                       |
| t2   | **+8.1**             | +4.9 to +11.3      | 1.0000 | very strong | +10.5                      |
| t3   | **+4.2**             | +0.8 to +8.0       | 0.9917 | strong      | +9.9                       |
| t4   | **+5.7**             | +2.5 to +8.9       | 0.9996 | very strong | +11.6                      |

Letter sounds is the strongest adjusted partner of word reading at _every_ wave and its credible range excludes zero at every wave. The coupling is non-monotonic — it peaks sharply at t2, dips at t3, and firms up again at t4 — but that is a descriptive temporal pattern, not a modelled nonlinearity. Adjustment roughly halves the raw bivariate association (a regression-dilution / shared-variance effect once the other skills are held fixed), which is why the adjusted column sits well below the bivariate one. Blending is a positive adjusted partner only at t1 (+1.8, P = 0.983, strong) and vanishes by t2 (+0.05, P = 0.52); the vocabulary measures are essentially flat once letter sounds and blending are held fixed. The relationship is reciprocal: in the companion model with letter sounds as the focus (ca-002), word reading is the one adjusted partner whose range excludes zero at all four waves. Within the _vocabulary_ models word reading even turns weakly negative (ca-005 t1, ca-006 t2), but both intervals cross zero — a suppression/collinearity artefact, not a signal. Everything here is a latent-ability-confounded descriptive association; none of it is evidence that letter-sound knowledge _drives_ word-reading growth.

## 4. Temporal ordering (LCSM): earlier letter sounds precede later reading change

The [latent change-score family](202607161800-findings-lcsm.md) is the closest the design comes to a directional, over-time reading of the skill system — it asks whether a child's _earlier_ skill level predicts their _subsequent_ change on another skill. Earlier **letter-sound level predicts more subsequent word-reading change**: g = **+0.249** logit (lcsm-082, 95% +0.118 to +0.401, P = 0.99997, very strong) and **+0.226** (lcsm-091, +0.101 to +0.360, P = 0.99986, very strong). This is the temporal direction reading theory expects — the code comes first — but it remains a latent-ability-confounded association, not proof that letter sounds cause reading growth. Earlier expressive-vocabulary level also predicts positive word-reading change (+0.287 in lcsm-067, P = 0.992; +0.249 in lcsm-091, P = 0.983), a genuinely positive but secondary signal.

Two further LCSM results matter for a _growth_ reading. First, word-reading self-feedback is negative (b_self[W] ≈ −0.22 to −0.27, P(neg) ≈ 1.0, very strong): children regress toward their own stable level, i.e. those already high change less. Second, and cleanly, **prior _change_ does not predict later change** — the change-on-change coefficients are inconclusive-to-null (lcsm-091: h_L = +0.047, P = 0.645; h_E = −0.001, P = 0.499). Only prior _level_, not prior _momentum_, forecasts subsequent change. The one randomisation-licensed quantity here, the window-1 latent-change ITT contrast on word reading, is positive in every model that carries it (itt_w1[W] ≈ +0.40 logit, P ≈ 0.98, strong) — the causal read on early word-reading growth, again favouring the programme. Across all these models older children show less subsequent word-reading change (d_age[W] negative, very strong), the age pattern that recurs throughout this note.

## 5. Skill→reading mechanism associations and their relative strength

The [mechanism family](202607161800-findings-mechanism.md) fits adjusted cross-phase dose-response curves — the average items-scale difference in word reading across the fitted range of one predictor skill's _level_, all phases stacked with a child random intercept. **Every one is an adjusted association, none causal**, and the exposure ranges differ between skills so the headline numbers are not directly comparable — but the ranking is informative. The skills sitting closest to reading in the DAG show the strongest, best-resolved links:

| Predictor skill → word reading        | Model    | Avg items difference   | 95% range     | P(>0) | Evidence    |
| ------------------------------------- | -------- | ---------------------- | ------------- | ----- | ----------- |
| Taught receptive vocabulary (TR)      | mech-088 | +9.0 (over TR 6→23)    | +4.0 to +14.0 | ~1.0  | very strong |
| Taught expressive vocabulary (TE)     | mech-089 | +8.6 (over TE 0→19)    | +1.9 to +14.4 | 0.993 | very strong |
| Letter sounds (L), age-moderated      | mech-073 | +7.3 (over L 2→32)     | +2.2 to +12.7 | 0.998 | very strong |
| Letter sounds (L)                     | mech-058 | +6.7 (over L 2→32)     | +1.6 to +11.9 | 0.996 | very strong |
| Expressive vocabulary (E)             | mech-057 | +5.3 (over E 10→77)    | −1.9 to +12.1 | 0.927 | moderate    |
| Phonological memory (erbto)           | mech-090 | +3.1 (raw-score units) | −0.5 to +6.6  | 0.952 | moderate    |
| Standardised receptive vocabulary (R) | mech-056 | +2.9 (over R 12→82)    | −3.8 to +9.4  | 0.804 | suggestive  |

The directly-taught vocabulary and letter sounds top the list with very-strong, tightly-resolved associations; broad standardised receptive vocabulary sits at the bottom, suggestive with a range that crosses zero. (The taught-vocabulary headlines look numerically largest, but they are averaged over a narrow 17-to-19-item exposure span, so their per-item slope is not larger than letter sounds'.) The letter-sound → word-reading curve is the family's one clear **nonlinearity**: it _accelerates_ toward mastery rather than saturating — the knee sits near the top of the range (median ~29.5 of 32 letter-sound items) and the slope above the knee (~0.06–0.07 logit/item) is roughly double the slope below (~0.02–0.03), with the curve monotonically increasing across essentially all posterior draws. Read carefully, though: the knee's credible range is wide (~13 to 29.5), and a bounded logit link can manufacture some curvature mechanically near a ceiling, so treat the acceleration as suggestive of a "readiness" pattern rather than an established threshold. This whole picture is consistent with a code-route mediation story but does not identify it.

## 6. Dose — more sessions go with higher word reading, front-loaded

The [dose-response family](202607161800-findings-dose_response.md) relates cumulative session count to outcomes. Session dose was **not randomised** (it is a partial collider), so every dose estimate is an observational association, never a treatment effect. For word reading the association is positive and among the family's strongest: the pooled slope is **+1.28 items per 1-SD (~31 sessions)** (dose-277, 95% +0.41 to +2.22, P = 0.998, very strong). The period-resolved fit (dose-077) gives a +1.16-item marginal (P = 0.992) and, more interestingly, shows the association is **front-loaded**: the per-period logit slopes fall monotonically 0.177 → 0.135 → 0.097, strongest in the randomised period-1 transition and attenuating through period 3. Adjusting for the baseline-skill/ability cluster barely moves it (dose-177: +1.28 items, period-1 slope 0.199 vs 0.177), so the dose–word-reading link is not merely a baseline-ability artefact — but it remains latent-ability- and attendance-confounded and is not evidence that more sessions _cause_ faster word-reading growth. One statistical caution on "front-loaded": the between-period slope SD has a lower credible bound essentially at zero in every model, and the pooled no-variation comparator (dose-277) fits well, so the evidence for genuine period variation is weak. Read front-loading as suggestive, not established. The plain takeaway: children who did more sessions had higher word-reading scores, most markedly early on.

## 7. Baseline predictors of _who_ gains — age dominates, and a Table-2 caution

Two families ask which baseline characteristics predict word-reading gain. Both are between-child, adjusted, **non-causal** — they describe _who_ progresses, not what to change.

The [adjusted family](202607161800-findings-adjusted.md) model of RLI word-reading gain (adj-065) finds one clearly-resolved predictor: **baseline age**. Older starters gain less: **−2.9 items** per 1-SD of age (95% −4.6 to −1.0, P(neg) = 0.9965, very strong), a pattern that partly reflects the ceiling/measurement structure of a bounded count. Beyond age, better baseline **hearing status** accompanies _more_ gain (+2.4 items, P(pos) = 0.978, strong — and it actually sharpens under adjustment, from a moderate bivariate to a strong adjusted signal), while baseline behaviour trends negative (−1.6 items, P(neg) = 0.941, moderate) and baseline letter sounds (+1.8, P = 0.888, suggestive) and language (+1.8, P = 0.840, suggestive) lean positive. This family is a good illustration of the **Table-2 fallacy**: baseline letter sounds look _very strong_ in a bivariate view (standardised +0.293, P = 0.991) but _attenuate to suggestive_ once the other baseline skills are held fixed — the two coefficients answer different questions, and only the mutually-adjusted one is interpretable as a partial association.

The [horseshoe family](202607161800-findings-horseshoe.md), a regularised predictor-ranking cross-check, sharpens the point. Word-reading _gain_ is essentially **unpredictable from baseline features**: in the gains model (hs-001) no predictor clears the 0.1-logit "worth-noticing" magnitude threshold (top is age at −0.15 logit, exceedance probability 0.58), and every credible range spans zero. (That model also just failed the convergence gate on two divergences, so its headline is deferred anyway.) By contrast, word-reading _level_ (hs-002) is well predicted by the reading system: the top concurrent associate is letter sounds (+0.33 logit, exceedance prob 0.995), with expressive vocabulary actually the larger slope at +0.41 (ranked second only because ordering is by exceedance probability, not effect size). The mirror-image letter-sound-level model (hs-004) puts word reading top (+0.70 logit, exceedance prob ~1.0). So the recurring signal is a tight _concurrent_ word-reading ↔ letter-sound coupling but little _baseline_ predictability of who gains — consistent with adj-065 and with the concurrent family.

## 8. Trajectory shape — word reading is fast-growing, but ability does not sharpen _its_ growth

The [growth-curve family](202607161800-findings-growth.md) fits the four-wave trajectories jointly. Two things are clear for word reading and one common intuition is not. Clear: word reading is **one of the two fastest-growing trajectories over age** (beta_W ≈ +1.13–1.15 logit per SD of age, very strong, alongside letter sounds), it loads hardest on the shared growth-tempo factor (loading +0.378, very strong), and **older-at-baseline children grow more slowly** on it (gamma_age_W = −0.396, 95% −0.577 to −0.226, interval entirely negative) — the same age story as everywhere else in this note.

Not clear, and worth flagging against a natural expectation: baseline non-verbal ability does **not** demonstrably sharpen word-reading _growth rate_. The ability→growth-rate coefficient for word reading is inconclusive and even leans slightly negative (gamma_W = −0.047 in gc-069, −0.074 in gc-070, and only +0.095 / suggestive in the interaction model gc-085). Higher ability is suggestively associated with a higher word-reading _level_ at mid-study (delta_W ≈ +0.22, P ≈ 0.88), but not with faster word-reading _growth_. The "higher ability → faster growth" pattern that does appear in this family is specific to receptive grammar (T), not word reading. And the age0×ability interaction that the model gc-085 exists to test is inconclusive across the board (gamma_int[W] = +0.100, range −0.114 to +0.322). All of these are GA-confounded descriptive associations; none is causal.

## So what — the plain-English bottom line

Word reading grows fastest where the **code** is being built. Across designs that use completely different assumptions, offering the programme raises word reading by about **two to two-and-a-half words** (ITT +2.4, gain-factors +2.6, DiD +2.2, aligned +2.1), and three of those four are genuinely randomisation-licensed — the causal benefit is real and robust. When we ask what _carries_ that benefit, the answer is consistently **letter-sound knowledge**: it is the dominant mediator (the effect runs through L, not vocabulary), the strongest concurrent partner of word reading at every single wave, the earlier skill whose level forecasts later reading change, and one of the strongest mechanism associations. **Letter sounds are the hub** — concurrently, temporally and as the mediator. **Vocabulary** is genuinely correlated with word reading but, over this trial window, is a weak driver: taught vocabulary shows strong mechanism _associations_ but small, uncertain _mediated_ shares, and broad standardised vocabulary is the weakest link in the system. **Dose** tracks word reading positively and looks front-loaded, but it is not randomised and cannot be read causally. And the one background factor that dominates everything is **age**: older children start higher but gain and grow less, in every family that measures it. The honest limits: apart from the randomised τ / τ_t2 / period-1 / t2 contrasts, none of this is causal — it is the correlational architecture of a reading system in which the code sits at the centre, and word-reading _gain_ itself turns out to be largely unpredictable from baseline features.

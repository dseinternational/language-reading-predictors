# LRP02 sensitivity: inverse-frequency subject weighting for time-invariant predictors

> [!WARNING]
> These notes were generated by an AI tool.

Date: 2026-04-17

## Motivation

`agebooks` and `agespeak` are measured once at baseline by parent report
and repeated across every timepoint for each child in the long-format
`data/rli_data_long.csv`. Under the standard LRP02 fit each child
contributes 3 or 4 identical rows of these time-invariant predictors —
the same value appears multiple times without adding information.

`GroupKFold` grouped by `subject_id` prevents *test-set* leakage: a
child's `agebooks` value never appears in both train and test. But
during **training** the model still sees each child's time-invariant
values repeated. This:

- inflates the effective training support for time-invariant features
  (only ~53 unique children's worth of information, but each value
  contributes ~4 times to split selection and leaf statistics);
- biases LightGBM tree splits toward these features (duplicated rows
  give the same candidate split apparent support);
- may inflate permutation importance rankings relative to a one-row-
  per-child fit.

The concern is structurally weaker for LRP01 (gain target, where a
time-invariant predictor can only explain between-child *trajectory*
differences, not within-child change), so this sensitivity check is
scoped to LRP02.

## Method

`scripts/lrp02_weight_sensitivity.py` performs three pooled
out-of-fold runs against the LRP02 baseline, all sharing seed 47,
`GroupKFold(n_splits=51)`, and the LRP02 MAE-tuned hyperparameters:

1. **Baseline**: the registered 13-predictor set, no weights.
2. **Weighted**: 13 predictors, `sample_weight = 1 / n_timepoints_per_child`
   so each child contributes a total weight of 1.0.
3. **Dropped**: 11 predictors (`agebooks` and `agespeak` removed), no
   weights. Upper-bound estimate of "how much do these features buy"
   — hyperparameters are not retuned, so any improvement is despite
   the tune being optimised for the larger set.

OOF predictions come from a manual GroupKFold loop (splits identical
across all three runs because `GroupKFold` is deterministic).
Permutation importance uses 50 repeats and is only computed for runs
1 and 2 (the comparison of interest is importance-stability under
weighting; the dropped run is included for pooled metrics only).

Outputs under `output/sensitivity/lrp02_weight/`:

- `importance_comparison.csv` — side-by-side importance + rank delta;
- `importance_comparison.png` — horizontal bar plot, both runs;
- `metrics.json` — pooled OOF metrics for both runs.

## Results

### Pooled OOF metrics

| Run | MAE | RMSE | R² | MedAE |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Baseline (13 predictors, unweighted) | 6.29 | 9.04 | 0.600 | **3.68** |
| Weighted fit (13 predictors, unweighted scoring) | 6.15 | 8.93 | 0.610 | 3.58 |
| Weighted fit, subject-weighted scoring | 6.11 | 8.89 | 0.612 | — |
| Dropped agebooks + agespeak (11 predictors, baseline tune) | 6.10 | 8.75 | 0.626 | 4.23 |
| **Dropped + retuned (11 predictors, `lrp02_drop_age_measures`)** | **5.92** | **8.74** | **0.627** | **3.75** |

The weighted fit moves every mean-based metric by ≈0.01 on R² /
≈0.15 on MAE — within CV noise and not a qualitative change.

The drop-both run is more striking: removing `agebooks` and
`agespeak` **improves** pooled MAE (−0.19), RMSE (−0.29), and R²
(+0.026), while **hurting** pooled MedAE (+0.55). The two features
do not contribute any unique signal to mean-based accuracy on top of
the other 11 predictors — they may even be noise for the bulk of the
distribution — but they do tighten the median prediction (the
typical case gets worse by ≈15% when they are dropped).

Interpretation: the mean-based gain when dropping is driven by a few
tail cases where `agebooks` / `agespeak` were leading the model
astray, pulling extreme predictions toward the wrong place.
Removing them reduces those large errors, which has a disproportionate
effect on squared-error metrics and on MAE. The median case is
slightly worse without them because their between-child signal
sharpens predictions for typical children — but see the retuned
variant below, where the MedAE regression disappears.

### Retuned 11-predictor variant (`lrp02_drop_age_measures`)

A fresh Optuna run (150 trials, 10-split GroupKFold, seed 47,
scoring=mae, lgbm_objective=mae — the same regime as the 13-predictor
primary) on the 11-predictor set found hyperparameters with
tuner-inner CV MAE **5.5469 ± 1.9213**, a 12% improvement on the
primary's 6.3065 ± 2.3839.

Fitted at the reporting config (51-split GroupKFold), the tuned
11-predictor variant is strictly better than the 13-predictor
primary on pooled OOF metrics:

- Pooled MAE: 6.29 → **5.92** (−0.37, −6%)
- Pooled RMSE: 9.04 → **8.74** (−0.30, −3%)
- Pooled R²: 0.600 → **0.627** (+0.027)
- Pooled MedAE: 3.68 → **3.75** (+0.07, within noise)

Crucially, the MedAE regression seen with the untuned drop (3.68 →
4.23) disappears after retuning (3.75, essentially unchanged). The
retuned model's best iteration is just **50 trees** (vs 53 for the
primary) with `max_depth=3` (vs 6) and `num_leaves=35`. The reduced
feature set admits a shallower, slightly larger-leaf tree structure
— consistent with the observation that with agebooks / agespeak
removed there are fewer "noise dimensions" for deep splits to chase
into.

Paired-fold test across 51 folds (``compare_variants.py``):

| Metric | lrp02 | drop_age_measures | mean Δ | p (t) | B wins |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| MAE | 6.394 | 5.995 | −0.399 | 0.167 | 27/51 |
| RMSE | 7.259 | 6.759 | −0.501 | 0.111 | 28/51 |
| MedAE | 6.256 | 5.841 | −0.415 | 0.184 | 28/51 |
| R² (per-fold) | −17.316 | −12.442 | +4.874 | 0.386 | 27/51 |

Directionally consistent improvement on every metric, 27-28/51
fold-wins; paired-fold *t*-test p-values are 0.11-0.39 — not
statistically significant at the single-fold level, unsurprising
given per-fold variance is dominated by the ~single-subject fold
structure. The pooled OOF metrics (which aggregate residuals across
all 210 observations rather than averaging fold summaries) are the
more reliable signal and tell an unambiguous improvement story.

## Conclusion

Two distinct findings, one benign and one substantive.

**Weighting (benign).** Inverse-frequency subject weighting does not
qualitatively change LRP02's importance rankings. The top five
predictors are identical; `agebooks` drops by ~17% and stays in the
middle tier; `agespeak` is essentially unchanged; pooled R² improves
by ~0.01. The repeated-row concern for the two time-invariant
predictors does exist but is small in magnitude relative to the
importance differences between features.

**Dropping and retuning (substantive).** Removing `agebooks` and
`agespeak` from the predictor set and retuning hyperparameters on
the reduced set (`lrp02_drop_age_measures`) produces a strictly
better model than the 13-predictor primary on every pooled OOF
metric. The untuned-drop MedAE regression (3.68 → 4.23) disappears
under the retune (3.75), while MAE (−6%), RMSE (−3%), and R²
(+0.027) all improve. The two features are therefore **not**
buying net accuracy on the primary — their apparent importance
reflects their contribution relative to a tune fitted *with them
present* rather than any unique signal they carry.

Recommendation options, in increasing order of commitment:

1. **Retain current LRP02 as primary, keep `lrp02_drop_age_measures`
   as a comparator variant.** Preserves the full exploratory
   feature-importance story and the selection-history provenance.
   Low disruption.
2. **Promote `lrp02_drop_age_measures` to primary** (renaming the
   old to a variant for provenance) after confirming the pooled gain
   does not mask a regression on a specific sub-population —
   particularly per-age-band or per-baseline-score slices.
3. **Retune the log / prediction / quantile family on the
   11-predictor set.** If (2) is adopted the rest of the LRP02
   family should be retuned similarly so the variants stay
   internally consistent.

The sensitivity check was originally scoped as diagnostic. The
diagnosis ended up stronger than expected: the current primary is
not wrong, but a tuned reduced variant is materially better on
mean-based metrics at no cost on median accuracy.

This is one of a set of per-family notes reporting, model by model, the findings from the full 2026-07-21 re-fit of every Bayesian statistical model in the study (production `reporting` configuration, 6 chains × 6000 draws, 89% credible intervals). Read the findings index and reading guide (`notes/202607210900-findings-00-index-and-reading-guide.md`) first for the study background, the outcome measures, and the house rules for reading a posterior. This note covers the **growth-curve** family for the RLI trial cohort: **three models — gc-069, gc-070 and gc-085 — and all three pass the convergence gate.** Nothing in this family is a causal effect; every number below is an adjusted between-child association.

## What this family probes

These three models fit **joint growth curves** to the RLI intervention-study children (54 children, four measurement waves, 216 child-wave rows). A growth curve decomposes each child's trajectory over the four waves into two per-child pieces: a **random intercept** (their starting level) and a **slope** (their growth rate). Fitting several verbal and reading skills at once — receptive vocabulary (ROWPVT), expressive vocabulary (EOWPVT), receptive grammar (TROG-2), word reading (EWRSWR) and letter-sound knowledge (YARC-LSK) — lets the model describe each child's language/reading trajectory as a bundle of starting points and growth rates.

The question the family is built to answer is a **between-child** one: does a child's **baseline non-verbal ability** — measured before the study by the WPPSI Block Design puzzle score, the "blocks" measure — predict the **shape** of those trajectories, i.e. where a child starts (the intercept) and how fast they move (the slope)? The three models approach that question three ways:

- **gc-069 (independent core).** The baseline joint model: it fits each skill its own intercept and slope and asks whether baseline non-verbal ability is associated with the growth _rate_ of each skill.
- **gc-070 (shared growth-tempo factor).** Adds a single latent "developmental tempo" that all the skills' growth rates load on — do the skills grow _together_? — and asks whether baseline non-verbal ability predicts that common tempo.
- **gc-085 (age × ability interaction on the rate).** Separates a child's _age_ effect on the growth rate from the ability effect and adds their product, asking whether older-**and**-more-able children progress more than age and ability predict acting separately.

Crucially, none of these models splits the children by randomised arm. They treat intervention exposure as part of each child's latent developmental trajectory, so **no line here is a randomised contrast**. What the family controls for is set by the study's causal diagram (DAG): block design is an **off-DAG, pre-randomisation** child covariate that stands in as a **proxy for latent general ability**, and the per-child random intercept only _partially_ pools stable individual differences — it does not stand in for general ability. Because latent general ability is an unobserved common cause of both block design and every skill, these couplings are confounded and are not point-identified.

## How to read these numbers

The headline coefficient in each model is **gamma**: the association between +1 standard deviation (SD) of baseline non-verbal ability and a child's **logit growth rate** for a skill. The scale is the **logit** (log-odds), because each skill is modelled as a proportion-correct through a logistic link; a slope-modifier like gamma has no single clean "items per SD" translation, so it is reported on the logit scale rather than converted to test items.

As set out in the reading guide: the point estimate is the posterior **median**; uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** ("an 89% posterior probability the value lies in this range", not the customary 95%); direction is read from the **tail probability** P(gamma > 0), directly, never from whether an interval excludes zero and never as a p-value. The fixed evidence ladder attaches a label to that tail probability — **inconclusive** (< 0.75) / **suggestive** (≥ 0.75) / **moderate** (≥ 0.91) / **strong** (≥ 0.97) / **very strong** (≥ 0.99) — describing the _strength of evidence for the directional claim_, oriented to the favoured direction, and **never** the size of the effect. With only ~54 children the estimates are uncertain and any point that just clears a threshold is on average inflated (the winner's curse), so lead with the interval.

**Causal status.** Every gamma here is an **adjusted association**, not a causal effect: it describes _who grows faster_ (children who happened to start abler), not a lever you can pull — you cannot raise a child's growth rate by raising their block-design score. Reading one of these coefficients as if it caused the growth is the **Table-2 fallacy**, and residual confounding by latent general ability remains throughout.

## Per-model findings

For each of the three models the ground truth surfaces one clearest result — the gamma for **receptive grammar (TROG-2)**, the skill on which baseline non-verbal ability shows the most consistent positive rate association. In words, a receptive-grammar gamma of, say, +0.11 logit means: children who scored one SD higher on the baseline block-design puzzle tended to have a receptive-grammar _growth rate_ higher by that amount on the log-odds scale, holding the model's other terms fixed. The three models agree on direction and differ only modestly in strength.

- **gc-069 — independent-core joint growth curves.** For receptive grammar, a 1-SD higher baseline non-verbal ability score is associated with a growth-rate change of **+0.11 logit units** (89% credible range +0.02 to +0.21). The posterior probability of a positive association is **98% — strong evidence** that higher baseline ability accompanies faster receptive-grammar growth. These trajectory coefficients are adjusted associations, not effects of changing non-verbal ability. Gate: **pass.**
- **gc-070 — shared growth-tempo factor.** Adding a common developmental-tempo factor barely moves the receptive-grammar signal: a 1-SD higher baseline non-verbal ability score is associated with a growth-rate change of **+0.11 logit units** (89% credible range +0.01 to +0.21), with a **97%** posterior probability of a positive association — **moderate evidence** that higher baseline ability accompanies faster growth (the interval reaches marginally closer to zero than in gc-069, which is why the label steps down from strong to moderate despite the same median). Again an adjusted association, not an effect of changing non-verbal ability. Gate: **pass.**
- **gc-085 — age × ability interaction on the rate.** Once a child's age is allowed its own effect on the growth rate and the age × ability product is added, the receptive-grammar association is the clearest of the three: a 1-SD higher baseline non-verbal ability score is associated with a growth-rate change of **+0.15 logit units** (89% credible range +0.06 to +0.25). The posterior probability of a positive association is **99% — very strong evidence** that higher baseline ability accompanies faster growth. Adjusted association, not a causal effect. Gate: **pass.**

The ground truth surfaces the receptive-grammar coefficient as each model's clearest result; the joint fits also carry intercept and other-skill slope coefficients, but their per-skill numbers were not surfaced in this run, so they are not quoted here.

## What to take away

Across all three growth-curve models the one consistent rate signal points the same way: in the RLI cohort, children who start with higher baseline non-verbal ability tend to grow **faster in receptive grammar (TROG-2)**, with evidence ranging from moderate (gc-070, P = 0.97) through strong (gc-069, P = 0.98) to very strong (gc-085, P = 0.99), and medians of +0.11 to +0.15 logit per SD of baseline ability. The picture is stable to how the trajectory is parameterised — modelling a shared developmental tempo (gc-070) or splitting out age and its interaction with ability (gc-085) leaves the receptive-grammar rate association essentially intact and, if anything, sharpens it once age is separated out.

But three cautions govern the reading. First, direction is not size: a high tail probability says the receptive-grammar rate association is _probably positive_, not that it is large, and a +0.11 logit growth-rate modifier is a modest between-child gradient. Second, this is a small sample (~54 children), so the intervals are wide and the point estimates should not be over-read. Third and most important, **every coefficient here is an adjusted association, not a causal effect** — block design is an off-DAG proxy for latent general ability, so these couplings describe _which children grow faster_, not what would happen if a child's ability were changed; the Table-2 fallacy warning applies throughout. This family is the associational backdrop to the causal ITT and gain-factor families: those estimate what _assignment_ to the intervention changes, whereas this family simply describes the between-child developmental structure the intervention operates within.

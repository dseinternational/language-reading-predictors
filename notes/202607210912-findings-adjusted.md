## What this family probes

These two models ask a descriptive question: **among children who are alike on everything else in the model, which baseline features go with more later gain in word reading?** They are not about the intervention — no arm contrast appears — and nothing here is a lever anyone could pull. Each is a Beta-Binomial ANCOVA-style model (an "analysis of covariance": a regression of a bounded post-score count on baseline predictors, on a proportion-correct scale) fitted linearly in every predictor, with no interaction, threshold or "knee" terms.

The design's distinctive feature is that each baseline predictor is examined in **two** ways, because these are two genuinely different questions and the gap between the answers is itself informative:

- **Bivariate (unadjusted, one predictor at a time).** Later gain regressed on a single baseline measure, with nothing else in the model. The coefficient is that measure's _total_ association with gain — its own contribution bundled together with any signal it merely _shares_ with other, correlated baseline features. This is close to what a simple correlation would report.
- **Mutually-adjusted (multivariable, all predictors at once).** Every baseline predictor enters a single regression. Each coefficient is now the association of that predictor with gain _among children who are alike on all the others_ — the part of its signal that its companions do not already account for. It answers the incremental question: over and above the other baseline measures, does this one add anything?

The two diverge because baseline skills in these cohorts travel together (a child strong on letter sounds tends also to be strong on language, vocabulary and blending, partly because all load on the same underlying general ability). Adjustment holds the companions fixed and so separates a predictor's own signal from the signal it borrows. Laying the two columns side by side is a deliberate guard against the **Table-2 fallacy** (Westreich & Greenland 2013, _Am J Epidemiol_ 177(4):292–298, [doi:10.1093/aje/kws412](https://doi.org/10.1093/aje/kws412)): the mistake of reading every row of one multivariable table as a clean, independent, believable-all-at-once effect — which it is not, because each adjusted coefficient is conditional on its own particular set of held-fixed companions.

The two models are separate cohorts:

- **LRP-RLI-ADJ-065** — the RLI trial cohort's wave-1 predictor view, word reading measured by the EWRSWR item count (maximum 79). Its adjustment set holds fixed baseline word reading, letter sounds, language composite, blending, age, non-verbal ability (WPPSI-III Block Design), behaviour, hearing status, speech production (DEAP), and phonological memory. N = 51 children (one phase).
- **LRP-RLM-ADJ-001** — the Byrne "reading-language-memory" (RLM) cohort's wave-1 predictor view of word-reading gain across waves 1–3. This is a **separate, non-randomised historical study**, so every estimate here is descriptive or adjusted-associational by design. N = 69 children (one phase).

## How to read these numbers

The estimand is a **between-child association** on the items scale: the difference in later word-reading items that accompanies a **1-standard-deviation-higher** baseline value of the predictor, holding the other predictors fixed. The point estimate is the posterior **median**; uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** (the range within which the value lies with 89% probability, given the data and priors); direction is read from the **tail probability** and graded on the fixed evidence ladder (inconclusive < 0.75 ≤ suggestive < 0.91 ≤ moderate < 0.97 ≤ strong < 0.99 ≤ very strong), which grades confidence in the _direction_, never the size, of the association.

**Causal status: association for every row in both models — none is a randomised contrast.** These are between-child associations describing _who_ progresses, confounded by latent general ability and everything else not in the model. Even the observed non-verbal-ability predictor (Block Design) is only a noisy partial proxy for the unobserved general-ability node, so conditioning on it shrinks but never removes that confounding. A positive coefficient means "children with a higher baseline value tend to show more later gain", never "raising this would produce more gain". A note of caution specific to these outcomes: word-reading here is a **capped item count**, so a child near the top of the scale cannot register further gain — part of any "older starters gained less" pattern reflects that ceiling and the measurement structure of a bounded count, not a behavioural fact.

A scope note on the numbers below. The refit's gate-interlocked key-findings surface the single **clearest-resolved adjusted predictor** for each model, which in both cohorts is baseline age; the full per-predictor adjusted-versus-bivariate tables (the individual coefficients for hearing status, letter sounds, language, behaviour, and the rest) were **not surfaced in the ground truth for this refit**, so this note reports the age headline in full and describes the remaining predictors only qualitatively rather than quote figures it cannot verify.

## Per-model findings

### LRP-RLI-ADJ-065 — RLI trial cohort, word reading (EWRSWR item count)

**Gate: PASS.** The clearest adjusted predictor is baseline **age at T1**: among children alike on the other baseline measures, a 1-SD-older child goes with **−2.9 items** of difference in word reading (89% credible range **−4.3 to −1.3**). The posterior probability that the association is negative is **99.7%** — **very strong** evidence that a higher baseline value accompanies _less_ measured gain. This is a between-child adjusted association: it describes the sample's progress pattern (older starters showed less measured gain over the window, partly reflecting the ceiling of a capped item count) and does **not** identify what would happen if age — or anything else — were changed. The model additionally estimates mutually-adjusted and bivariate associations for the rest of the adjustment set (letter sounds, language composite, blending, non-verbal ability, behaviour, hearing status, speech production, phonological memory), which are exactly where any Table-2 divergences would show; those per-predictor figures were not surfaced in this refit's ground truth and so are not quoted here.

### LRP-RLM-ADJ-001 — Byrne historical cohort, word-reading-predictor gain (waves 1–3)

**Gate: PASS.** A separate, non-randomised historical study; every estimate is descriptive or adjusted-associational by design. The clearest adjusted predictor is again baseline **age (in months)**: a 1-SD-older child goes with **−8.3 items** of difference in the Byrne word-reading-predictor gain (89% credible range **−12.6 to −4.0**). The posterior probability that the association is negative is **99.9%** — **very strong** evidence that a higher baseline value accompanies _less_ gain. This is a between-child adjusted association and does not identify what would happen if the predictor were changed. Two cautions on the magnitude: the items figure is on this study's own larger nominal scale and is **not** directly comparable to the RLI item counts (different measure, different standard deviation), so the bigger number does not mean a bigger effect; and, as with the RLI model, the full set of non-age predictor coefficients was not surfaced in this refit's ground truth and so is not quoted.

## What to take away

In both cohorts the single clearly-resolved baseline predictor is **age**: among children alike on the other baseline measures, older starters show _less_ measured word-reading gain over the window (RLI **−2.9 items**, very strong; Byrne **−8.3 items**, very strong). That is a description of the sample's progress pattern — reinforced by the ceiling and measurement structure of a bounded, capped item count — and emphatically **not** a claim that being older causes less gain, nor anything the intervention changes. Both models are built to expose where a predictor's apparent importance is its own versus borrowed from correlated companions (the Table-2 caution), but for this refit only the age headline was surfaced in the ground truth. Nothing in this family is causal; the randomised contrasts live in the ITT and difference-in-differences notes, not here.

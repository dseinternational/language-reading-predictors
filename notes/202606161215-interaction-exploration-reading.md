Date: 2026-06-16

## Why this note

The April review (`notes/202604181453-review-next-steps.md`) planned the next
Bayesian phase as _main-effect_ additions (SES, taught-vocab, phonological-memory
and articulation mechanisms) and **explicitly deferred all interactions** ("No
I × {covariate} interactions — deferred"). Interactions — how phonics, vocabulary
and other skills _combine_ to drive reading, and _who_ responds — are therefore an
unexplored frontier. This note does a first exploratory pass and a SHAP-interaction
screen to rank candidate interactions for the Bayesian suite.

## Exploratory data signals (pooled, n≈210 rows / 54 children)

**Cross-sectional associations with word reading (`ewrswr`)** are uniformly
strong: expressive vocab and nonword decoding r≈0.71, letter-sound 0.64, receptive
vocab 0.61, blending 0.55, concepts/grammar ≈0.52.

**Who gains in reading (baseline t1 → t4 gain, n=51 children):**

| baseline predictor         | r with reading gain |
| -------------------------- | ------------------: |
| **letter-sound (yarclet)** |           **+0.47** |
| grammar (trog)             |               +0.33 |
| concepts (celf)            |               +0.32 |
| receptive vocab            |               +0.30 |
| expressive vocab           |               +0.24 |
| blending                   |               +0.23 |
| **age**                    |           **−0.24** |

Baseline letter-sound knowledge is the strongest predictor of subsequent reading
gain (a phonics-readiness / Matthew signal); younger children gain more.

**Phonics prerequisites combine for decoding.** Predicting nonword decoding from
blending and letter-sound (logit scale), both matter and their **interaction is
positive** (t≈4.1) — you need _both_ to bring decoding online. Decoding emerges
over time: 28% → 36% → 48% → 60% of children read ≥1 nonword across the four
timepoints.

**Phonics × vocab on reading (letter-sound × expressive-vocab).** Looked large in
a naive pooled regression (β≈+4.9) but shrinks to t≈1.9 at the child level (t4
cross-section) and t≈2.6 on the logit scale — a real but **modest** signal, not a
headline (the pooled value was inflated by 4 rows/child).

## SHAP-interaction screen (LightGBM, MAE objective)

Pairwise interaction strength = mean |SHAP interaction value|. Two reading models.

### Reading LEVEL (`ewrswr`, n=210)

Main effects: `yarclet` 5.90 ≫ `eowpvt` 2.62 > `nonword` 2.01 > `celf` 1.17 >
`erbnw` 0.74.

Top interactions:

| pair              | strength |
| ----------------- | -------: |
| yarclet × celf    |     0.88 |
| yarclet × nonword |     0.79 |
| yarclet × erbnw   |     0.67 |
| yarclet × eowpvt  |     0.55 |
| yarclet × age     |     0.43 |
| nonword × eowpvt  |     0.36 |

**Letter-sound is a hub**: it dominates the main effect _and_ every top
interaction. Reading is driven by letter-sound knowledge, but how much it converts
into reading is modulated by concepts (celf), decoding (nonword), phonological
memory (erbnw) and vocabulary (eowpvt). The theory-led phonics×vocab interaction
(`yarclet × eowpvt`) is confirmed but rank 4.

### Reading GAIN (`ewrswr_gain`, n=157) — who responds

Main effects: `age` 0.84 > `attend` (dose) 0.68 > `yarclet` 0.64 > `celf` 0.51 >
`blending` 0.46.

Top interactions:

| pair                      | strength |
| ------------------------- | -------: |
| age × yarclet             |     0.28 |
| age × ewrswr (baseline)   |     0.23 |
| age × blending            |     0.17 |
| yarclet × attend          |     0.16 |
| age × eowpvt              |     0.13 |
| group × ewrswr (baseline) |     0.13 |

**Age is the dominant moderator** of reading gain and interacts with letter-sound,
baseline reading, blending and vocab — a developmental-window signal (younger
children gain more, and skills convert to gains differently by age). `letter-sound
× attendance` (dose × readiness) and `group × baseline` (the aptitude-treatment
interaction) are present but mid-pack.

## Prioritised proposals for the Bayesian suite

1. **Moderated letter-sound → reading (top priority).** Extend the LRP58 mechanism
   (L → W) with a moderator: does the letter-sound→reading slope depend on
   vocabulary / concepts? Test moderators in screen order — `celf`, `nonword`,
   `eowpvt`. Reading _level/mechanism_ is better powered (all phases) than gains,
   the interactions are the strongest in the screen, and it directly answers "how
   do phonics and language combine to produce reading?" (the lexical-quality /
   dual-route hypothesis).
2. **Age as a developmental moderator of reading gain.** Let skill→gain effects
   vary with age (age × baseline-skill, or age-varying effects via the existing
   GP). Characterises _when_ skills convert to reading gains; younger children gain
   more.
3. **Who converts intervention into gains.** `letter-sound × attendance`
   (dose × readiness) and `group × baseline` (ATI) — the response-to-intervention
   question; ties to the deferred dose-response model (LRP65).
4. **The phonics route (separate outcome).** Model decoding (`nonword`) as the
   product of blending × letter-sound, then decoding → reading — the dual-route /
   triangle-model picture, and the basis for the proposed reading-route model.

## Caveats

- Small sample (54 children); interaction terms are data-hungry — use regularising
  priors, report wide credible intervals, treat as hypothesis-generating.
- The screen pools 4 rows/child (pseudo-replication) and SHAP can attribute
  link-function curvature to "interactions"; letter-sound's dominance inflates its
  interaction share. Confirm any interaction in a Bayesian model with subject
  random effects on the logit scale.
- The wait-list phase structure means treatment×covariate interactions must be
  defined within the ITT phase.

## Reproducing the screen

LightGBM (`objective="mae"`, 300 trees, lr 0.03) on `ewrswr` and `ewrswr_gain`;
`shap.TreeExplainer(...).shap_interaction_values(X)`; rank by mean |off-diagonal|.
Predictors as listed above. A scripted version belongs in `scripts/` as a
`screen_interactions.py` discovery utility (see the companion Claude Code handoff).

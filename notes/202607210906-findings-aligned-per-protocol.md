## What this family probes

The waitlist-crossover trial randomised children with Down syndrome to receive the reading intervention **immediately** or after a **wait**, measuring each child at four timepoints. Most other families in the suite use that randomisation directly. This family does something deliberately different: it re-aligns time so that _both_ arms are viewed from the moment their own intervention actually **started**. For the immediate-onset cohort that first on-intervention window runs from timepoint 1 to timepoint 3; for the wait-list cohort it runs from timepoint 2 to timepoint 4 (their crossover onset). Each child then contributes a single cross-sectional row — their post-window score, their score at onset, their age at onset, and their general ability — and the model is a Beta-Binomial ANCOVA (analysis of covariance: a regression of the post-window item count on the onset score plus covariates) through a logit link (so coefficients live on a log-odds scale and are read back as changes in proportion-correct, then translated to items). There is **no child random intercept**, because one row per child leaves nothing to pool.

The question this family answers, and that no other family answers, is a **per-protocol** ("as-treated") one: _once both arms have actually been on the intervention for their first roughly 40-week window, how do the two onset cohorts compare, and which child characteristics track the aligned post-window score?_ It complements the intention-to-treat and gain-factor families by asking about the dose children actually received rather than the arm they were assigned to.

**The single most important caveat, stated up front: nothing in this family is causal.** The cohort contrast compares immediate-onset children against wait-list-onset children, but in the aligned frame those two groups are **not** a randomised comparison — they differ systematically in **age at onset** (the wait-list cohort is older when it starts) and in **cohort/calendar timing**. Every coefficient here, the cohort gap included, is therefore an **adjusted association**, never a lever you can pull. This contrasts with the intention-to-treat (ITT) effect for the same outcomes, which _is_ causal because it rests on the original randomisation. Where the aligned cohort gap and the randomised ITT effect point the same way, treat that as mutual corroboration; where they diverge, the randomised effect is the authority for cause and the aligned gap is downgraded to confounded description.

## How to read these numbers

See the shared reading guide for the full version. In brief: the point estimate is the posterior **median**; uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** ("an 89% posterior probability the value lies in this range"); **direction** is read from the tail probability P(gap > 0), not from whether the interval excludes zero and never as a p-value. The **evidence ladder** attaches fixed labels to that tail probability — inconclusive (< 0.75), suggestive (≥ 0.75), moderate (≥ 0.91), strong (≥ 0.97), very strong (≥ 0.99) — describing the strength of evidence for a _directional_ claim, never the size of the effect. A high probability means _probably in that direction_, not _large_. Effects are estimated on the probability (proportion-correct) scale and translated to **items** by multiplying by the test maximum, because "+2 of 32 letter sounds" is far easier to grasp than a log-odds coefficient. A flat result is **inconclusive**, never "no effect".

For this family specifically: **no term is causal.** The headline estimand is the **cohort gap** — the aligned immediate-minus-wait-list difference, positive favouring the immediate-onset cohort. Across all nine models the covariate that is by far the most clearly resolved is the child's **own onset score**: children who start the window higher end it higher (a 99.9% posterior probability for that positive direction in every model), which is ordinary autoregression and, again, an adjusted association, not a claim that a high starting point _causes_ the later score. Reading any covariate here as a cause would be the Table-2 fallacy, and residual confounding by latent general ability remains throughout.

**Code-related skills (cohort gap leans positive).** The two skills closest to the intervention's directly-taught code targets show the clearest positive cohort associations. On **letter-sound knowledge (al-004, LS)** the immediate-onset cohort scores a median **+2.2 of 32 letter sounds higher** (89% credible range +0.2 to +4.2); the posterior probability of a positive association is **96%** — **moderate** evidence that the immediate cohort tends to score higher. This is the strongest, best-resolved cohort association in the family, and it lands on exactly the code-related skill that the randomised families identify as benefiting — so it is _consistent with_ that causal story without itself establishing it. On **word reading (al-001, WR)** the gap is a median **+2.1 of 79 words** (89% −0.5 to +4.8), with a **91%** probability of a positive association — **suggestive**. Both intervals still straddle or touch zero, so lead with the range, not the point.

**Broad language measures (flat or leaning negative).** The broad standardised language outcomes do not show a positive cohort lean. **Receptive vocabulary (al-002, RV)** is a median **+2.7 of 170** (89% −1.8 to +7.2), probability of a positive association **83%** — **suggestive** but on a 170-item scale a gap of a couple of items is slight, and this is a measure the randomised analysis treats as inconclusive, so the mild positive lean should not be over-read. **Expressive vocabulary (al-003, EV)** points the _other_ way: a median **−3.1 of 170** (89% −6.9 to +0.7), with a **90%** probability of a _negative_ association — **suggestive** that the immediate-onset cohort ends lower. **Basic concepts (al-007, LF)** is a median **−0.6 of 18** (89% −1.7 to +0.5), probability of a negative association **82%** — **suggestive**. **Receptive grammar (al-008, RG)** is a median **−1.4 of 32** (89% −3.1 to +0.3), probability of a negative association **91%** — **suggestive**. Because these are non-randomised cohort contrasts confounded by age at onset and calendar timing, the sensible reading of the negative leans is confounding (the wait-list cohort being older at onset, and broad language tracking general ability), **not** that the intervention harms these skills. For any causal judgement on these outcomes, the randomised ITT effect is the place to look.

**Floored / low-precision skills (inconclusive).** **Phonetic spelling (al-005, PS)** shows a cohort gap of essentially **+0.0 items** (89% −0.1 to +0.1), probability of a positive association **70%** — **inconclusive**; this near-zero gap with a very tight interval reflects how little movement there is on this hard, heavily-floored spelling measure over a single window. **Phoneme blending (al-006, PA)** is a median **+0.3 of 10** (89% −0.6 to +1.2), probability of a positive association **71%** — **inconclusive**; the 10-item scale is simply too imprecise here to add much, and it says so honestly. Blending is a skill the randomised analyses flag as benefiting, so the flat aligned view is a matter of precision rather than a contradiction.

**Dose sensitivity variant (al-101).** This is a variant of al-001 for word reading that adds the **cumulative number of sessions** delivered over the aligned window as an extra covariate. Session count is a **collider** on the causal diagram — a common descendant of both cohort (the immediate arm accrues sessions earlier) and ability/availability (more able or more available children attend more) — so conditioning on it can _open_ a back-door path and bias the other coefficients. It is included only as a sensitivity check and its slope must be read as a within-arm dose association, **never** a causal dose-response. Reassuringly, adding it barely moves the headline: the cohort gap is a median **+2.1 of 79 words** (89% −0.4 to +4.8), probability of a positive association **91%** — **suggestive**, essentially identical to al-001. The sensitivity check therefore corroborates al-001 rather than overturning it.

## What to take away

Read as a whole — and remembering that **not one coefficient in this family is causal** — the aligned per-protocol view tells a coherent descriptive story that lines up with the randomised families without pretending to replace them. The immediate-onset cohort tends to end its first on-intervention window higher on the **code-related skills** — letter-sound knowledge (al-004, moderate, +2.2 of 32) and word reading (al-001/al-101, suggestive, +2.1 of 79) — the very outcomes where the randomised analysis finds a genuine benefit. On the **broad language measures** the aligned gap is flat or mildly negative (expressive vocabulary, basic concepts, receptive grammar), which, given the age-at-onset and cohort-timing confounding these models cannot randomise away, is best read as the wait-list cohort being older and broad language tracking general ability, not as the intervention doing harm. The dominant signal everywhere is autoregression — a child's onset score all but sets their window-end score (99.9% probability positive in every model), an association, not a cause. Convergence is not a concern anywhere: all nine models pass the gate. The binding limitations are structural, not computational — the per-protocol cohort contrast is not randomised, the sample is small (N = 52–54), and phonetic spelling is heavily floored — so where a causal claim is wanted, defer to the ITT-suite note; this family's contribution is a per-protocol, association-only description of who ends each window where.

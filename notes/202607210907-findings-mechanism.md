## What this family probes

The study is a **waitlist-crossover randomised trial**: about 54 children with Down syndrome were randomly assigned to receive a reading/phonics intervention immediately or after a wait, and were tested at four timepoints. The mechanism family does **not** use that randomisation. Instead each model asks a purely descriptive question about how two _measured_ skills go together: **"among children who are otherwise alike, how does one skill (Y, the outcome) tend to differ as another skill (X, the exposure) is higher, and what is the shape of that relationship — straight-line, or with a threshold/'knee' above which it takes off?"**

Each model stacks every measurement phase and fits a curve of the outcome on the exposure with a **subject random intercept** — a per-child offset that partially pools stable between-child differences so that repeated measures on the same child are not double-counted. That random intercept is _not_ a control for latent general ability. Every model then adjusts for the covariates the study's causal diagram (its **DAG** — directed acyclic graph, a diagram that encodes which variables must be held fixed) pre-specifies for that particular skill pair. Those adjustment sets vary by pair and typically include general ability (GA, block-design), age (A), the outcome's own earlier score (the `_pre` term), and any upstream skills the diagram dictates. Because the adjustment set is fixed in advance from the DAG, a skill missing from a model was excluded by the diagram, not found unimportant.

This is what the causal families do not give you: a **scaffolding map** of the reading system — which skill tracks which, how steeply, and whether the link is roughly straight or has a readiness threshold. It is the observational complement to the randomised results. Crucially, **nothing in this family is causal.** Every slope, curve, knee, moderator and covariate here is an **adjusted association** — it describes _who progresses_, not a lever you can pull. Latent general ability remains an unmodelled confounder throughout, so none of these coefficients can be read as "X drives Y". The randomised causal claims live in the ITT suite and its difference-in-differences and gain-factor re-analyses; the attempted causal split of the reading gain is the mediation family's.

## How to read these numbers

Full conventions are in the shared reading guide. In brief:

- The point estimate is the posterior **median** (the posterior is the full probability distribution for a quantity given data and priors; the median is preferred because it does not change when the effect is re-expressed from the model's logit — log-odds — scale to a probability). With ~54 children, **lead with the interval, not the point**.
- Uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** ("an 89% posterior probability the value lies in this range"); 89%, not the customary 95%, is the deliberate house standard. Where the ground truth supplies it, the inner **50%** interval is the "most-of-the-mass" range.
- Direction is the **tail probability** P(association > 0), read directly, never as a p-value (there are none here). Its **evidence label** is fixed by round odds and describes the strength of the _directional_ claim only, never the size of the effect: _inconclusive_ (P < 0.75), _suggestive_ (≥ 0.75), _moderate_ (≥ 0.91), _strong_ (≥ 0.97), _very strong_ (≥ 0.99).
- Effects are modelled on a probability (proportion-correct) scale and translated to **items** (test units) by multiplying by the outcome test's maximum. Each headline below is the items difference in the outcome across the exposure's _observed range_ (bottom to top). Because those ranges and the outcome maxima differ enormously — word reading WR is out of 79, receptive/expressive vocabulary RV/EV out of 170, letter sounds LS and receptive grammar RG out of 32, basic concepts LF out of 18, phoneme blending PA out of 10, and **nonword reading NW out of only 6** — the raw items numbers are **not comparable across rows**. A large items swing on a 6-item scale is not "more important" than a smaller one on a 170-item scale; read each row against its own outcome maximum and lean on the per-model prose, not a ranking of the items column.
- **Everything here is an adjusted association.** Reading any mechanism slope, moderator coefficient or covariate as a lever is the **Table-2 fallacy**, and it is wrong for every quantity in this family.

Note on the surfaced ground truth: the headline items difference, its 89% interval, tail probability and (for the flexible fits) a knee are what these models produce, but only the range headline was surfaced for this note. Where a model's internal detail — the exact knee location, a below-versus-above-knee slope, or a moderator's own coefficient — is not in the ground truth, it is described qualitatively here and the precise figure is not quoted.

### Letter-sound knowledge → word reading (mech-058, and its comparators 101, 158, 173)

This is the dominant, robust relationship in the family, and it is re-estimated four ways that all agree. **mech-058** (the central estimate, across roughly 2 to 32 of 32 sounds) gives **+6.7 word-reading items [89% CI +2.6, +10.9], P(>0) = 0.996 — very strong** evidence the association is positive. **mech-101** is the deliberately simple **linear anchor** (a straight-line fit, no curvature allowed): **+9.8 items [+6.0, +13.5], P(>0) = 0.999, very strong**. **mech-158** is the **complete-case comparator** — the 43 children with no imputed confounders, a check that missing-data imputation is not driving the result — and lands essentially on top of mech-058: **+6.9 items [+2.4, +11.4], P(>0) = 0.996, very strong**. **mech-173** adds an age main effect (the no-interaction baseline for the age-moderation model mech-073): **+6.6 items [+2.5, +10.9], P(>0) = 0.996, very strong**. Read plainly: children who know more letter sounds tend to read more words, and this holds across every specification — but it is an **adjusted association**, not evidence that teaching more sounds _causes_ the extra reading.

### Vocabulary → word reading (mech-056, mech-057), and its flexible re-tests (mech-156, mech-157)

Broad standardised vocabulary shows a weaker, less certain link to word reading than letter sounds do. **mech-056** (receptive vocabulary RV, linear) gives **+2.9 items [−2.6, +8.2], P(>0) = 0.80 — only suggestive**, with the interval straddling zero. **mech-057** (expressive vocabulary EV, linear) gives **+5.3 items [−0.5, +10.9], P(>0) = 0.93 — moderate**, again with the interval touching zero. Both are far less resolved than the letter-sound curve. Two "GP knee-test" re-fits — a **Gaussian process** is a flexible, nonparametric curve that lets the data choose the shape and test for a threshold rather than forcing a straight line — corroborate that fragility. **mech-156** (RV → WR) gives **+0.2 items [−1.6, +3.5], P(>0) = 0.61 — inconclusive**, and **mech-157** (EV → WR) gives **+0.5 items [−1.1, +5.1], P(>0) = 0.70 — inconclusive**: once curvature is allowed, both broad-vocabulary associations are effectively flat. Both are gate-review fits (mech-156 carries 24 divergences = 0.067% of 36000 draws; mech-157 carries 16 = 0.044%), well under the 1% guidance and usable with that note; their internal knee locations are only weakly identified and are not quoted here.

### Taught vocabulary → word reading (mech-088, mech-089), and its knee-tests (mech-188, mech-189)

The specific words the intervention teaches track word reading steeply. **mech-088** (taught-receptive TR, linear) gives **+9.0 items [+4.9, +13.0], P(>0) = 0.999 — very strong**, and **mech-089** (taught-expressive TE, linear) **+8.6 items [+3.2, +13.4], P(>0) = 0.99 — very strong**. These are among the steepest associations in the family, but note that taught vocabulary is itself an intervention target, so the link partly reflects shared exposure to teaching rather than a distinct skill channel. The flexible re-fits are less resolved: **mech-188** (TR → WR, GP knee-test) gives **+3.6 items [+0.0, +8.3], P(>0) = 0.95 — moderate** (3 divergences = 0.008%), and **mech-189** (TE → WR, GP knee-test) **+1.8 items [−0.8, +7.8], P(>0) = 0.83 — suggestive** (1 divergence = 0.003%), the interval crossing zero. Both are usable-with-a-note gate-review fits; the threshold each implies is only weakly identified and is not quoted here.

### Phonological memory → word reading (mech-090)

**mech-090** relates phonological memory (word/nonword repetition, RW; entered as a raw-score covariate rather than a bounded item count) to word reading: **+3.1 items [+0.1, +6.0], P(>0) = 0.95 — moderate**, with the interval only just clearing zero. An adjusted association, and one whose lower limit sits close to zero.

### The decoding channel: letter sounds → nonword reading (mech-096, mech-172, mech-072)

Letter-sound knowledge tracks **nonword reading** (NW — decoding of unfamiliar letter strings, out of only 6 items) very tightly. **mech-096** (linear) gives **+3.7 of 6 nonword items across the letter-sound range [+2.8, +4.5], P(>0) = 0.999 — very strong**. **mech-172** is the two-predictor baseline (letter sounds plus a phoneme-blending main effect, the no-interaction companion to mech-072): letter sounds give **+3.3 items [+2.2, +4.2], P(>0) = 0.999 — very strong**. **mech-072** lets phoneme blending (PA) _moderate_ the letter-sound slope (a moderation model asks whether a second skill makes the first skill's slope steeper or shallower); the letter-sound marginal is **+4.0 items [+2.8, +4.8], P(>0) = 0.999 — very strong**. The family context flags mech-072 as the one clear interaction in the family — letter-sound knowledge and blending appear partly to substitute for each other in predicting decoding — but the interaction coefficient itself was not surfaced in this ground truth, so only the marginal association is quoted. Because NW has just 6 items, all three of these swings cover most of the nonword scale; read them as "letter-sound knowledge tracks almost the full nonword range", not as a large absolute-item effect, and remember they are not comparable with the WR-scale or vocabulary-scale rows.

### Negative-control outcomes (mech-097, mech-098, mech-099, mech-100)

These four models are **negative controls**: if letter-sound knowledge specifically drove _word reading_, it should show little association with skills it has no direct channel to. Instead all four "light up" with very strong positive associations after adjustment — **mech-097** LS → RV (receptive vocabulary) **+16.2 items [+9.2, +23.3], P(>0) = 0.999**; **mech-098** LS → EV (expressive vocabulary) **+13.8 items [+7.5, +20.1], P(>0) = 0.999**; **mech-099** LS → RG (receptive grammar) **+4.6 items [+1.6, +7.5], P(>0) = 0.99**; **mech-100** LS → LF (basic concepts) **+5.2 items [+2.9, +7.4], P(>0) = 0.999** — every one very strong. This is an interpretive flag, not a discovery: a skill that predicts _everything_ after adjustment is behaving like a marker of **shared general ability**, so the letter-sound associations (including the flagship LS → WR) are partly carried by a common factor the model cannot remove. It is the family's clearest in-built reminder that these are associations, not specific causal channels.

### Moderation / interaction models (mech-071, mech-093, mech-094, mech-095, mech-073)

These fit the LS → WR curve and let a second skill — the _moderator_ — shift its steepness. All report a positive letter-sound marginal: **mech-071** (× expressive vocabulary EV) **+5.2 items [+1.0, +9.8], P(>0) = 0.98 — strong**; **mech-093** (× receptive vocabulary RV) **+6.5 items [+2.4, +11.0], P(>0) = 0.996 — very strong**; **mech-094** (× taught-receptive TR) **+4.7 items [+0.7, +9.1], P(>0) = 0.98 — strong**; **mech-095** (× taught-expressive TE) **+5.0 items [+1.0, +9.5], P(>0) = 0.98 — strong** (a gate-review fit, 2 divergences = 0.006%, usable with a note); and **mech-073** (× age) **+7.3 items [+3.2, +11.7], P(>0) = 0.998 — very strong**. The moderation/interaction coefficients themselves — whether each second skill steepens or flattens the letter-sound slope — were not surfaced in this ground truth, so only the letter-sound marginal is quoted for each; the LS → WR association is positive and well-resolved in every one.

### Remaining knee-tests: phoneme blending and attendance (mech-190, mech-191)

Two further GP knee-tests probe exposures that are not resolved on their own. **mech-190** (phoneme blending PA → WR) gives **+0.5 items [−0.8, +4.3], P(>0) = 0.74 — inconclusive**, with the interval crossing zero — blending on its own does not trace a resolved word-reading curve (its role is clearer as a moderator in mech-072); it carries 31 divergences (0.086%, the most in the family, still under the 1% guidance). **mech-191** (intervention sessions attended IS → WR) gives **+2.1 items [+0.1, +4.2], P(>0) = 0.97 — moderate** (29 divergences = 0.081%). Attendance/dose is a special case: it is an adjusted association at best (and a collider elsewhere in the study), so this is emphatically **not** "more sessions cause more reading". Both are usable-with-a-note gate-review fits whose implied thresholds are weakly identified and not quoted here.

## What to take away

The mechanism family draws a consistent **developmental scaffolding map**, entirely in the language of association. The dominant, robust relationship is **letter-sound knowledge → word reading**: very strong across every specification (mech-058, mech-101, mech-158, mech-173), and echoed in the decoding channel **letter sounds → nonword reading** (mech-096, mech-172, mech-072). By contrast, **broad standardised vocabulary → word reading is weak and fragile** — only suggestive-to-moderate as straight lines (mech-056, mech-057) and effectively vanishing once the curve is allowed to bend (mech-156, mech-157). Taught vocabulary tracks reading steeply (mech-088, mech-089, mech-188) but partly because it is itself a teaching target. The moderation set (mech-071, mech-093, mech-094, mech-095, mech-073) leaves the letter-sound marginal positive and well-resolved throughout.

This triangulates cleanly with the causal families: the ITT suite finds the randomised benefit concentrated on the code-related and directly-taught skills (letter sounds, blending, word reading, taught expressive vocabulary) and inconclusive on broad vocabulary, and the mechanism map explains _why the pieces fit_ — letter-sound knowledge is the skill most tightly wired to word reading and decoding. But the family cannot make that causal split itself, and it says so out loud: the negative-control models (mech-097–100) show letter-sound knowledge predicting vocabulary, grammar and basic concepts just as strongly, which flags that a shared general-ability factor inflates all of these associations. That is the family's own honesty check, and the reason every number here stays an adjusted association. With ~54 children (43 in the complete-case mech-158), lead with the intervals — several (mech-056, mech-090, and the flat knee-tests mech-156/157/189/190) straddle or barely clear zero — and remember the items headlines are not comparable across rows because the exposure ranges and outcome maxima differ so widely (NW has only 6 items). Twenty of the 27 fits pass the gate cleanly; the seven divergence-only fits (mech-095, and the six knee-tests mech-156/157/188/189/190/191) are usable with the noted caveat, but their knee _locations_ are only weakly identified and are not reported here.

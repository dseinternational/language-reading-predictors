## Plain-language context

These statistical models are the second step after the exploratory
gradient-boosting review. The gradient-boosting models asked which variables
were useful for prediction. These models ask more structured questions: how large
the intervention effect might be, how uncertain that estimate is, and whether
the same story holds under companion or sensitivity models.

The study has repeated observations on children across time. Some models use the
randomized comparison between intervention groups, some use the waitlist
crossover structure, and some examine possible mechanisms such as letter-sound
knowledge or vocabulary. Those designs are not interchangeable. A positive
coefficient in one model may be a causal intervention effect, while a similar
positive coefficient in another model may only be an adjusted association.

The most important reading rule is therefore: check diagnostics first, then
check whether the model family supports causal language, then interpret the
size and uncertainty of the estimate.

## How to read this note

- A posterior distribution is the model's uncertainty distribution after
  combining the data with the prior assumptions. Instead of producing one best
  number, the model produces a range of plausible values.
- `p+` is the posterior probability that the reported quantity is greater than
  zero. Values near 1 favour a positive effect or association; values near 0
  favour a negative effect or association. This is not a frequentist p-value.
- Intervals are posterior credible intervals. A 95 percent credible interval
  means that, under the model and priors, 95 percent of the posterior mass for
  that quantity lies in that interval.
- `PASS` means the recorded convergence gate passed the project thresholds for
  R-hat, effective sample size, divergences, and BFMI. `FAIL` means do not treat
  the estimates as final, even if the posterior direction looks clear.
- R-hat, effective sample size, divergences, and BFMI are sampling diagnostics.
  In plain words, they ask whether the algorithm explored the posterior
  distribution well enough to trust the summaries.
- Only terms explicitly marked causal by the model family should be read
  causally: ITT `tau`, DiD deltas, and the randomized treatment terms in the
  main gain/level factor models. Mechanism, mediation, adjusted, aligned,
  horseshoe, and measurement-model terms are associations unless the model
  documentation says otherwise.
- A logit-scale coefficient is on the model's internal scale, not directly in
  test-score points. Where a marginal item-scale or probability-scale summary
  exists, I report that too because it is easier to interpret.
- LOO and Pareto-k are checks for predictive reliability and influential cases.
  A few bad Pareto-k values do not automatically invalidate a model, but they
  do mean that model comparison or prediction summaries should be treated with
  extra caution.

## Model-family guide

| Family                           | Plain-language question                                                                       | Causal interpretation?                                                                    |
| -------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| ITT                              | Did assignment to the intervention group improve the outcome?                                 | Yes, for `tau`, subject to diagnostics and the randomized design.                         |
| Joint ITT                        | Do the ITT effects look similar when outcomes are modelled together?                          | Yes for the joint `tau` estimates, but still outcome by outcome.                          |
| DiD                              | Does the waitlist crossover reproduce the intervention contrast within children over time?    | Yes for the DiD treatment contrast; dose variants are more conditional.                   |
| Gain factors                     | Does intervention status predict post-score after accounting for pre-score and other factors? | Yes for the randomized treatment term in the main models. Other terms are associations.   |
| Level factors                    | How do score levels differ across time and group?                                             | The randomized early time contrast is the cleanest; later contrasts are more descriptive. |
| Aligned                          | What is associated with gains after aligning children by intervention onset?                  | No. Cohort and dose terms are not randomized here.                                        |
| Mechanism and mediation          | Are changes in one skill statistically consistent with carrying part of the reading effect?   | Treat as exploratory model-based associations, not proof of mechanism.                    |
| Horseshoe and measurement models | Which predictors or latent domains look most important after regularization or pooling?       | No. These are ranking and measurement-structure checks.                                   |

## Cross-model Findings

The randomized ITT suite shows the clearest intervention signal for letter-sound knowledge (`L`), blending (`B`), word reading (`W`), and taught vocabulary (`TR`/`TE`). Untaught receptive/expressive vocabulary (`R`/`E`) and phoneme-level outcomes (`P`/`N`) are weak or inconclusive in the primary ITT models.

The waitlist-crossover DiD models broadly agree for the reading-code outcomes: positive item-scale contrasts for `W`, `L`, `B`, and taught expressive vocabulary, but not for untaught receptive vocabulary. Dose-response variants are positive, especially for `L`, but some dose models failed the divergence gate and need refitting before being used as evidence.

The gain-factor models reinforce that the randomized treatment term is strongest for `W` and `L`; `B` and `F` are positive but less certain; `R`, `E`, `P`, and `T` are inconclusive. Treated-only companion models mainly show strong own-baseline associations and domain links, which are useful descriptively but not causal.

The level-factor and aligned models are consistent with baseline skill being a dominant predictor. Cohort contrasts in the aligned models are not randomized and are mostly inconclusive, so they should not be used as intervention-effect estimates.

The mediation and latent-change models suggest that improvements in letter sounds and broader reading-route measures carry much of the association with later word-reading gains. This is plausible and coherent with the intervention theory, but these decomposition terms are model-based associations and need to be presented with that caveat.

Mechanism curves are visually and numerically exploratory. Several mechanism models failed because of divergences, and the curve intervals are often wide. They are better treated as generators of hypotheses for focused refits than as stable standalone findings.

## Reader's Short Version

The most accessible interpretation is:

- The clearest intervention-related evidence is for code-related reading skills:
  letter sounds, word reading, blending, and taught vocabulary.
- Evidence is weaker for broader untaught vocabulary and phoneme-level outcomes.
  That does not prove no effect; it means the current fitted models leave more
  uncertainty.
- The strongest causal claims should come from the randomized ITT, DiD, and main
  gain-factor treatment terms, not from every positive coefficient in the
  report.
- Baseline skill is a dominant predictor in many companion models. That is
  useful for understanding who scores higher later, but it is not an
  intervention effect.
- Mediation and latent-change results fit the theory that letter-sound and
  reading-route skills are central, but they should be described as exploratory
  support rather than proof of a developmental mechanism.
- Failed fits should be reported as failed fits. Their numbers can guide
  debugging or future modelling, but they should not be used as final evidence.

## Reporting Implications

- Put convergence first. For the 10 failed models, report that the fit did not pass diagnostics and avoid interpreting posterior estimates until refitted.
- Keep causal language narrow. ITT and DiD treatment contrasts can be described as intervention effects; most other coefficients should be called adjusted associations.
- Prefer item-scale summaries in prose when available, then give the model-scale coefficient in tables or technical appendices.
- The most defensible substantive claim from this run is that the intervention has its strongest evidence for code-related reading outcomes, especially letter sounds and word reading, while broader vocabulary and phonological outcomes are more uncertain.
- Mechanism and mediation results are coherent with a letter-sound/reading-route account, but they should be framed as exploratory model-based decompositions rather than proof that one skill causes another.

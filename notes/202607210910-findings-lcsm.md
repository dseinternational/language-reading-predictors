## What this family probes

A **latent change-score model** (also called a latent difference-score model) describes each child's skill as an unobserved (latent) "true score" trajectory across the four measurement waves, cleaned of measurement error, and then splits that trajectory into a _level_ at each wave and a _change_ from one wave to the next. Its distinctive question is about **developmental ordering**: does a child's earlier **level** of one skill predict the **later change** in a _different_ skill? That earlier-level-to-later-change link is called a **cross-lagged coupling**, and it is the family's headline quantity. A positive coupling `LS -> WR change` means children who are further along in letter-sound knowledge at one wave tend to make larger word-reading gains over the next interval, holding the rest of the model fixed. Where the other families ask "did the intervention work?" (ITT) or "how does it propagate?" (mechanism/mediation), the LCSM family asks which skill tends to _move first_ and appears to scaffold another — for example, does letter-sound knowledge at one wave forecast subsequent word-reading growth.

Each model works within a single child: the latent trajectory partially pools stable between-child differences, and every coupling is estimated adjusting for child age and the DAG-pre-specified confounders, so the coupling is a within-child predictive link rather than a raw correlation. The five models differ only in which pair (or triple) of skills they couple, and in which direction they test — several are built specifically to referee a direction-of-ordering question or to serve as a trimmed comparator.

**These couplings are associations, not causal effects.** They are conditional predictive links among latent trajectories in an observational within-child structure, and they remain confounded — most seriously by latent general ability, which the child-specific trajectory only partially absorbs and does **not** stand in for. A coupling does not license "skill X drives skill Y"; the cross-lag direction is _suggestive_ of ordering but is not proof of cause. Read them as hypothesis-generating evidence about sequence, cross-checked against the mechanism and ITT families — never as a lever you could pull (reading a coupling, an age term, or any adjustment covariate as if it were causal is the "Table-2 fallacy"). Four of the five models also write a separate **randomised window-1 contrast**, which _is_ causally interpretable and is reported below only as a consistency check, not as the coupling headline.

## How to read these numbers

Full conventions are in the shared reading guide; in brief:

- The point estimate is the posterior **median** (the posterior is the full probability distribution for the quantity given data and priors; the median is used because it is unchanged by re-scaling). Lead with the **interval**, not the point — with ~54 children an estimate that just clears a threshold is on average inflated.
- Uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** ("an 89% posterior probability the value lies in this range"). 89%, not the customary 95%, is the deliberate house standard.
- Direction is the **tail probability** — for instance a 99.9% posterior probability that a coupling is positive — read directly, never as a p-value. Its **evidence label** is fixed by round odds: _inconclusive_ (< 0.75), _suggestive_ (≥ 0.75), _moderate_ (≥ 0.91), _strong_ (≥ 0.97), _very strong_ (≥ 0.99). The label grades the _evidence for a directional claim_; it never describes the _size_ of the coupling. Direction and size are separate claims: a high probability says "probably in this direction", not "large".
- **Units.** A coupling is a slope between two latent quantities on the model's logit (log-odds) change scale — the logit is the log of the odds, the scale on which these bounded proportion-correct skills are modelled. A cross-lagged coupling is not a marginal proportion-correct effect, so — unlike an ITT effect — it does **not** translate cleanly into "items on a test"; the project reports these couplings in **logit units** and this note follows suit. A positive number means "higher earlier source level → larger later target gain"; a larger absolute number means a stronger link.

## Per-model findings

All five models were fit on the same data: **216 observations from 54 children** (4 waves each, 3 transition phases). The convergence gate (R-hat ≤ 1.01, effective sample size ≥ 400, BFMI ≥ 0.3, zero divergences) **passes for all five**. Each headline below is the model's clearest longitudinal coupling — the one carrying the strongest directional evidence — quoted from the fit's own key-findings output.

### LCSM-067 — do prior letter-sounds (LS) and vocabulary (EV) predict later word-reading (WR) change?

This model couples earlier levels of letter-sound knowledge and expressive vocabulary to subsequent word-reading change, within child. Of all its couplings, the one carrying the clearest directional evidence is the **age** term: older children make _less_ subsequent word-reading change on the logit scale (age → reading change = **−0.15 logit units**, 89% credible range **−0.21 to −0.09**). The posterior probability that this association is negative is **99.9%** — _very strong_ evidence that a higher earlier level accompanies less later change. Age here is an adjustment covariate, so this is an association describing who gains less, not a lever. The couplings among the latent skill trajectories are conditional predictive associations, not causal skill-to-skill effects. LCSM-067 does not report a randomised window-1 contrast.

### LCSM-081 — does prior word reading (WR) predict later change in taught vocabulary (TE, TR)?

This model tests a _reverse_ coupling — reading leading taught vocabulary — alongside the within-vocabulary link. The dominant, clearest coupling is **within taught vocabulary**: a higher earlier level of taught _receptive_ vocabulary predicts a larger later gain in taught _expressive_ vocabulary (prior TR → TE change = **+0.43 logit units**, 89% credible range **+0.26 to +0.62**). The posterior probability of a positive association is **99.9%** — _very strong_ evidence that a higher earlier level accompanies greater later change. In developmental-ordering terms, receptive knowledge of the taught words appears to lead expressive production of them, which is developmentally sensible; but it remains a conditional predictive association among latent trajectories, not a causal effect. The model's separate **window-1 randomised consistency contrast** was **+0.42 latent-logit units** (89% credible range **+0.09 to +0.75**); it is causally interpretable but is a check on the crossover design, not the coupling headline.

### LCSM-082 — reciprocal dominance: does blending (PA) lead word reading (WR) or vice versa, once letter-sounds (LS) are adjusted?

Built to referee a direction-of-ordering debate between phoneme blending and word reading, this model fits both cross-directions together while adjusting for letter-sound knowledge. The clearest coupling is neither of the blending↔reading directions but the **letter-sound lead**: prior letter-sound level predicts later word-reading change (prior LS → WR change = **+0.25 logit units**, 89% credible range **+0.14 to +0.37**), with a **99.9%** posterior probability that the association is positive — _very strong_ evidence that a higher earlier level accompanies greater later change. The developmental-ordering reading is that letter-sound knowledge is the upstream skill, though — as with every coupling here — this is a conditional predictive association among latent trajectories, not a causal skill-to-skill effect. The **window-1 randomised consistency contrast** was **+0.68 latent-logit units** (89% credible range **+0.31 to +1.06**); it is a check, not the coupling headline.

### LCSM-091 — change-on-change: do prior LS and EV _change_ predict later word-reading (WR) change?

This model adds "change-of-source" terms to ask whether later reading growth tracks how far a child has _got_ in letter-sounds and expressive vocabulary or how _fast they are currently moving_ in them. The clearest coupling reproduces the LCSM-067/LCSM-082 ordering: prior letter-sound level predicts later word-reading change (prior LS → WR change = **+0.22 logit units**, 89% credible range **+0.12 to +0.33**), with a **99.9%** posterior probability the association is positive — _very strong_ evidence that a higher earlier level accompanies greater later change. This is a conditional predictive association among latent trajectories, not a causal effect. The **window-1 randomised consistency contrast** was **+0.68 latent-logit units** (89% credible range **+0.31 to +1.05**); it is a check, not the coupling headline.

### LCSM-181 — the no-reverse-coupling comparator for LCSM-081

LCSM-181 is a deliberately trimmed twin of LCSM-081: it keeps the taught-receptive → taught-expressive coupling and the adjustment set but **drops the prior-word-reading terms**, serving as the leave-one-out (LOO) baseline — a cross-validation reference for out-of-sample predictive fit — against which LCSM-081's extra reading terms are judged. The within-vocabulary coupling is essentially unchanged when the reading terms are removed: prior TR → TE change = **+0.44 logit units** (89% credible range **+0.28 to +0.64**), with a **99.9%** posterior probability of a positive association — _very strong_ evidence that a higher earlier level accompanies greater later change. That stability (versus +0.43 in LCSM-081) confirms the receptive-leads-expressive ordering does not hinge on the reading terms. This too is a conditional predictive association, not a causal effect. The **window-1 randomised consistency contrast** was **+0.42 latent-logit units** (89% credible range **+0.09 to +0.75**); it is a check, not the coupling headline.

## What to take away

A single developmental-ordering story runs through the family, and it is internally consistent. **Letter-sound knowledge appears to lead word reading** (LCSM-082 and LCSM-091 both put prior LS → WR change at +0.22 to +0.25 logit units with very strong directional evidence), and within taught vocabulary **receptive knowledge leads expressive production** by a wide and very-strongly-supported margin that is stable whether or not the reading terms are included (prior TR → TE change ≈ +0.43 to +0.44 in LCSM-081 and its LCSM-181 comparator). LCSM-067 adds that older children make smaller subsequent word-reading changes (the age coupling, −0.15 logit units, very strong). Every one of these is an **association** — a conditional predictive link among latent trajectories, still open to residual confounding by latent general ability — so they are offered as corroborating evidence about sequence, not as independent causal claims. The one causally-interpretable quantity in each of the four crossover-aware models, the randomised window-1 contrast, is positive throughout (+0.42 to +0.68 latent-logit units), echoing the ITT suite and indicating the latent-trajectory machinery is not distorting the randomised signal. All five gates pass cleanly, so no coefficients are held and no funnel caveat applies; but with only 54 children and four waves the trajectories are lightly identified, so lead with the intervals and treat the ordering as a hypothesis for the mechanism and mediation families to test, not as settled cause.

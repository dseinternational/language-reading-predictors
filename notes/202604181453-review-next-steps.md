## Cross-walk: where the two approaches agree, and what is exclusive to each

**Strong agreement — directional effect on reading**

Both approaches identify **word reading** and **letter-sound
knowledge** as the outcomes most affected by the intervention and
most predictable from other language-reading measures.

- GB: LRP02 (W level, R² 0.57) and LRP06 (L level, R² 0.52) are the
  best-fitting level models among reading-adjacent outcomes.
- Bayesian: LRP52 τ_W credibly negative; LRP55 τ_L the largest
  credibly negative effect in the set.
- Both identify that W and L interact: LRP06's top predictor is
  `ewrswr` (importance 0.495); LRP58 finds L → W credibly mediates
  on the logit scale.

**Strong agreement — vocabulary predicts vocabulary**

- GB LRP04 (E level): top predictors are `aptinfo`, `celf`, `rowpvt`,
  `yarclet`, `ewrswr` — mix of language and reading.
- GB LRP08 (R level): top predictors are `b1reto`, `aptinfo`,
  `eowpvt`, `celf`, `trog` — pure language cluster.
- Bayesian LRP53/54 γ-couplings: γ_F, γ_R, γ_E, γ_T exchange modest
  probability mass but none reach 95 % credibility individually.

**Partial disagreement — gain models vs ITT**

- GB gain models (LRP01/03/05/07/09) assign lowest R² to the gain
  tasks (0.06–0.25) and frequently send cross-domain pre-scores to
  zero importance (LRP07 has 11 predictors at exactly 0.000).
- Bayesian ITT models recover credibly non-zero τ on W and L
  despite the same small sample and the same target difficulty.

The Bayesian approach is more powerful here because it partitions
variance over a single treatment indicator rather than learning a
function approximation of the outcome surface. It finds signal
where the GB approach does not — consistent with GB flagging that
predicting *gains* from pre-scores is intrinsically hard even
though there *is* a mean-difference between arms.

## Considerations: which of these should enter the Bayesian set?

### Tier 1 — confounder adjustment for ITT models (LRP60)

**Candidates**: `mumedupost16`, `dadedupost16`, `agebooks`.

These are *time-invariant* baseline characteristics. They confound
the intervention effect only if they correlate with group
assignment (randomisation *should* break this), but they also
explain residual between-child variance and therefore tighten the
τ credibility interval. GB selection consistently returns
`mumedupost16` in 5/10 models and `dadedupost16` in 5/10, with
non-trivial importances.

**Recommendation**: add as optional linear covariates in the ITT
factory (`build_itt_model(..., adjust_for=("mumedupost16",
"dadedupost16"))`). Fit a new variant **LRP60** alongside LRP52
(same outcome, W). If τ_W shifts materially, the intervention
effect is partly confounded with SES; if it does not, the τ_W CI
just narrows modestly. Either outcome is informative.

Caveat: missingness is ≥5 % for both mother's and father's
education in the data; would need to decide drop vs impute.
`agebooks` has similar missingness.

### Tier 2 — intervention-fidelity outcomes (LRP61)

**Candidates**: `b1exto`, `b1reto` (Block 1 taught expressive and
receptive vocabulary). Similar Block 2 measures exist but are only
recorded at t3/t4.

These are directly teaching-sensitive: they measure knowledge of
the specific words explicitly taught during each block. They are
the most proximal outcomes of the intervention and should show the
*largest* treatment effects of any outcome in the study.

**Recommendation**: add **LRP61**, a new ITT analysis with
`b1exto` as the outcome (and a companion for `b1reto`). This would:

1. Serve as a positive control for the ITT methodology — if the
   intervention does not measurably raise taught-vocabulary
   outcomes, something is wrong with the pipeline or the data.
2. Quantify the teaching gradient that the broader outcomes
   (W, L, etc.) are downstream of.

The denominator for these outcomes is the number of items tested
(see `data_variables.py`); the pre-score is t1 for Block 1, so
this fits cleanly into the existing RCT-phase ITT framework with
the same 53 children per fit.

### Tier 3 — phonological-memory mechanism (LRP62)

**Candidates**: `erbword` (word repetition), `erbnw` (nonword
repetition). Core phonological-memory measures that are
theoretically upstream of letter-sound knowledge and word reading
in most accounts of literacy development.

**Recommendation**: add **LRP62** — a mechanism model with
`erbword` or `erbnw` as the mechanism variable on word reading.
Adjustment set would mirror LRP56/57/58 logic:

- G, A — standard confounders.
- W_pre — baseline.
- Should R (or E, or L) be conditioned on? Phonological memory
  is proposed to be upstream of all three, so conditioning on
  them would block legitimate indirect paths. Keep them out of
  the adjustment set pending a DAG review with the research team.

If `f^erbword` on W is credibly positive, phonological memory is
a candidate alternative pathway to letter-sound knowledge for
explaining the intervention's reading effect.

### Tier 4 — articulation mechanism (LRP63)

**Candidates**: `deappin`, `deappvo`, `deappfi`, or the composite
`deappav`/`deapp_c`. The DEAP picture-naming subtests measure
initial, vowel, and final phoneme accuracy, with GB repeatedly
selecting `deappin` (7/10 models).

**Recommendation**: lower priority than Tier 3 but conceptually
similar. Articulation may mediate the intervention's effect on
letter-sound knowledge and word reading through phoneme
awareness. **LRP63** — mechanism model with `deappav` (composite)
on L and on W, two variants. Adjustment set mirrors LRP58's L → W
(so E, R) plus whatever DAG reasoning says about
articulation→phonology dependencies.

### Tier 5 — additional ITT outcomes (LRP64)

**Candidates**: `aptgram` (expressive grammar raw score),
`aptinfo` (expressive information raw score). These are currently
*not* bounded counts in the usual binomial sense — they are raw
scores on the Action Picture Test without a fixed item maximum in
our data dictionary. They would need either:

- a Gaussian likelihood on the raw scale, or
- a confirmed item-max so Beta-Binomial can be used.

**Recommendation**: treat as a data-dictionary follow-up.
Confirm maxima with the research team, then **LRP64** as an
additional ITT outcome family for grammar-specific outcomes. Will
complement T (TROG receptive grammar) which is already in the
eight-outcome joint model.

### Deferred — intervention dosage (LRP65+, was originally LRP59+ in the brief)

**Candidates**: `attend`, `attend_cumul`.

Per the original brief:

> No intervention dosage (I) — deferred.
> No I × {covariate} interactions — deferred with I.

The GB results make this even more compelling: `attend` appears
in every gain model (LRP01, LRP05, LRP07, LRP09). A dose-response
Bayesian model would estimate how τ_W, τ_L etc. scale with actual
intervention attendance, and would also let us decompose into
natural-direct and natural-indirect effects.

**Recommendation**: defer as planned to a subsequent work phase.
Requires an explicit research decision about how to treat the
wait-for-intervention arm's post-phase-0 attendance, since in
phases 1 and 2 both arms are on the intervention (see
`project_rli_phase_structure.md` in project memory).

## Other predictors not recommended for inclusion now

- `gender`, `numchil`, `vision`, `hearing` — appear in ≤2 GB
  models with low importance. Conditioning on them would widen
  credible intervals without substantive gain.
- `time` — already handled as phase-specific intercepts in the
  mechanism models; adding it linearly in ITT is redundant under
  the RCT-phase-only framing.
- `area` — categorical study-site variable; small sample per site,
  unlikely to be identifiable.
- Language sample measures (`lsam*`) — only recorded at t1/t2, not
  usable across all phases.
- `sdq` — only at t1.

## Priority order for the next Bayesian work phase

1. **LRP60 (SES-adjusted ITT)** — lowest effort (one factory-level
   `adjust_for` flag), largest near-term benefit. Gets the
   intervention-effect story onto a firmer footing.
2. **LRP61 (taught-vocabulary ITT)** — confirmation control. If
   this does not show a strong effect, something is wrong. If it
   does, the teaching gradient is quantified for the first time in
   this suite.
3. **LRP62 (phonological-memory mechanism)** — broadens the
   mechanism picture beyond L → W and picks up the
   phonological-memory literature.
4. **LRP63 (articulation mechanism)** — extends mechanism picture;
   lower priority than LRP62 because the theoretical case is
   weaker.
5. **LRP64 (grammar ITT)** — contingent on confirming item maxima
   for `aptgram` / `aptinfo`.
6. **LRP65+ (dose-response with attendance)** — reserved for a
   later work phase; major analytical addition.

## Analytical spec sketches

### LRP60 — SES-adjusted ITT

Add a 3-element linear block to `build_itt_model`:

    eta_i += beta_mum * mumedu_i + beta_dad * dadedu_i + beta_books * agebooks_i

Priors: `Normal(0, 0.3)` on each (same scale as `gamma_cross`).
Standardise each covariate before use. Fit with `outcome="W"`
first (LRP60), replicate for R/E (LRP60a, LRP60b).

Dropped-row rate after adding these three will exceed the 2 %
from LRP52's baseline drop — flag the delta in the report.

### LRP61 — taught-vocabulary ITT

Register `V.B1EXTO` and `V.B1RETO` in `measures.py` with their
item maxima (per the study protocol: 24 items each for Block 1
Expressive and Receptive combined). Add to `ITT_OUTCOMES` (or a
new symbol family to keep the eight-outcome joint model stable).
Reuse `build_itt_model` with `outcome_symbol` set to the new
Block 1 symbol.

### LRP62 — phonological-memory mechanism

Identical template to LRP56–58 with `mechanism_symbol="erbword"`
or `"erbnw"` and adjustment set `["G", "A", "W_pre"]`. Add
`measures.py` entries with item maxima (ERB: word 18, nonword 18
per the test manuals — confirm).

### LRP63 — articulation mechanism

Template as above with `mechanism_symbol="deappav"` (composite).
`deappav` is already bounded and scored on a percentage scale;
will need a transform or re-coding to use the Beta-Binomial
likelihood consistently. May warrant a Gaussian-likelihood
mechanism factory variant.

## How this helps answer the research question

The research question is "what influences progress in language and
reading for children with Down syndrome?" The current Bayesian
suite answers it for the *eight outcomes* as a group but cannot
distinguish:

- intervention effects from SES effects (Tier 1),
- broad-transfer effects from directly-taught vocabulary effects
  (Tier 2),
- letter-sound mediation from phonological-memory or articulation
  mediation (Tiers 3–4),
- total (ITT) effects from dose-response (deferred).

Adding Tiers 1–3 would materially sharpen the story for the
existing RCT-phase estimands. The GB programme has already pre-
selected which predictors carry robust information; the
Bayesian models have not caught up on that front yet.

## What this family does, and the one question it answers

The waitlist-crossover trial randomised children to receive the reading intervention **immediately** or after a **wait**. The other families use that randomisation directly. This family does something different: it re-aligns time so that _both_ arms are viewed from the moment their own intervention **started**. For the immediate arm that first on-intervention window runs from timepoint 1 to timepoint 3; for the wait-list arm it runs from timepoint 2 to timepoint 4 (their crossover onset). Each child then contributes a single cross-sectional row: their post-window score, their score at onset, their age at onset, and their general ability. The model is a Beta-Binomial ANCOVA (analysis of covariance — a regression of the post-score on the pre-score plus covariates) on the bounded item count, through a logit link (so coefficients live on a log-odds scale and are read back as changes in proportion-correct). There is **no child random intercept** — one row per child means there is nothing to pool.

The question it answers, and that no other family answers, is: _once both arms have actually been on the intervention for their first window, how do the two onset cohorts compare, and which child characteristics track the aligned post-score?_ That is a "per-protocol" (as-treated) view, not an "as-randomised" one.

**The single most important caveat, stated up front and repeated below: nothing in this family is causal.** The cohort contrast compares immediate-onset children against wait-list-onset children, but those two groups are **not** a randomised comparison in the aligned frame — they differ systematically in **age at onset** (the wait-list arm is older when it starts) and in **calendar timing/cohort**. Every coefficient here, the cohort contrast included, is an **adjusted association**, never a lever you can pull. Contrast this with the intention-to-treat (ITT) effect τ for the same outcomes (ITT-suite note, `notes/202607200901-findings-itt-suite.md`), which _is_ causal because it rests on the original randomisation. Where the aligned cohort gap and the ITT τ point the same way, that is mutual corroboration; where they diverge, trust the randomised τ for cause and read the aligned gap only as description.

## How to read these numbers (recap)

See the shared reading guide for the full version. In brief: the point estimate is the posterior **median** (the posterior is the full probability distribution for a quantity given data and priors; the median is its middle value); uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** ("89% posterior probability the value lies in this range"), with the inner **50%** interval as the "most-of-the-mass" range; **direction** is read from the tail probability P(effect > 0), not from whether an interval excludes zero and never as a p-value. The **evidence ladder** attaches fixed labels to that tail probability — inconclusive (< 0.75), suggestive (≥ 0.75), moderate (≥ 0.91), strong (≥ 0.97), very strong (≥ 0.99) — describing the strength of evidence for a _directional_ claim, never the size of the effect. Effects are estimated on the probability (proportion-correct) scale and translated to **items** by multiplying by the test maximum.

For this family specifically: **no term is causal.** Reading a covariate coefficient (age, ability, baseline) as if it caused the outcome is the Table-2 fallacy, and residual confounding by latent general ability remains throughout (the block-design ability term helps but does not fully stand in for it). The two headline tables per model are `cohort_marginal.csv` (the immediate-vs-wait-list gap, on both scales) and `factor_summary.csv` (every coefficient with its direction label).

Every gate passes cleanly: zero divergences, maximum R-hat ≤ 1.001, minimum effective sample size ≥ 7,650 across all nine fits. Convergence is not a concern anywhere in this family.

Across all models the **own aligned baseline** (`gamma_own`) is the dominant predictor — very strong and positive everywhere (P(> 0) = 1.00 for W, R, E, L, P, B, F, and 0.99997 for T), with logit slopes from about 0.47 (RG) to 0.81 (E, P). In plain words: where a child stands at onset almost entirely sets where they stand at the end of the window. This is expected autoregression, and it is an association, not a claim that a high baseline _causes_ the later score.

**al-001 — word reading (WR).** The immediate-onset cohort scores a median **+2.1 of 79 words** higher (probability +0.027; 50% CrI +1.1 to +3.2 words, 89% −0.5 to +4.8), P(> 0) = 0.906 — **suggestive** evidence of a positive cohort association, not a randomised effect. The notable covariate association is **age at onset** (`gamma_A`): median −0.179 on the logit scale, P(< 0) = 0.975, **strong** — older children reach a lower aligned word-reading score once baseline and ability are held fixed. General ability (`gamma_ability`, block design) is positive but only suggestive (+0.107, P(> 0) = 0.864). The randomised, causal counterpart for word reading is the ITT τ in the ITT-suite note; here we can only say immediate-onset children _tended_ to end higher.

**al-002 — receptive vocabulary (RV).** Cohort gap median **+2.7 of 170** (probability +0.016; 50% +0.8 to +4.6, 89% −1.8 to +7.2), P(> 0) = 0.832, **suggestive**. Ability is suggestive-positive (+0.043, P = 0.797) and age-at-onset weakly negative (−0.036, favoured-negative probability 0.777, suggestive). This broad standardised vocabulary measure is one the randomised ITT analysis finds inconclusive and probably negligible, so the mild positive lean here should not be over-read.

**al-003 — expressive vocabulary (EV).** The cohort gap points the _other_ way: median **−3.1 of 170** (probability −0.018; 50% −4.7 to −1.5, 89% −6.9 to +0.7), P(> 0) = 0.100, i.e. P(< 0) = 0.900 — **suggestive** evidence that the immediate-onset cohort ends _lower_ on expressive vocabulary in the aligned frame. Because this is a non-randomised cohort contrast confounded by age-at-onset, the sensible reading is confounding, not harm: age-at-onset is negative and **strong** (−0.093, P(< 0) = 0.982) and ability is positive and **very strong** (+0.136, P(> 0) = 0.996), so more able and younger-at-onset children have higher expressive vocabulary regardless of arm. The randomised ITT τ, not this gap, is the place to judge whether the intervention changes expressive vocabulary.

**al-004 — letter-sound knowledge (LS).** The strongest cohort association in the family: median **+2.2 of 32 letter sounds** (probability +0.070; 50% +1.4 to +3.1, 89% +0.2 to +4.2), P(> 0) = 0.961, **moderate** and positive. Here age-at-onset and ability are both inconclusive (0.594 and 0.561 respectively), so the cohort lean is not obviously explained by those two confounders — though age-at-onset and calendar timing still are not randomised away. Letter-sounds is exactly the code-related skill the randomised ITT and gain-factor analyses find a strong causal benefit on, so this per-protocol association is _consistent with_ that causal story while not itself establishing it.

**al-005 — phonetic spelling (PS).** This outcome is heavily floored (many children score zero), so the aligned model uses the suite **floor rule**: an off-the-floor Bernoulli likelihood, and the cohort marginal is therefore a **risk difference** in the probability of being off the spelling floor, _not_ an items count. Immediate-onset children are a median **+3.2 percentage points** more likely to be off the floor (+0.032; 50% −1.0 to +7.4 points, 89% −7.2 to +13.2), P(> 0) = 0.698 — **inconclusive**. The one clear association is **general ability**: +0.457 on the logit scale, P(> 0) = 0.973, **strong** — more able children are markedly more likely to have moved off the spelling floor, holding cohort and onset fixed. Age-at-onset is inconclusive here (P = 0.551).

**al-006 — phoneme blending (B).** Cohort gap median **+0.3 of 10** (probability +0.030; 50% −0.1 to +0.7, 89% −0.6 to +1.2), P(> 0) = 0.706, **inconclusive**. No covariate reaches even the suggestive bar (age-at-onset P(< 0) = 0.667, ability P(> 0) = 0.596). Blending is a skill the randomised analyses flag as benefiting; the aligned view is simply too imprecise on this 10-item scale to add much, and it says so honestly.

**al-007 — basic concepts (LF).** Cohort gap median **−0.6 of 18** (probability −0.034; 50% −1.1 to −0.2, 89% −1.7 to +0.5), P(> 0) = 0.180, i.e. P(< 0) = 0.820 — **suggestive** of a lower aligned score for the immediate-onset cohort. Both covariates are inconclusive (age-at-onset P(< 0) = 0.507; ability P(< 0) = 0.548). As with expressive vocabulary, read this negative lean as a non-randomised cohort/timing artefact, not as evidence the intervention lowers basic concepts.

**al-008 — receptive grammar (RG).** Cohort gap median **−1.4 of 32** (probability −0.045; 50% −2.2 to −0.7, 89% −3.1 to +0.3), P(> 0) = 0.091, i.e. P(< 0) = 0.909 — **suggestive** of a lower aligned grammar score in the immediate-onset cohort. The dominant associations are **general ability** (+0.273, P(> 0) = 0.9995, **very strong**) and, weakly, age-at-onset (−0.059, P(< 0) = 0.793, suggestive): grammar tracks general ability strongly, which is why a cohort contrast that does not randomise ability apart from onset is easy to misread. Grammar is a broad language measure well outside the intervention's directly-taught targets.

**al-101 — word reading, cumulative-session dose sensitivity variant (WR).** This is a variant of al-001 that adds the **cumulative number of sessions** delivered over the aligned window as an extra covariate (`use_dose = True`). Dose is a **collider** on the causal diagram — a common descendant of both group (the immediate arm accrues sessions earlier) and ability (more able or more available children attend more) — so conditioning on it can _open_ a back-door path and bias the other coefficients. It is included only as a sensitivity check, and its slope must be read as a within-arm dose association, **never** a causal dose-response. The dose slope `gamma_dose` is median +0.034 on the logit scale (89% −0.115 to +0.191), P(> 0) = 0.641 — **inconclusive**, exactly the weak, collider-tainted signal expected once onset baseline, age-at-onset and ability are already in the model. Reassuringly, adding dose barely moves the headline: the cohort gap is +2.1 of 79 words (probability +0.027, P(> 0) = 0.909), essentially identical to al-001's +2.1 words, and age-at-onset stays negative and now **moderate** (−0.171, P(< 0) = 0.969). The sensitivity check therefore supports al-001 rather than overturning it, while the dose term itself carries no interpretable causal content.

## What the family concludes

Read as a whole, and remembering that **not one coefficient here is causal**, the aligned per-protocol view tells a coherent descriptive story that lines up with the randomised families without pretending to replace them. The immediate-onset cohort tends to end its first on-intervention window higher on the **code-related skills** — letter-sound knowledge (al-004, moderate, +2.2 of 32) and word reading (al-001/al-101, suggestive, +2.1 of 79) — the very outcomes where the randomised ITT τ finds a genuine causal benefit. On the **broad language measures** the aligned cohort gap is flat or mildly negative (expressive vocabulary al-003, basic concepts al-007, receptive grammar al-008), which, given the strong age-at-onset and ability confounding those models expose, is best read as the wait-list cohort being older and (on grammar and expressive vocabulary) tracking general ability, not as the intervention doing harm. The consistent, dominant signal everywhere is autoregression — onset level predicts window-end level — with general ability a strong secondary predictor of spelling (al-005) and grammar (al-008), and age-at-onset a negative predictor of word reading (al-001/al-101) and expressive vocabulary (al-003).

Triangulation: where this family agrees with the ITT and gain-factor results (code skills up), it corroborates them descriptively; where it disagrees or goes flat (broad vocabulary/grammar), the randomised τ is the authority and the aligned gap is downgraded to confounded description. The dose sensitivity variant (al-101) confirms that the word-reading cohort association is robust to conditioning on sessions attended, while the dose slope itself is inconclusive and collider-tainted.

## Caveats and convergence

Convergence is excellent across all nine models — zero divergences, R-hat ≤ 1.001, effective sample sizes in the thousands — so nothing is held on technical grounds. The binding limitations are structural, not computational. **First and foremost, the per-protocol cohort contrast is not randomised**: it is confounded by age-at-onset and by cohort/calendar timing, so no term is causal and residual confounding by latent general ability remains even after the block-design ability covariate. **Second, the sample is small** (N = 52–54; one to two rows dropped per model for missing data), so single point estimates that just clear a threshold are on average magnitude-inflated — lead with the intervals, several of which straddle zero. **Third, floor effects**: phonetic spelling (al-005) is modelled off-floor as a risk difference, so its cohort marginal is not comparable to the items-scale gaps of the other outcomes. For any causal claim about these skills, defer to the ITT-suite note (`notes/202607200901-findings-itt-suite.md`); this family's contribution is a per-protocol, association-only description of who ends each window where.

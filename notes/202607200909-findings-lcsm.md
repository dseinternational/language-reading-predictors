## What this family does, and the question it answers

A **latent change-score model** describes each child's skill as an unobserved (latent) trajectory across the four measurement waves, then splits the trajectory into a _level_ at each wave and a _change_ from one wave to the next. Its distinctive question is about **developmental ordering**: does a child's earlier **level** of one skill predict the **later change** in a _different_ skill — and, when you fit the two directions together, does one direction lead the other? That earlier-level-to-later-change link is called a **cross-lagged coupling**, and it is the family's headline quantity (rows containing `->` in `coupling_summary.csv`).

Concretely: a positive coupling `L -> W change` means children who are further along in letter-sound knowledge at one wave tend to make larger word-reading gains over the next interval, holding the rest of the model fixed. The other families ask "did the intervention work?" (ITT), "how does it propagate?" (mechanism/mediation) or "who scores higher at each wave?" (level factors); the LCSM family instead asks which skill tends to _move first_ and pull another along behind it.

**These couplings are associations, not causal effects.** They are conditional predictive links among latent trajectories in an observational within-child structure. A coupling does not license "skill X drives skill Y": both directions are confounded, most seriously by latent general ability, which the child-specific part of the model only partially absorbs and does **not** stand in for. Read them as hypothesis-generating evidence about ordering, cross-checked against the mechanism models and the ITT suite — never as a lever you could pull. (Reading a coupling, or any adjustment covariate below, as if it were causal is the "Table-2 fallacy".) Four of the five models also write a _randomised_ window-1 contrast, which **is** causally interpretable and is described separately as a consistency check.

## How to read these numbers (recap)

Full conventions are in the shared reading guide; in brief:

- The point estimate is the posterior **median** (the posterior is the full probability distribution for the quantity given data and priors; the median is used because it is unchanged by re-scaling). Lead with the **interval**, not the point — with ~54 children an estimate that just clears a threshold is on average inflated.
- Uncertainty is the **89% equal-tailed credible interval** ("89% posterior probability the value lies in this range"), with the inner **50%** interval as the "most-of-the-mass" range. 89%, not 95%, is the deliberate house standard.
- Direction is the **tail probability** `P(coupling > 0)`, read directly, never as a p-value. Its **evidence label** is fixed by round odds: _inconclusive_ (< 0.75), _suggestive_ (≥ 0.75), _moderate_ (≥ 0.91), _strong_ (≥ 0.97), _very strong_ (≥ 0.99). The label grades the _evidence for a directional claim_; it never describes the _size_ of the coupling.
- **Units.** A coupling is a **slope between two latent quantities on the model's logit (log-odds) change scale** — how many logit units of later change in the target skill accompany a one-unit-higher earlier latent level of the source skill. Unlike an ITT effect, a cross-lagged coupling is _not_ a marginal proportion-correct effect, so it does **not** translate cleanly into "items on a test"; the project reports these couplings in **logit units** and this note follows suit. A positive number means "higher earlier source level -> larger later target gain"; a larger absolute number means a stronger link. Where a model provides SD-standardised couplings (LCSM-082's dominance table) that put the two directions on a comparable footing, those are used to compare directions.

### LCSM-067 — do prior letter-sounds (L) and vocabulary (E) predict later word-reading (W) change?

Both focal couplings point the same way and the evidence is _very strong_ for each. A higher earlier level of **expressive vocabulary** is associated with a larger subsequent gain in **word reading** (+0.286 logit units, 89% CI +0.096 to +0.479; P(>0) = 0.992), and so is a higher earlier level of **letter-sound knowledge** (+0.137, 89% CI +0.047 to +0.233; P(>0) = 0.993). In developmental-ordering terms: children who are further ahead in spoken vocabulary and in letter-sound knowledge tend to move fastest on word reading next — both skills appear to _lead_ word reading. Of the two, the expressive-vocabulary coupling is the larger, though the intervals overlap, so the ranking between them is not settled.

Three within-model adjusted associations round out the picture (all associations, not levers): word reading shows negative **self-feedback** (b_self[W] = -0.220, 89% CI -0.291 to -0.153; P(>0) ≈ 0), i.e. children already high on word reading gain less next — the diminishing-returns pattern expected of a bounded, partly ceiling-limited score; a positive average change intercept (a_change[W] = +0.321, 89% CI -0.008 to +0.642; P(>0) = 0.941, moderate); and a negative **age** coupling (d_age[W] = -0.147, 89% CI -0.209 to -0.087; P(>0) ≈ 0, very strong), older children making smaller later word-reading changes on the logit scale. This is the one model of the five with a pooled child intercept rather than the crossover-aware arm×window structure, so it writes **no** window-1 randomised contrast.

### LCSM-081 — does prior word reading (W) predict later change in taught vocabulary (TE, TR)?

This model tests a _reverse_ coupling — reading leading vocabulary — alongside the within-vocabulary link. The dominant coupling is **within taught vocabulary**: a higher earlier level of taught _receptive_ vocabulary predicts a larger later gain in taught _expressive_ vocabulary (g_TR->TE = +0.428, 89% CI +0.255 to +0.619; P(>0) = 1.000, very strong). Receptive knowledge of the taught words clearly _leads_ expressive production of them — you understand a taught word before you produce it, which is developmentally sensible.

The reverse reading-to-vocabulary couplings are genuine but **small**: prior word reading predicts later taught-_receptive_ change (g_W->TR = +0.084, 89% CI +0.049 to +0.121; P(>0) = 1.000, very strong) and later taught-_expressive_ change (g_W->TE = +0.062, 89% CI +0.015 to +0.112; P(>0) = 0.984, strong). So the _direction_ of a reading->taught-vocabulary link is well supported, but its magnitude is roughly a fifth to an eighth of the within-vocabulary receptive->expressive coupling. Reading nudges taught-word learning; taught-receptive knowledge does the heavy lifting for taught-expressive gains.

Adjustment covariates (all associations; the DAG's exogenous confounders): the Early Repetition Battery total (**erbto**, phonological memory / speech repetition) is positively coupled to taught-vocabulary change (b_erbto = +0.122, 89% CI +0.046 to +0.198; P(>0) = 0.994), the DEAP picture-composite speech score (**deapp_c**) is _negatively_ coupled (b_deapp_c = -0.112, 89% CI -0.180 to -0.045; P(>0) = 0.004, strong evidence of a negative association — but note the project's caveat that the trial-era DEAP scoring is lenient and not directly comparable to gold-standard phonology scoring), and hearing (**hs**) is inconclusive (+0.016, P(>0) = 0.623). Both taught measures show negative self-feedback (b_self[TE] = -0.386; b_self[TR] = -0.193) and age couplings near zero.

**Window-1 randomised contrast (causal consistency check, latent-logit change scale):** the immediate-minus-waitlist arm difference at the first randomised window is positive for all three tracked skills — TE +0.294 (89% CI +0.020 to +0.567; P(>0) = 0.956), W +0.416 (89% CI +0.088 to +0.748; P(>0) = 0.980), TR +0.210 (89% CI -0.047 to +0.465; P(>0) = 0.907). These are the only causally-interpretable numbers in the model and agree in direction and rough size with the ITT suite; they are a check, not a new effect.

### LCSM-082 — reciprocal dominance: does blending (B) lead word reading (W) or vice versa, once letter-sounds (L) are adjusted?

This is the model built to referee a direction-of-causation debate. Fit together, the two **blending<->word-reading** cross-couplings are both weak: prior word reading -> later blending change (g_W->B = +0.055, 89% CI -0.036 to +0.147; P(>0) = 0.835, suggestive) and prior blending -> later word-reading change (g_B->W = +0.040, 89% CI -0.059 to +0.135; P(>0) = 0.749, inconclusive — the interval straddles zero). Neither direction is well supported once letter-sounds are in the model.

What _is_ strong is that **letter-sound knowledge leads both**: prior L -> later W change (+0.245, 89% CI +0.140 to +0.368; P(>0) = 1.000, very strong) and prior L -> later B change (+0.155, 89% CI +0.042 to +0.284; P(>0) = 0.987, strong). The developmental-ordering reading is therefore that letter-sound knowledge is the upstream skill pulling both word reading and blending along, and the apparent blending<->reading link is largely accounted for by that shared driver rather than by either skill leading the other.

The `dominance_summary.csv` puts the two blending<->reading directions on a comparable SD-standardised footing (each coupling rescaled by the ratio of the source-level and target-change latent standard deviations): standardised W->B = +0.185 (89% CI -0.125 to +0.490) versus standardised B->W = +0.087 (89% CI -0.126 to +0.289), and the dominance contrast |W->B| - |B->W| = +0.081 (89% CI -0.173 to +0.397; P(>0) = 0.688). That is **inconclusive** — the data lean very slightly towards reading leading blending, but do not establish either direction as dominant. Adjusted covariates behave as in LCSM-081 (positive erbto, near-zero hearing and DEAP), with negative self-feedback and negative age couplings for W. The three window-1 randomised contrasts are all positive (W +0.405, P = 0.980; B +0.487, P = 0.967; L +0.682, P = 0.998), consistent with the ITT suite.

### LCSM-091 — change-on-change: is it the earlier _level_ or the recent _change_ in L and E that predicts later word-reading (W) change?

This model adds "change-of-source" terms to separate two hypotheses: does later reading growth track how far a child has _got_ in letter-sounds and expressive vocabulary (their level), or how _fast they are currently moving_ in them (their recent change)? The answer is clearly **level, not recent change**. The two level couplings are well supported — prior L level -> W change (+0.224, 89% CI +0.124 to +0.332; P(>0) = 1.000, very strong) and prior E level -> W change (+0.248, 89% CI +0.061 to +0.443; P(>0) = 0.983, strong) — reproducing the LCSM-067 ordering (letter-sounds and expressive vocabulary lead reading). But the recent-_change_ couplings are flat: prior L change -> W change (+0.047, 89% CI -0.167 to +0.259; P(>0) = 0.645, inconclusive) and prior E change -> W change (-0.001, 89% CI -0.476 to +0.473; P(>0) = 0.499, essentially undecided — a textbook "no information" posterior centred on zero).

So the signal is a _standing-level_ one: being further along in letter-sounds and expressive vocabulary predicts later reading gains, whereas having recently gained ground in those skills adds nothing detectable beyond the level itself. Word reading again shows negative self-feedback (b_self[W] = -0.261) and a negative age coupling (d_age[W] = -0.109, P(>0) = 0.004). Window-1 randomised contrasts are positive and match the suite for W (+0.395, P = 0.981) and L (+0.682, P = 0.998); the E contrast is inconclusive (+0.017, 89% CI -0.138 to +0.172; P(>0) = 0.572).

### LCSM-181 — the no-reverse-coupling comparator for LCSM-081

LCSM-181 is a deliberately trimmed twin of LCSM-081: it keeps the taught-receptive -> taught-expressive coupling and the adjustment set, but **drops the prior-word-reading terms** (g_W->TE, g_W->TR). Its role is to serve as the leave-one-out (LOO) baseline against which LCSM-081's extra reading terms are judged — LOO being a cross-validation score for out-of-sample predictive fit. The within-vocabulary coupling is essentially unchanged when the reading terms are removed: prior TR -> TE change = +0.444 (89% CI +0.277 to +0.638; P(>0) = 1.000, very strong) here, versus +0.428 in LCSM-081. That stability confirms the receptive-leads-expressive ordering does not depend on the reading terms, and that word reading is a minor, not a load-bearing, predictor of taught-vocabulary growth.

The adjusted covariates are consistent with LCSM-081 and slightly sharper here: erbto positive (+0.180, 89% CI +0.108 to +0.256; P(>0) = 1.000), DEAP composite negative (-0.137, 89% CI -0.205 to -0.069; P(>0) = 0.001), hearing positive but modest (+0.067, P(>0) = 0.908, moderate). Window-1 randomised contrasts match LCSM-081 (TE +0.288, W +0.417, TR +0.211).

## What the family concludes

A single developmental-ordering story runs through all five models, and it is consistent: **letter-sound knowledge and expressive vocabulary tend to lead word reading**, not the other way round (LCSM-067, LCSM-091, and the L-adjusted LCSM-082), and it is the child's _standing level_ in those skills — not their recent rate of change — that carries the signal (LCSM-091). Within taught vocabulary, **receptive knowledge leads expressive production** by a wide and very-strongly-supported margin (LCSM-081, LCSM-181), while word reading feeds taught-vocabulary learning only weakly. The one head-to-head direction test, blending versus word reading (LCSM-082), comes out **inconclusive** once letter-sounds are adjusted: the apparent blending<->reading link is largely a shadow of their shared upstream driver, letter-sound knowledge.

This triangulates with the other families rather than duplicating them. The randomised window-1 contrasts embedded in four of the models reproduce the ITT suite's positive effects on W, L, B and taught vocabulary, so the latent-trajectory machinery is not distorting the causal signal. And the ordering "letter-sounds/vocabulary -> reading" is the observational counterpart of what the mechanism and mediation families model as causal routes — but here it remains an **association**, offered as corroborating evidence about sequence, not as an independent causal claim.

## Caveats and convergence

- **All five gates pass cleanly** — 0 divergences each, max r̂ ≤ 1.007 (LCSM-081), min ESS ≥ 1207 — so no held coefficients and no funnel caveat applies to this family.
- **Every coupling is an association.** Residual confounding — above all by latent general ability, which the child-specific trajectory partially pools but does **not** proxy — applies to all of them. Do not read a coupling, a self-feedback term, an age term, or the hearing/erbto/DEAP covariates as causal (the Table-2 fallacy). Only the window-1 arm contrasts are causal, and only as consistency checks.
- **Units.** Couplings are latent logit-scale slopes and are reported in logit units; they do not translate into test items the way an ITT proportion-correct effect does, so no items figures are quoted for them.
- **Small N and four waves.** With 54 children and only four measurement occasions, the latent trajectories are lightly identified, the wider intervals (e.g. the change-on-change terms in LCSM-091, and the dominance contrast in LCSM-082) are honest reflections of how little four waves can pin down a reciprocal-causation direction, and point estimates that just clear a threshold are on average inflated — hence the lead-with-the-interval rule.
- The DEAP picture-composite coupling should be read with the project's standing caveat that the trial-era DEAP scoring is lenient and not directly comparable to gold-standard phonology measures; its consistently negative sign here is noted but not over-interpreted.

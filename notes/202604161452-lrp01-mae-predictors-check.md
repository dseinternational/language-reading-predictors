# LRP01 predictor experiments — b1exto vs eowpvt

> [!WARNING]
> These notes were generated by an AI tool.

Date: 2026-04-16

## Motivation

The final LRP01 model uses `b1exto` (Block 1 total expressive vocabulary) as its
expressive-vocabulary predictor. During MAE-based feature selection, `eowpvt`
(Expressive One-Word Picture Vocabulary Test) was dropped at step 5 (importance
0.004 at that point). Both measure expressive vocabulary but differ:

- `b1exto` is a study-specific measure — words taught and untaught in the
  intervention's Block 1 vocabulary assessment
- `eowpvt` is a standardised clinical instrument (Brownell, 2000) with broader
  coverage and established psychometric properties

If eowpvt performs comparably, it may be preferable for interpretability and
generalisability since it is a widely used clinical measure.

## Experiment: swap b1exto for eowpvt

Same model configuration (MAE-tuned params, no outlier exclusion, n=157),
6 predictors in both cases.

### Interpretation

b1exto performs slightly better than eowpvt across all CV metrics:

- CV MAE: 2.898 vs 2.980 (+0.082, ~3% worse with eowpvt)
- CV R2: 0.076 vs 0.049 (lower explained variance with eowpvt)
- CV MedAE: 1.955 vs 2.005 (+0.050)

The differences are modest and within the fold-to-fold variability. Both models
produce similar importance rankings — age, attendance, and letter-sound knowledge
dominate regardless of which vocabulary measure is used.

b1exto ranks slightly higher in its model (rank 3, importance 0.035) than eowpvt
does in its model (rank 6, importance 0.032). This may reflect that the
intervention-specific vocabulary measure captures signal more directly relevant
to reading gains in this study population.

## Conclusion

b1exto is the better choice for this dataset. The improvement is small but
consistent across metrics. The study-specific vocabulary measure appears to
capture intervention-relevant signal that the general-purpose eowpvt misses.
This is consistent with the intervention design — children were explicitly taught
Block 1 vocabulary, so their acquisition of those words may be a more direct
indicator of learning trajectory.

For generalisability to other populations, eowpvt would be preferable since
b1exto is specific to this study's intervention protocol. The performance cost
of switching is modest (~3% CV MAE).

## Experiment 2: add gender back in

Gender was dropped at MAE selection step 3 (importance 0.001). However, it was
rank 7 (importance 0.064) in the RMSE-tuned 15-predictor model and rank 7
(importance 0.093) in the RMSE test-config run. The MAE model's shallow trees
may have undervalued it. Test whether adding it back to the 6-predictor set
improves the model.

### Results (dev config, 5-fold CV)

| Metric | lrp01 (6 predictors) | lrp01_exp_gender (7 predictors) |
|---|---|---|
| Predictors | age, b1exto, celf, blending, yarclet, attend | + gender |

| CV R2 | 0.076 +/- 0.131 | 0.073 +/- 0.150 |
| CV MedAE | 1.955 +/- 0.350 | 1.923 +/- 0.314 |
| In-sample MAE | 2.550 | 2.527 |

### Interpretation

Adding gender back produces marginal improvements on some metrics and marginal
degradation on others:

- CV MAE: 2.898 -> 2.893 (trivially better)
- CV RMSE: 4.052 -> 4.056 (trivially worse)
- CV MedAE: 1.955 -> 1.923 (slightly better typical-case predictions)
- CV R2: 0.076 -> 0.073 (trivially worse)

Gender has non-trivial importance (0.014, rank 7/7) — it is not pure noise.
The improvement in MedAE (1.96 -> 1.92) suggests it helps for typical cases.
However, the differences are within fold-to-fold variability and do not justify
adding complexity. Gender is a borderline predictor under the MAE objective.

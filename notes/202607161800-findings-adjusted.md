## What these models ask

"Among children alike on everything else in the model, which **baseline** features go with **more later gain** in word reading?" Each predictor is reported twice — its **mutually-adjusted** coefficient (holding the others fixed) and its **bivariate** coefficient (on its own) — because those answer different questions and often disagree (the "Table-2 fallacy" reminder). Nothing here is causal: these are **between-child associations** describing _who_ progresses, not levers.

## Convergence gate

Both **passed** cleanly (0 divergences).

## Results

- **LRP-RLI-ADJ-065** (RLI trial cohort, word reading): the most clearly-resolved adjusted predictor was **age at t1** — a 1-SD older child was associated with **−2.9 items** of word-reading difference (95% range −4.6 to −1.0; 99.7% negative, very strong). I.e. among children alike on the other measures, **older starters gained less** on this bounded measure over the window.
- **LRP-RLM-ADJ-001** (Byrne historical cohort — a _separate, non-randomised study_, #338): same pattern, larger scale — **age (months)** was the clearest adjusted predictor at **−8.3 items** (95% range −13.3 to −3.3; 99.9% negative).

## The one-paragraph story

In both cohorts the stand-out adjusted association is **age**: older children, holding other baseline skills fixed, show _less_ measured gain over the window. This is a description of the sample's progress pattern (and partly reflects ceiling/measurement structure on bounded scores), **not** a claim that being older _causes_ less gain, and certainly not something the intervention changes.

## What is causal

**Nothing.** These are adjusted between-child associations. The RLM model belongs to the Byrne descriptive study, where — with no randomisation — _every_ estimate is descriptive or adjusted-associational by design.
